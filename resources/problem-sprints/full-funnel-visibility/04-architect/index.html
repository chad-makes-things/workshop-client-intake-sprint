The Patient Funnel

Five stages from lead to revenue — the P&L SGA manages at every practice.

1
Leads Generated
Ads, organic search, referrals, walk-ins — a patient enters the funnel
2
Leads Connected With
Phone answered (human or AI), form responded to, chat engaged — two-way contact
3
Leads Booked
Appointment scheduled in PMS — patient has a date and time
4
Patient Shows Up
Patient arrives — show rates vary 60-85% depending on service type
5
Production / In the Chair
Treatment accepted, revenue generated — the bottom line

User Journey

How the Funnel OS works from Monday morning through month-end.

Step 1 — Monday 7:15 AM
CEO Opens the Funnel Dashboard
Network-wide funnel view loads — 5 stages, color-coded health, Top 5 AI alerts sidebar. Southeast phone answer rates are down 12%. One click to drill down.
Dashboard loads in <3 seconds with data no more than 4 hours old. Network → region → practice in 2 clicks.
Step 2 — Drill-Down
From Alert to Root Cause in 2 Minutes
CEO clicks the alert → region heatmap → Towne Lake Dental highlighted red. AI annotation: "Front desk headcount dropped from 3 to 2 on March 3rd. Recommended: activate AI phone agent overflow." CEO forwards to VP Ops.
AI correlates phone data + HR data to explain WHY, not just WHAT. Actionable in 2 minutes instead of discovered 30 days later.
Step 3 — 9:00 AM
HQ Ops Team Morning Standup
Ops queue shows 14 practices needing attention, ranked by revenue impact. 3 phone issues, 4 show-rate drops, 2 production gaps, 5 lead dips. Each item has AI summary + recommended action + estimated revenue impact.
Queue must be opinionated — ranked, not just listed. Top 3 must be clearly the top 3. Revenue impact is the ranking function.
Step 4 — Practice, 7:45 AM
Office Manager's 20-Minute Agenda
Jennifer at Garner Dental opens the Nerve Center. 3 priority cards: call back 4 missed calls ($1,200 est.), confirm 3 at-risk appointments, follow up 2 unscheduled treatment plans ($8,400). She completes 2 of 3 in 12 minutes.
Zero learning curve. 3 cards, each tappable. Phone numbers pre-loaded, text templates pre-written. Execute, don't figure out.
Step 5 — 11:30 AM
AI Phone Agent Catches a Missed Call
Front desk is busy. Sierra answers on ring 4, checks the PMS schedule, books a whitening appointment for Tuesday 2 PM. Event flows into the funnel: Lead Generated → Connected → Booked. AI notes 35% of bookings this week are AI-assisted (baseline: 20%).
Sierra's actions flow into the SAME funnel as human actions. The system tracks who handled it to identify capacity gaps.
Step 6 — Tuesday 2:00 PM
Proactive Show Rate Intervention
AI detects 4 unconfirmed appointments for tomorrow at Troy Family Dental. Predicted show rate: 40%. Auto-sends confirmation texts. Flags 2 high-value appointments ($4,500 implant consult) for personal phone calls. Result: 4 of 6 show instead of predicted 3.6.
Service-type-aware: routine cleaning confirmation ≠ implant consult confirmation. Different value, different risk, different approach.
Step 7 — Friday 4:00 PM
Weekly Executive Review
AI-generated Week in Review: 4,200 leads, 75% connection rate, 80% booking rate, 85% show rate, $3.2M production. 3 practices flagged. 2 interventions completed with positive outcomes. 1 emerging trend flagged. 15-minute review, not a 2-hour spreadsheet argument.
Auto-generated, zero human assembly. Shows OUTCOMES of interventions — builds confidence the system works.
Step 8 — Practice, 5:30 PM
End-of-Day Scorecard
Jennifer checks her scorecard: 92% answer rate, 4 new patients booked, 1 no-show managed, $18,400 production (102% of target). 2 of 3 priorities completed. The unscheduled treatment plans roll to tomorrow's agenda.
The scorecard is the feedback loop. Jennifer sees the impact of her 20-minute session: "You called 4 patients → 2 booked → $1,200 in pipeline."
Step 9 — Sunday Night
AI Learning Loop
The system reviews all interventions. Which were executed? Which improved metrics? Models update: phone calls improve high-value show rates by 18%, texts by 8%. Next week's recommendations will be more accurate.
Transparent learning: HQ can ask "why this recommendation?" and get a data-grounded answer. Explainable AI builds trust.
Step 10 — March 31st
Month-End: The Old World vs. The New World
No scramble. No 30-day wait. The monthly view has been building in real-time. $42.8M production. Funnel conversion rates. Practice rankings. All live, all accurate. The CEO shares it on the investor call — real-time data, not 30-day-old approximations.
The real test: if the Funnel OS makes month-end reconciliation obsolete — real-time data within 2% of financial close — the system has proven its value.

Architecture Decisions

Key decisions and their rationale — the "why" behind the architecture.

ADR 1: Unified Platform vs. Best-of-Breed Assembly

▶
Decision: Build a unified platform rather than assembling BI tool + data warehouse + AI service + practice app as separate systems.

Rationale: At 260+ practices, system fragmentation is the current problem. Building a fragmented solution perpetuates the problem at a different level. The unified platform costs more upfront but reduces long-term maintenance, ensures data consistency, and enables AI to operate across all layers natively.

Tradeoff: Slower time to first value — mitigated by using an off-the-shelf BI tool as the Stage 1 dashboard while the platform is being built.

ADR 2: BI Tool as Stage 1 Dashboard (Buy Then Replace)

▶
Decision: Use Metabase or Superset as the Stage 1 executive dashboard, then replace with a custom dashboard in Stage 3.

Rationale: Executives need to see data in 30 days. Building a custom dashboard takes 3-5 months. The BI tool validates the data model and executive needs before investing in custom UI. It's disposable infrastructure — it served its purpose once the custom dashboard is built.

Tradeoff: Stage 1 looks "generic." But executives see real data, in real-time, from day 30. That's the priority.

ADR 3: Per-Practice Baselines (Not Network Averages)

▶
Decision: Calculate anomaly baselines per-practice from rolling 90-day history, not from network averages.

Rationale: A 2-chair rural practice and a 12-chair metro practice have completely different "normal." Network averages create false positives (flagging healthy small practices) and false negatives (missing problems at large practices). Per-practice baselines ensure alerts are meaningful.

Tradeoff: Requires 90 days of data before baselines are reliable. Mitigation: use cohort-based baselines as initial priors until practice-specific data accumulates.

ADR 4: Practice Nerve Center Extends Practice Concierge

▶
Decision: Build the Practice Nerve Center as an extension of the Practice Concierge (from the Digital Storefront sprint), not as a separate application.

Rationale: Both share the same users, interaction model, data infrastructure, and adoption challenge. Merging them creates one tool, one interface, one relationship. Staff don't need to learn two things.

Tradeoff: More complex application. Mitigation: Concierge and Nerve Center are separate modules within the same app — different screens, shared navigation and AI. Think Gmail tabs.

ADR 5: Service-Type-Aware Automation

▶
Decision: All automation is parameterized by service type — cleaning ≠ implant consult ≠ full-arch procedure.

Rationale: SGA spans general dentistry, periodontics, oral surgery, implantology, and cosmetic. Patient psychology, no-show risk, revenue per visit, and appropriate communication tone vary dramatically. Generic automation erodes patient trust. Contextual automation builds it.

Tradeoff: More complex automation rules and templates. But the revenue difference between a $150 cleaning no-show and a $30,000 implant no-show justifies the investment.

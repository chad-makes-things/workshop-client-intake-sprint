# Solution Sketches

## Sprint Target (Confirmed)

**Morning Huddle as Culture Flywheel** — Design a morning huddle content system and activation framework so compelling and easy to run that 80% of SGA practices complete a structured hospitality huddle at least 4 days per week within 6 months of launch.

All 5 solutions are designed around this target while addressing the broader hospitality operationalization challenge. Each solution represents a genuinely distinct philosophy, not just feature variations.

---

## SOLUTION 1: The Huddle OS — Content-First Daily Ritual Platform

**One-line pitch**: Build a Netflix-style daily content engine inside Dental Intel that delivers ready-to-run, personalized 5-minute huddle experiences — so any practice can run a world-class hospitality huddle with zero preparation.

### Narrative

Every morning at 7:50 AM, the Dental Intel screen auto-loads "Today's Huddle" — a 5-minute guided experience built on content produced by SGA's culture team. The huddle opens with a 60-second "Caring with Charisma Moment" from Grant or a certified C&C Champion — a story, a reminder, or a challenge related to this week's theme (trust transfer, environmental standards, phone hospitality, exit experience). Then a 90-second "Yesterday's Score" segment pulls live data from Swell: how many reviews came in, which ones mentioned staff by name, any 5-star milestones. Then a 90-second "Today's Focus" segment with a specific behavior practice — the team does a 30-second role-play exercise prompted by the screen. The huddle closes with a 60-second team intention statement (the facilitator reads the card, the team repeats a single phrase as a collective commitment).

The content library has 52 weeks of weekly themes × 5 daily variations = 260 unique huddle modules per year. Grant films 2-3 "Culture Moment" videos per week. The remaining content is built by the SGA culture team using a templated production system — the same format, rotated topics, 30-minute production time per module.

Practice owners see a dashboard: this week's huddle completion rate, this month's streak, and a comparison to the network average. The leaderboard shows top 25 practices by huddle streak. Practices in the bottom 25% get a personal outreach from their regional director with a "Huddle Health Check."

Certification lives alongside the Huddle OS: new hires are assigned micro-modules within Dental Intel before they participate in their first live huddle. The huddle becomes the graduation ceremony — your first huddle is the moment you officially join the team culture.

### Strengths
- Highest potential for immediate daily impact — every practice can run an excellent huddle from day 1 with no facilitator skill required
- Content becomes an SGA brand asset — the content library grows in value over time and creates the curriculum foundation for dental school adoption
- Platform already exists (Dental Intel) — no new tech, just new content and activation design

### Risks
- Content production at scale is an ongoing operational commitment — requires a dedicated culture content team or significant vendor budget
- Dental Intel platform dependency: if Gen 4 offices don't all use Dental Intel, the delivery mechanism is inconsistent
- High quality bar required: if the video content isn't Grant-level engaging, practice owners will skip it within 2 weeks

### Feasibility
**HIGH** — Dental Intel is already in use. Content production is a known challenge (SGA has already done it for the phone skills videos). The biggest investment is committing to ongoing content creation as a core SGA function, not a one-time project.

### Key Assumption Being Tested
If we make the morning huddle turnkey — great content delivered automatically, no preparation needed — practice owners will run it consistently even without direct supervision.

## SOLUTION 2: The Caring with Charisma Academy — Certification-Led Culture Platform

**One-line pitch**: Build a portable, career-value certification system ("Caring with Charisma Academy") with tiered credentials, a physical jacket, and an industry-recognized standard — so staff complete training for themselves, not just for SGA.

### Narrative

"Caring with Charisma Academy" (CCA) launches as SGA's formal credentialing institution for dental patient experience. The Academy has three levels: CCA Bronze (required for all new hires within 30 days), CCA Silver (12 months of certified behaviors + peer review), and CCA Gold (sustained excellence, leadership behaviors, eligible for jacket and bonus pool).

The curriculum is built as 10 micro-modules × 5-7 minutes each, covering: the "Caring with Charisma" origin story and philosophy, the 5 hospitality domains (phone, arrival, clinical, trust transfer, exit), scripted response practice for each domain, environmental standards verification, morning huddle facilitation, and peer mentorship basics. Each module ends with a behavior commitment and an observable practice scenario.

Bronze certification requires completing all 10 modules and passing a scenario assessment. Silver certification requires 6 months of Swell review scores that meet a minimum threshold at the practice level, plus a peer nomination from the practice owner or lead CCA. Gold certification requires 12 months of sustained performance, demonstrated leadership in the practice huddle, and a review by a CCA panel.

The physical jacket is Gold only — ordered by the practice owner when the staff member achieves Gold, funded by SGA as an investment in the culture. The credential lives on a public-facing portal where anyone can verify a staff member's certification level by name. The LinkedIn integration allows certified staff to add "Caring with Charisma Academy — Gold" to their professional profiles.

The morning huddle connects: CCA Bronze students have a dedicated "Certification Track" huddle module. CCA Gold holders are the Huddle Champions at their practice — they lead the daily ritual and receive a small stipend for doing so.

### Strengths
- Creates genuine career value that drives intrinsic motivation — staff complete it for themselves
- Builds an industry-standard credential over time — the pathway to dental school adoption runs through CCA legitimacy
- High-turnover problem partially addressed: certified staff carry the credential with them, making SGA-trained staff a market signal

### Risks
- 12–18 months to see meaningful Gold certification cohorts; requires sustained organizational patience
- Silver and Gold certification criteria require behavior data infrastructure that may not exist on day 1
- Content development for 10 high-quality modules requires significant upfront investment

### Feasibility
**HIGH for Bronze (short-term)** / **MEDIUM for Silver/Gold (requires behavior measurement infrastructure)**. The Bronze track can launch in 90 days. The full tiered system requires measurement infrastructure from Solution 4 or 5.

### Key Assumption Being Tested
If the certification is portable and career-valuable (LinkedIn-visible, resume-worthy, jacket-earning), staff in high-turnover roles will voluntarily complete it and maintain their certification level.

## SOLUTION 3: The Hospitality Score Engine — Data-Led Accountability Platform

**One-line pitch**: Build a unified "Hospitality Score" for every SGA practice — automatically calculated from Cinnamon, Swell, and Dental Intel signals — that turns culture from a soft aspiration into a visible, competitive metric.

### Narrative

The Hospitality Score Engine aggregates signals from all three available data sources into a single per-practice "H-Score" updated daily. Cinnamon contributes: phone answer rate, greeting phrase compliance (detecting whether specific hospitality phrases are used vs. generic greetings), hold time, and call sentiment. Swell contributes: 7-day review velocity, average star rating, review sentiment for hospitality language (patients using words like "warm," "friendly," "welcoming," "explained everything," "felt comfortable"). Dental Intel contributes: huddle completion rate, morning check-in streak, certification module completions.

Each source contributes a weighted score: Cinnamon 35%, Swell 45%, Dental Intel 20%. The weights are designed to favor patient outcome signals over internal compliance signals. The H-Score runs from 0-100 with five levels: Emerging (0-39), Developing (40-59), Established (60-74), Exceptional (75-89), and Elite (90+).

Every practice owner receives a weekly H-Score digest: their score, their trend, top 3 contributing factors, and a personalized "This Week's Opportunity" recommendation generated from the lowest-scoring component. Regional directors see a portfolio view — every practice ranked by H-Score with trend arrows. The network leaderboard shows the top 25 "Elite" practices and the most-improved practices month over month.

The H-Score connects to incentives: practices achieving "Exceptional" or "Elite" status for 3 consecutive months unlock a bonus pool contribution for staff. Practices in the bottom 10% receive a mandatory "Culture Intervention" — a structured program of targeted training, increased huddle frequency, and regional director check-ins.

The morning huddle becomes the venue where the daily H-Score movement is reviewed. "Yesterday we moved from 67 to 71 — here's why and here's today's target."

### Strengths
- Makes culture visible and competitive — the leaderboard creates self-reinforcing motivation without ongoing executive pressure
- Closes the feedback loop: staff see their behavior connected to outcomes within 24-48 hours
- Data infrastructure that enables the "dental school adoption" argument — SGA can publish research showing correlation between H-Score and new patient acquisition

### Risks
- Attribution is genuinely difficult — H-Score may reflect factors outside staff control (billing disputes, wait times, clinical outcomes) and erode trust if staff see it as unfair
- Requires significant data engineering investment to connect three platforms via API into a unified scoring model
- Risk of gaming: if bonus pool is tied to Swell reviews, practices may solicit reviews inappropriately to inflate scores

### Feasibility
**MEDIUM** — Data exists in three systems; the integration work is real but achievable (all three platforms have APIs). The bigger challenge is the attribution model design and ensuring the score is trusted by practitioners as fair.

### Key Assumption Being Tested
If practice owners and staff can see a daily hospitality score that reflects their actual behaviors, they will change behavior to improve it — and they will trust the score as a fair representation of their performance.

## SOLUTION 4: The Trust Transfer OS — Behavior-Specific Verification System

**One-line pitch**: Focus the entire sprint on one high-leverage behavior — the trust transfer protocol — and build a lightweight verification system that makes the 3-step handoff trackable, trainable, and directly connected to patient outcomes.

### Narrative

Rather than trying to operationalize all hospitality behaviors simultaneously, Solution 4 concentrates on the single highest-leverage clinical touchpoint: the trust transfer protocol (hygienist → DA → doctor, each reinforcing the same patient-centered message). This is the moment that most directly drives patient confidence, treatment acceptance, and 5-star reviews — and it is currently invisible in the data.

The system works in three layers. Layer 1 — Training: A single "Trust Transfer Certification" module (available in Dental Intel and on mobile) teaches the exact language for each handoff step, with video demos by Grant and two practicing hygienists. The module includes scenario practice for common situations (anxious patient, first-time patient, treatment refusal). Staff pass a scenario assessment before their certification is marked complete. Layer 2 — Prompt System: At the start of each patient appointment, the DA's Dental Intel checklist includes a "Trust Transfer Check" — a single checkbox: "Did you complete the handoff protocol?" This is lightweight documentation (30 seconds) that creates data and, more importantly, a behavior prompt before every patient interaction. Layer 3 — Signal Attribution: Swell review language is analyzed weekly for hospitality markers that correlate with trust transfer success (patients mentioning "the whole team," "everyone was so helpful," "they explained everything," "I felt like they all knew me"). Practices with higher Trust Transfer Check completion rates are tested against their Swell sentiment scores to validate the correlation.

The morning huddle becomes the daily ritual for trust transfer reinforcement: every morning, the huddle includes a 60-second "Trust Transfer Reminder" — today's patient caseload is previewed, the DA reminds the team of the handoff protocol, and the team nominates a "Trust Transfer Champion of the Day."

Over 90 days, SGA has: a completion rate for Trust Transfer Certification, a daily checklist completion rate per practice, and an initial correlation analysis between handoff compliance and review scores. This data becomes the proof of concept for the broader H-Score system (Solution 3) and the CCA Silver certification (Solution 2).

### Strengths
- Laser focus on one behavior reduces change management burden — staff can master one protocol completely rather than partially adopting many
- Data model is clean and interpretable — one behavior, one signal, one outcome correlation
- Creates the proof of concept SGA needs to justify broader investment in the full platform

### Risks
- Narrow focus may underserve the full hospitality vision — does not address phone behaviors, environmental standards, exit experience, or certification
- Checklist fatigue: if this becomes "just another box to check," the behavior prompt loses its power
- The correlation between trust transfer and reviews may be hard to isolate statistically given the many variables affecting patient satisfaction

### Feasibility
**HIGH** — Smallest scope, highest technical feasibility. Can launch in 30-45 days. The constraint is Grant's availability for the training video production.

### Key Assumption Being Tested
If we build one specific, verified behavior protocol (trust transfer) and make it trackable, we can demonstrate measurable improvement in patient satisfaction scores — proving that targeted behavior training works and justifying the investment in the full system.

## SOLUTION 5: The Culture OS — People-First Peer Amplification Network

**One-line pitch**: Instead of building a platform, build a people system — a network of "Caring with Charisma Champions" across 260 practices who own the culture at the local level, supported by monthly content drops, peer community, and a recognition program that makes championing the culture a coveted role.

### Narrative

Solution 5 inverts the tech-heavy assumptions of the other solutions. Rather than asking the platform to deliver culture, it asks people to carry it — supported by just enough infrastructure to scale.

The system begins by identifying and recruiting one "Caring with Charisma Champion" at each practice. The Champion role is filled by a self-nominated or owner-nominated staff member who already embodies the "Caring with Charisma" spirit — likely a senior hygienist, front desk lead, or office manager. Champions receive a dedicated onboarding: a 2-day virtual "Champion Summit" facilitated by Grant and the SGA executive team, where they are trained in the hospitality philosophy, the morning huddle facilitation structure, the certification curriculum, and their role as culture ambassadors.

Champions return to their practices equipped with: a "Culture Starter Kit" (physical and digital), a Champion playbook for facilitating monthly culture sessions, a standing monthly call with their regional Champion cohort (15-20 practices per region), and direct access to Grant's monthly "Culture Brief" video. They own the morning huddle at their practice — facilitating daily, personalizing content, and reporting huddle completion to the regional director.

Every quarter, SGA runs a "Champion Showcase" — a virtual event where Champions from across the network share their best practice stories, patient experiences, and culture innovations. The top 3 Champions in each region receive public recognition and a travel stipend to attend the annual SGA conference as hospitality culture speakers.

The content cadence is lighter than Solution 1 but still systematic: a weekly "Culture Card" (digital one-pager with this week's hospitality theme, a discussion prompt, and a review highlight) distributed to all Champions every Sunday. Champions decide how to use it in their practice's context. Monthly "culture drop" — a 10-minute video from Grant with the month's hospitality focus, shareable in huddles.

The morning huddle is Champion-led: the Champion owns the ritual, adapts it to their practice culture, and reports back on what worked.

### Strengths
- Culturally authentic — people carry culture more effectively than platforms, especially in healthcare where human relationships are the core product
- Lower tech dependency — no new platform required; WhatsApp + Dental Intel + Swell is sufficient infrastructure
- High network effects: 260 Champions knowing each other creates a self-sustaining culture community that SGA doesn't have to manage

### Risks
- Single point of failure at each practice: if the Champion leaves, culture progress can disappear quickly without institutional backup
- Harder to measure — peer-driven culture systems generate less quantitative data than platform-driven systems
- SGA HQ has limited visibility and control — the culture lives with Champions, not in a dashboard; executive accountability requires trust rather than data

### Feasibility
**HIGH for launch** / **MEDIUM for sustaining at scale** — The initial Champion recruitment and Summit can happen in 60-90 days. The sustainability challenge is keeping Champions engaged and empowered 12+ months in without the novelty factor.

### Key Assumption Being Tested
If we identify and empower the right people at each practice to own the culture locally, the hospitality standard will sustain and evolve from the ground up — and the platform infrastructure can be built around a people system that already works, rather than hoping a platform produces human behavior change.

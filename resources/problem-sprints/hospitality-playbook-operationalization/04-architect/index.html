The Playbook

Caring with Charisma — What We Believe

A dental visit can be one of the most anxious experiences in a person's life — or one of the most affirming. The difference is not clinical skill. It is the quality of every human interaction before, during, and after the chair. "Caring with Charisma" is SGA's system for designing that difference at scale.

The Philosophy

Modeled after the Ritz Carlton, Chick-fil-A, and Disney University, "Caring with Charisma" treats hospitality not as a personality trait but as a designed, teachable, measurable system. Every behavior in the playbook is specific, observable, and verifiable. No ambiguity. No "just be nicer." Exact protocols that any staff member can learn, practice, and perfect.

The north star: dental schools adopt "Caring with Charisma" as curriculum — making SGA-trained staff the recognized gold standard for patient experience in dentistry, anywhere in the country.

The Daily Ritual

The Morning Huddle — 5 Minutes That Change Everything

Every morning, before the first patient arrives, the entire practice team gathers for 5 minutes. The platform delivers the experience — Grant on screen, yesterday's reviews surfaced live, a behavior practice, and a shared intention. No preparation required. Just press play.

0:00

Culture Moment

Grant or a Champion tells a hospitality story — 60 seconds, direct to camera, emotional. This week's theme: Trust Transfer. Grant describes the moment a patient said "I felt like the whole team had been briefed about me." The team leans in.

1:00

Yesterday's Wins

The platform pulls live Swell data from the last 24 hours. H-Score trend line: 7-day sparkline. Any review that mentions a staff member by name is highlighted and read aloud. The team sees the direct result of yesterday's behaviors.

2:00

Today's Focus — Team Practice

This week's hospitality behavior introduced. 30-second team exercise: pairs practice the trust transfer phrase out loud. The screen provides the exact script. No one has to improvise. The behavior gets practice before the first patient.

3:30

Accountability Moment

Trust Transfer Champion of the Day nominated or rotated. Any CCA certification completions since yesterday are celebrated. The environmental checklist: three items confirmed — welcome space ready, refreshments stocked, ambient standards met.

4:30

The Intention

"Today, every guest leaves knowing their name matters here."

Spoken by the Champion or practice owner. Repeated by the team. The same structure, every day. The ritual IS the culture delivery system.

Architecture Decision Records

Five Decisions That Define This Platform

Each decision captures a significant architectural choice — what was decided, why, and what consequence it has for every future design decision.

Decision Record
ADR-01
The Morning Huddle is the primary content delivery container — not a standalone app or new LMS
The morning huddle is the only moment in the day when the entire practice team is together and not yet in patient-facing mode. Building a new app creates an adoption problem. Extending the morning huddle — which already exists — eliminates the adoption barrier entirely. The platform makes an existing behavior better rather than creating a new behavior.
ConsequenceEvery platform decision must ask "can this surface in the morning huddle?" before creating a separate interface. The huddle is the UX.
Decision Record
ADR-02
The H-Score algorithm is fully transparent and documented — no black box
In healthcare and high-trust service environments, opaque scoring systems create resentment. If staff believe the score measures things outside their control, they will game it or ignore it. Transparency about what the score measures and how it is calculated is the only path to the score being trusted as fair — which is the precondition for it being motivating.
ConsequenceAll H-Score documentation is written for a practice owner audience — plain language explanations of "here's what moves your score and why," not technical documentation.
Decision Record
ADR-03
H-Score benchmarking is segmented by practice type — rural practices are never compared against urban practices
A rural practice with 12 patients per day generates fundamentally different data volumes than an urban practice with 80. The same hospitality behaviors produce fewer reviews, fewer call analytics, and fewer huddle participation data points. Comparing them on raw metrics is unfair and demoralizing for rural practices — which would systematically bottom-rank regardless of actual hospitality quality.
ConsequencePractice type is a required field in all scoring calculations. A separate "Most Improved" leaderboard (percent-change, not absolute score) allows any practice type to compete and win network recognition.
Decision Record
ADR-04
The CCA credential is portable and externally verifiable from day one — not an internal training record
The difference between a credential and a training completion record is portability. If the credential only lives in SGA's HR system, it has no career value to staff — and career value is the entire motivation architecture for CCA engagement. Building external credentialing infrastructure at launch (not as a future upgrade) makes the credential genuinely valuable from the first day it is issued.
ConsequenceAll CCA content must meet the documentation standards required for academic submission. A higher quality bar for content production — which is a positive constraint, not a burden.
Decision Record
ADR-05
The "Caring with Charisma" playbook is a living, versioned document — never printed as final
The biggest failure mode for culture programs is the "binder on the shelf" problem — a playbook written once that sits unread in every practice's break room. By designing it as versioned software, SGA can evolve the culture standard as the network grows, as Champions contribute insights, and as academic research generates new best practices. The playbook is always current.
ConsequenceSGA needs a dedicated "Playbook Editor" role to manage the update cycle. All content is tagged to a playbook version. Staff who completed Bronze under v1.0 receive a "What's Changed" micro-module when v2.0 launches — not a full recertification.

Sprint Architecture

Workshop Intake Engine

Transforming client onboarding from manual chaos to guided experience

The Problem

Pre-workshop material collection is manual, fragmented, and unscalable. Materials arrive via text, email, Dropbox, and file shares. The facilitator spends 3-5 hours per client downloading, organizing, and mapping inputs into a structured framework -- a process that scales linearly with client volume.

The Solution

An AI-powered guided intake system that web-scrapes first, guides second, and delivers framework-ready output. Clients follow a conversational interview flow while the system auto-organizes everything into the facilitator's prompt framework -- reducing hours of manual work to minutes of review.

The 2-Year Goal

In 2 years, workshop clients will arrive at Day 1 with a complete, high-quality market narrative already built -- by submitting their raw materials through a guided intake system that automatically collects, organizes, and synthesizes business context into the structured framework the workshop requires.

5 Sprint Questions

The sprint identified five critical questions whose answers determine whether the system succeeds or fails. Weights reflect risk level -- HIGH-risk questions received a weight of 3 in scoring, MEDIUM-risk received 2.

HIGH RISK

Q1: Will clients actually use a structured system?

Clients currently text, email, and drop files ad hoc. Any solution requires behavioral change -- or must meet them where they are. If they won't use the intake system, nothing downstream matters.

HIGH RISK

Q2: Will the output match manual narrative quality?

The market narrative is the foundation of the workshop. If automated synthesis produces something generic or structurally wrong, the facilitator loses trust and reverts to manual work.

HIGH RISK

Q3: Can it handle the full sophistication spectrum?

Clients range from polished brand decks to "we just have our website and some stories." The system must serve both without making either feel inadequate.

MEDIUM RISK

Q4: Can it preserve the personal touch?

The workshop brand is "streamlined and loved." If intake feels like enterprise software -- transactional, cold -- it undermines the trust that makes the workshop succeed.

MEDIUM RISK

Q5: Will the facilitator trust and adopt the system?

Even with a working system, the facilitator may insist on manually reviewing every output -- effectively doing the same work with an extra step. The system must earn confidence quickly, or adoption stalls.

5 Solutions Explored

Five genuinely different approaches were designed, each making a different bet about what matters most. All were scored against the 5 sprint questions using risk-weighted criteria (max 65 points).

1

The Interview

Bets that structure and guidance will change client behavior

A TurboTax-style conversational guided intake that walks clients through branching questions mapped to the workshop's prompt framework. One question at a time, Typeform-style. Clients never see the framework -- they just answer questions. The system maps answers to slots behind the scenes.

Bets that doing the work for the client is better than asking them

The Decision: Interview + Head Start Hybrid

Solution 1 as the engine, Solution 3 as the differentiator

The Interview is the only solution that simultaneously (a) changes client behavior through structure rather than hoping they self-direct, (b) scales without adding facilitator labor, and (c) produces framework-mapped outputs by design rather than through post-hoc AI categorization.

The Head Start's web-scraping pre-population is integrated as a core feature: before the client begins the guided flow, the system scrapes their web presence and pre-fills answers. The client reviews pre-populated content and fills gaps. This hybrid captures The Head Start's "we did our homework" delight while retaining The Interview's structural guidance.

The Scaling Paradox

The Kickoff Call scored a perfect 65/65 -- the best answer to every sprint question in isolation. Live conversation produces the richest input, adapts to any client, and feels the most personal. But it requires 45 minutes of facilitator time per client. At 10 concurrent clients, that's 7.5 hours of calls before any synthesis begins. The highest-quality solution does not scale -- and scaling is the reason this sprint exists.

Key Learnings

Six insights emerged from the sprint that shaped the final architecture and will guide implementation.

1

The problem is behavioral, not technical

The root cause isn't bad tools or missing channels -- it's that clients don't know what to send, feel overwhelmed, and procrastinate. The solution must replace ambiguity with structure, not just build a better inbox.

2

Pre-population is a force multiplier, not a standalone

Web scraping works brilliantly as a feature within a guided flow. As a standalone solution, it has a floor problem -- thin-web-presence clients get an embarrassingly empty draft.

3

Voice input bridges the quality gap

Adding voice recording partially bridges the gap between typed form responses and live conversation. Clients who are "talkers, not writers" capture narrative richness that typing rarely achieves.

4

The facilitator's expertise lives in the question tree

The facilitator isn't replaced -- they're embedded. Their expertise is encoded in branching logic and framework mapping. This reframe from "automate the facilitator" to "leverage the facilitator's architecture" is critical for adoption.

5

Self-segmentation solves the spectrum

Rather than one path that works poorly for everyone, self-segmentation at the start routes clients to appropriate depth and question styles. Cleaner than trying to detect sophistication automatically.

6

Quality vs. scale is the central tradeoff

The highest-quality solution (live calls) doesn't scale. The most scalable solution (guided forms) loses conversational richness. The hybrid (guided flow + voice + pre-population) is the best available compromise.

What the Sprint Did NOT Resolve

  • Framework mapping automation -- the downstream challenge of auto-organizing collected materials into the prompt framework (Steps 7-10 on the problem map) needs its own sprint
  • AI narrative generation quality -- whether AI-generated narratives from structured inputs match the facilitator's manual quality is untested
  • Facilitator adoption in practice -- addressed architecturally, but real adoption requires building, using with actual clients, and iterating on gaps

Video Kickoff

60-90 second facilitator video preserves the personal, human connection.

+

Who Acts

Client watches the video. Session initializes at 0% progress.

What Happens

A clean, branded page with a facilitator video: "This little interview takes about 20 minutes. We've pulled some info from your website to save time." One button: "Let's Go."

Links client identity to pre-scraped data and framework template. Progress tracker initializes. Session start time logged.

Step 3

Self-Segmentation

Client picks their sophistication level. The system adapts the entire interview path.

+

Who Acts

Client selects from four options that describe their situation -- no jargon, no judgment.

What Happens

Options: "Materials ready to share" / "Scattered or outdated" / "Most is in our heads" / "Early stage." Each routes to an appropriate depth and style of interview.

System Response

Records segmentation, configures the question tree. "Scattered" clients get review mode + upload prompts. "In our heads" clients get open-ended storytelling questions.

Critical Design Detail

Options use client language, not framework jargon. No option implies inadequacy. System handles wrong-segment selection gracefully -- adapts mid-flow if answers don't match the selected path.

Step 4

Pre-Populated Review

"We did our homework" -- client reviews AI-drafted content from the web scrape.

+

Who Acts

Client reviews pre-filled framework sections with three options: "This is right" / "Let me edit" / "Let me rewrite."

What Happens

Instead of blank fields, cards show AI-generated content: "Based on what we found, [Company] operates in the direct-to-consumer wellness space..." Client edits inline.

System Response

Saves edits, tags sections as "client-reviewed and modified." Logs deltas between AI drafts and client edits -- these reveal what public narrative misses about actual strategy.

Content clearly marked as AI-generated. All three response options always visible. For thin-web-presence clients, system shifts to question mode: "We couldn't find much online -- let's build this together." Transition between review mode and question mode must be seamless.

Step 5

Guided Deep-Dive

Branching questions fill framework gaps. Contextual file upload prompts at the right moments.

+

Who Acts

Client answers one question at a time. System adapts follow-ups based on answers. Client uploads files in context.

What Happens

"Who are 2-3 companies your customers would consider?" Typed answers trigger smart follow-ups. "Do you have a competitive analysis you could upload?" -- timed perfectly.

System Response

Each answer tagged to framework slots. Uploaded files processed: text extracted, key claims identified, mapped to supplement answers. Redundant follow-ups skipped. Progress bar jumps.

Critical Design Detail

Branching logic is the intellectual core. If client says "we don't have competitors," system pivots: "Who were customers using before they found you?" Upload prompts are contextual, not generic. File processing fast enough to adapt subsequent questions.

🎤
Step 6

Flexible Input

Voice, text, links, file uploads -- whatever works for the client, on every question.

+

Who Acts

Client uses whichever input mode suits the question. A "talker" records a 90-second founder story. A "linker" pastes podcast URLs.

What Happens

Text field is default. Microphone icon for voice recording. Link icon for pasting URLs. File upload always available. Client mixes and matches freely.

System Response

Voice transcribed in near-real-time. Links queued for scraping. All inputs merged into unified framework sections with source attribution. System notes which input mode produced richest content.

Critical Design Detail

Voice must work reliably on mobile and desktop with a single tap. No app downloads. Client can review transcription before moving on. All four input types available on every question -- clients never forced into a single mode.

Step 7

Pause & Return

Auto-save, cross-device resume, smart reminders calibrated to workshop date.

+

Who Acts

Client closes browser mid-session. System auto-saved everything. Returns hours later on a different device.

What Happens

No save button, no warning dialog. 4 hours later, a reminder: "You're 65% done -- about 8 minutes left. Workshop in 11 days." Magic link restores exact position.

System Response

Auto-saves after every interaction. Magic-link URLs for session resumption without login. Cross-device tracking. Reminder timing adapts: gentle when distant, direct when close.

Critical Design Detail

No "are you sure?" dialogs. Max one reminder per 24 hours. Tone is "we saved your progress" not "you haven't finished." Magic links are single-use per click (token refreshes) for security.

Step 8

Review & Submit

Summary screen with completeness indicators per section. Submit when ready.

+

Who Acts

Client scrolls through all framework sections. Green checks for complete, yellow flags for thin (optional to add more).

What Happens

Summary shows merged content from all sources. Yellow-flagged sections have gentle suggestions. Client can tap in to add more or submit as-is.

System Response

Packages all inputs into structured framework document with metadata. Calculates completeness score. Notifies facilitator with score, file count, and review-ready link.

Critical Design Detail

Summary is scannable -- section titles with excerpts and status, not full text dumps. Yellow flags are optional. System never blocks submission for incomplete sections. Completeness score is for facilitator only -- client never sees a grade.

Step 9

Facilitator Dashboard

Structured framework view with gap detection and one-click follow-up approval.

+

Who Acts

Facilitator reviews structured submission on the dashboard. Content organized by framework section, not submission chronology.

What Happens

Framework sections as cards with completeness indicators and content type breakdown. Gaps flagged with one-click follow-up: system drafts the message, facilitator approves.

System Response

Shows content provenance per section (AI-drafted, client-edited, client-authored, file-extracted, voice-transcribed). Follow-up messages drafted in facilitator's voice. Pipeline view for all active clients.

Critical Design Detail

Content organized by framework section, not submission chronology. Facilitator doesn't care that a PDF was uploaded at 3:47 PM -- they care that Competitive Landscape has three competitors with differentiation. Follow-up messages use facilitator's voice, not system-speak.

Step 10

Framework-Ready Package

Compiled prompt inputs with source attribution, ready for narrative generation.

+

Who Acts

Facilitator clicks "Generate Narrative Package." Reviews compiled sections, adjusts as needed, triggers narrative generation.

What Happens

All sources compiled into structured prompt inputs: pre-populated content, client edits, interview answers, file extractions, voice transcriptions, follow-up responses. Sources cited per section.

System Response

Output maps one-to-one to the facilitator's existing prompt framework. Feeds into the AI narrative generation pipeline. Facilitator effort: ~15 minutes of review vs. 3-5 hours of manual collection.

Critical Design Detail

Output format is sacred -- the system adapts to the facilitator's workflow, not the other way around. Source attribution is essential: the facilitator needs to know if a claim came from the client's voice recording, uploaded document, or AI web scrape, because reliability weighting differs.

Architecture Decisions

Key Decisions

Five architecture decision records that shape the system's design. Each captures the context, the choice, the alternatives considered, and the consequences.

1

Branching logic as JSON config, not hardcoded

+

Context: The intake flow adapts based on client self-segmentation (4 paths) and prior answers. This branching logic is the intellectual core of the system.

Decision: Store the question tree as a JSON configuration in the Framework Template Store -- a directed graph where nodes are questions, edges are conditional transitions, and nodes reference framework section keys. The Intake Flow Engine traverses this graph at runtime.

Why not hardcode? The facilitator must adjust question sequences without code deployments. The question tree is a content/strategy artifact, not an engineering artifact.

Why not Typeform/Tally? No commercial form tool supports the hybrid of pre-populated review mode (confirm/edit/rewrite AI drafts) and question mode within a single flow.

Consequence: The facilitator can modify the question tree through configuration. A dedicated "question tree validator" must verify all paths are reachable and all terminal nodes produce complete framework coverage.

2

Two-tier web scraping (lightweight + deep async)

+

Context: Client websites vary enormously: static HTML, JS-rendered SPAs, behind login walls, minimal single-page sites, and complex multi-hundred-page corporate sites.

Decision: Tier 1 (fast): HTTP-based scraping with cheerio/jsdom for static pages. Tier 2 (fallback): headless browser (Playwright) for JS-rendered sites. Limit crawl to 2 levels, priority pages first, cap at 25 pages. LinkedIn/social via separate rate-limited workers.

Why not full recursive crawl? Large corporate sites produce thousands of irrelevant pages. The intake needs depth on key pages, not breadth.

Consequence: Scrape must complete in under 5 minutes (before welcome email sends). Login-walled pages are skipped and flagged. Thin-web-presence clients get adjusted email copy. Scrape failures never block the intake flow.

3

Multi-modal input on every question

+

Context: Clients must provide input via text, voice, file upload, and link pasting. Each type requires different processing.

Decision: Every question supports all four input types simultaneously. Text is the default; voice, file, and link are secondary. Non-text inputs processed asynchronously -- client gets immediate confirmation and can proceed without waiting.

Why not input-type-per-question? Removes client agency. A founder who prefers speaking should be able to voice-record any answer. Why not synchronous processing? A 90-second recording takes 10-15 seconds to transcribe; blocking during that time breaks the conversational rhythm.

Consequence: The system handles eventual consistency -- framework sections may update as async processing completes. The facilitator dashboard shows processing status indicators for pending items.

4

Magic-link sessions (no account creation)

+

Context: Clients start on one device, close the browser, and return hours or days later on a different device. The system must preserve their exact position.

Decision: Token-based magic-link sessions. URL contains the session token, also stored in HTTP-only cookie for same-device return. Auto-save after every interaction. Session URL included in all reminder communications. Tokens expire 30 days after workshop date.

Why not email/password accounts? Adding friction to a one-time, 20-minute flow is disproportionate. It would reduce completion rates. Why not browser-only sessions? Does not support cross-device resumption, which is a core requirement.

Consequence: Magic links are sensitive -- they grant access without authentication. Links are single-use per click (token refreshes on access). System handles concurrent access gracefully. Session data stored server-side, not in the browser.

5

Hybrid framework mapping (structural + AI)

+

Context: All inputs must be mapped to the facilitator's prompt framework categories. This mapping bridges raw client input and the structured output the facilitator needs.

Decision: First-pass is structural: each interview question is pre-tagged with its target framework section (100% confidence). Second-pass is AI-driven: uploaded files and voice recordings spanning multiple sections are analyzed and distributed with confidence scores. Facilitator can override AI mapping in the dashboard.

Why not purely structural? Uploaded files (e.g., pitch decks) contain content spanning multiple sections. Single-section tagging loses information. Why not purely AI-driven? Introduces uncertainty for content with an obvious home -- an answer to a competitor question belongs in competitive-landscape with certainty.

Consequence: 70-80% of content mapped with certainty via structural tagging. Only multi-section content requires AI classification, reducing cost and error rate. Initial calibration sessions with the facilitator are essential for tuning AI mapping prompts.

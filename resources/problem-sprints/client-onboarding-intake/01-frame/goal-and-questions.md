# Goal and Sprint Questions

## Long-Term Goal

**In 2 years, workshop clients will arrive at Day 1 with a complete, high-quality market narrative already built — by submitting their raw materials through a guided intake system that automatically collects, organizes, and synthesizes business context into the structured framework the workshop requires.**

This goal reframes the problem from "make the facilitator's prep work faster" to "make the client's contribution seamless and the narrative generation automatic." The facilitator should spend zero hours chasing materials and manually assembling prompts. The system handles intake, the AI handles synthesis, and the facilitator reviews and refines.

---

## Sprint Questions

### 1. Can we get clients to actually submit materials through a structured system instead of texting, emailing, and sending files ad hoc?

- **Risk Level:** HIGH
- **Rationale:** This is the single biggest behavioral risk. Clients currently send materials however is most convenient for them — a text with a thought, an email with a PDF, a Dropbox link dropped in a call. Any solution requires clients to change their behavior or requires the system to meet them where they already are. If clients won't use the intake system, nothing downstream works. The entire sprint hinges on whether material collection can be consolidated without creating friction that damages the client relationship or the brand of the workshop experience.

### 2. Will the system produce a market narrative of equal or better quality than what the facilitator creates manually?

- **Risk Level:** HIGH
- **Rationale:** The market narrative is the foundation of the first workshop session. If automated synthesis produces something generic, incomplete, or structurally wrong, the facilitator loses trust in the system and reverts to manual work — or worse, the workshop quality degrades. The current manual process benefits from the facilitator's judgment, pattern recognition, and ability to fill gaps by asking follow-up questions. The system must replicate or exceed this quality bar, or it becomes a liability rather than an asset.

### 3. Can we handle the full spectrum of client digital sophistication — from polished brand decks to "we just have our website and some stories"?

- **Risk Level:** HIGH
- **Rationale:** Client inputs vary wildly. Some businesses have brand guides, pitch decks, and market research. Others have a website they dislike and a founder who communicates in voice memos. The system must accommodate both extremes without making either feel inadequate. If the intake only works well for digitally mature clients, it excludes a significant portion of the market. If it only works for minimal-input clients, it fails to leverage the rich materials sophisticated clients bring.

### 4. Can we build this without making the pre-workshop experience feel impersonal or transactional?

- **Risk Level:** MEDIUM
- **Rationale:** The workshop itself is "streamlined and loved." The pre-workshop relationship is part of that brand. If clients go from texting directly with the facilitator to filling out a form in a portal, the warmth and personal touch could disappear. The onboarding experience sets the emotional tone for the entire engagement. A system that feels like enterprise software could undermine the trust and excitement that makes the workshop successful.

### 5. Will the facilitator actually trust and adopt this system, or will they continue to manually review and redo everything?

- **Risk Level:** MEDIUM
- **Rationale:** The facilitator is the business owner. They have deep expertise in what makes a good market narrative. Even with a working system, there is a risk they become a bottleneck by insisting on manually reviewing every output in detail — effectively doing the same work with an extra step. The system must produce outputs that earn the facilitator's confidence quickly, or adoption stalls at a single user.

---

## How These Questions Guide the Sprint

| Question | If we fail here... | What it means for the solution |
|----------|-------------------|-------------------------------|
| Q1 — Client submission behavior | Clients ignore the system; materials still arrive scattered | Must design for multi-channel capture OR radical simplicity |
| Q2 — Narrative quality | Facilitator can't trust the output; reverts to manual | Must nail the AI synthesis pipeline and prompt framework |
| Q3 — Sophistication spectrum | System only works for some clients; limits growth | Must design flexible intake that adapts to what clients have |
| Q4 — Personal touch | Brand experience degrades; clients feel processed | Must preserve relational warmth in a systematized flow |
| Q5 — Facilitator adoption | System is built but unused; no time saved | Must build confidence through transparency and control |

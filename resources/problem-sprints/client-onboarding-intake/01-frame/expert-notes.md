# Expert Notes — HMW Notes and Insights

## How Might We (HMW) Notes

### Theme 1: Meeting Clients Where They Are (Channel Flexibility)

1. **HMW let clients send materials however they want while still capturing everything in one place?**
2. **HMW turn a text message with a rambling thought into structured input for the narrative?**
3. **HMW automatically pull content from a client's website even if the client doesn't like it?** (The website still contains real data — company description, offerings, team, positioning signals.)
4. **HMW accept materials from Dropbox, Google Drive, email, and secure file shares without the facilitator manually downloading each one?**
5. **HMW capture verbal anecdotes and stories as usable narrative input?**

### Theme 2: Guiding Clients Through What to Provide (Intake Design)

6. **HMW help clients who "don't have much" realize they actually have enough?**
7. **HMW show clients exactly what we need without overwhelming them with a long checklist?**
8. **HMW progressively ask smarter follow-up questions based on what's already been submitted?**
9. **HMW make the intake feel like a conversation rather than a form?**
10. **HMW adapt the intake experience for clients at different levels of digital sophistication?**

### Theme 3: Automating the Messy Middle (Collection to Framework)

11. **HMW automatically categorize incoming materials against the prompt framework?**
12. **HMW extract text from PDFs, transcribe voice memos, and parse email threads without manual effort?**
13. **HMW detect when enough material has been collected to generate a quality narrative?**
14. **HMW identify gaps in collected materials and auto-generate the right follow-up questions?**
15. **HMW eliminate the manual step of organizing raw materials into the prompt structure?**

### Theme 4: Narrative Quality and Trust (AI Synthesis)

16. **HMW ensure the AI-generated narrative matches the quality the facilitator produces manually?**
17. **HMW let the facilitator review and adjust the narrative without redoing the entire process?**
18. **HMW build the facilitator's trust in the system incrementally — starting with assist, moving toward autopilot?**
19. **HMW create a feedback loop where facilitator edits improve future narrative generation?**

### Theme 5: Scaling Without Losing the Human Touch (Experience Design)

20. **HMW handle 10 concurrent client intakes without any single client feeling deprioritized?**
21. **HMW keep the pre-workshop experience warm and personal even when it's systematized?**
22. **HMW give clients visibility into their onboarding progress without making it feel like a project management tool?**
23. **HMW let the facilitator maintain a personal relationship with each client while the system handles logistics?**

### Theme 6: Business Model and Growth (Strategic)

24. **HMW use the intake system to actually improve the workshop product — not just save time?**
25. **HMW turn the onboarding process itself into a value-add that clients appreciate?**

## Expert Insights

### Technology Perspective

**AI document processing has matured significantly.** Modern LLMs can extract structured information from PDFs, parse unstructured text from emails and messages, transcribe audio, and scrape websites — all capabilities that directly address the manual consolidation bottleneck (Steps 5-7 on the map). The technology for the "messy middle" automation exists today. The challenge is not capability but integration: building a pipeline that handles the variety of input formats clients produce and maps them reliably to the prompt framework.

**Website scraping as a baseline input is underutilized.** Even when clients say "we don't like our website," the site contains structured data: company name, offerings, team bios, customer testimonials, positioning language, and market signals. A system that automatically ingests and parses the client's website on Day 0 would give the intake a running start — the client then fills gaps and corrects rather than starting from scratch.

> **Sprint question implication:** Directly addresses Q3 (sophistication spectrum) — website scraping provides a floor of input quality even for clients with minimal materials.

### Market Dynamics Perspective

**The "productized service" model is booming, and onboarding is the universal bottleneck.** Agencies, consultancies, and workshop businesses across industries face this exact problem: the service delivery is excellent, but pre-engagement intake is a manual, bespoke process that limits growth. Whoever solves onboarding intake for knowledge-intensive services creates a competitive moat. The market is moving toward self-serve onboarding — but the winners will be those who make self-serve feel concierge.

**Client expectations are set by consumer software, not B2B tools.** Clients expect the ease of iMessage, not the complexity of a project management portal. Any intake system that feels like "enterprise software" will face adoption resistance. The bar is set by the simplest digital experiences clients already use daily.

> **Sprint question implication:** Directly addresses Q4 (personal touch) and Q1 (client submission behavior) — the system must feel like a modern consumer experience, not a business portal.

### User Behavior Perspective

**People submit information in bursts, not batches.** Expecting a client to sit down and complete an intake form in one session is unrealistic. Real behavior is: they send a PDF from their laptop during a meeting, text a thought while driving, email a Dropbox link from their assistant, and mention something on a call. The system must accommodate asynchronous, multi-session, multi-channel input.

**The "what should I send?" anxiety is real.** Clients who lack polished brand materials feel embarrassed or uncertain. They delay. They apologize for their website. They ask "is this what you need?" repeatedly. A system that reduces this anxiety — by showing them what it already knows and asking only for what's missing — would accelerate the entire timeline.

**Follow-up fatigue kills momentum.** Every follow-up request from the facilitator is a minor friction point. After 2-3 rounds, clients start to disengage or rush through answers. The system should minimize follow-up cycles by asking the right questions upfront and making the gap between "what we have" and "what we need" visible.

> **Sprint question implication:** Directly addresses Q1 (client submission behavior) and Q3 (sophistication spectrum) — the system must handle burst submissions and proactively reduce client anxiety about what to provide.

### Business Model Perspective

**The facilitator's time is the most expensive resource in the business, and it's currently spent on the lowest-value work.** Downloading files from Dropbox, copying text from emails, and organizing PDFs into folders is work that should cost near zero but currently consumes hours per client. Every hour the facilitator spends on intake logistics is an hour not spent on selling, delivering, or improving the workshop. At scale, this is not just an operational problem — it is a revenue ceiling.

**The intake process is invisible to clients but defines the product ceiling.** If the narrative quality varies because the facilitator was rushed or a file was missed, the workshop suffers. Clients never see the intake process, but they experience its output. Systematizing intake is not just about efficiency — it is about consistency of the end product.

**There is an opportunity to make onboarding a revenue event, not a cost center.** If the intake system is well-designed, it can become part of the value proposition: "When you sign up, our AI immediately starts analyzing your market." This turns a cost (manual intake) into a feature (instant market intelligence).

> **Sprint question implication:** Directly addresses Q5 (facilitator adoption) and Q2 (narrative quality) — the system must free the facilitator from logistics while maintaining or improving output consistency. If the facilitator sees better narratives with less effort, adoption follows naturally.

## 1. Practice Acquisition: Deal Structure & Pricing Strategy

### Market Context: EBITDA Multiples Landscape

The DSO acquisition market in 2025-2026 operates within defined valuation bands. SGA's deal structure must be positioned relative to these benchmarks.

| Practice Profile | Typical EBITDA Multiple | Revenue Multiple | Key Drivers |
|---|---|---|---|
| Community practice (1-3 doctors, $400K-$800K EBITDA) | 5-7x EBITDA | 75-120% of revenue | Operator dependency, limited growth runway, rural/suburban location |
| Group practice (4-10 doctors, $800K-$2M EBITDA) | 7-9x EBITDA | 120-200% of revenue | Multiple providers reduce key-person risk, established systems, growth trajectory |
| High-performing group ($2M+ EBITDA, multi-location) | 9-12x EBITDA | 200-300% of revenue | Scale, diversified revenue, strong management, strategic geography |
| Specialty practice (perio, oral surgery, implantology) | 6-8x EBITDA | 100-175% of revenue | Specialist practices are harder to transition; referral dependency, narrow talent pool |
| Strategic cluster fill (any size, fills a geographic gap) | +0.5-1.5x premium above category | Premium for network effect | SGA pays more when a practice completes a regional cluster or enters a new market |

**2026 market ceiling:** 12x EBITDA remains the practical ceiling except in extraordinary cases (multi-location, high-growth, strategically critical targets).

### Van Westendorp-Style Analysis: What Practice Owners Expect vs. Reality

Based on voice of customer research and market data, practice owners arrive at the deal table with expectations shaped by broker conversations, peer anecdotes, and late-night Google searches. Their perception of value maps roughly as follows:

| Perception Threshold | Multiple Range | Seller Psychology |
|---|---|---|
| **"Too cheap" (insulting)** | Below 5x EBITDA | "They don't value what I built. This is a lowball." Sellers walk away. |
| **"Good value" (pleasant surprise)** | 6-7x EBITDA | "This is fair. I can see myself doing this." |
| **Indifference price point** | 7-8x EBITDA | Market center. Sellers feel neither overpaid nor underpaid. This is where most mid-market DSO deals close. |
| **"Getting expensive" (buyer hesitation)** | 9-10x EBITDA | Buyer pays this only for strategic acquisitions or high-growth targets. Sellers feel they "won." |
| **"Too expensive" (market rejection)** | Above 11x EBITDA | Only defensible for large, multi-location, high-EBITDA practices in strategic markets. |

**SGA's positioning:** Target the 7-9x band for standard acquisitions, with willingness to stretch to 10-11x for strategic cluster fills and high-performing groups. Below 7x, SGA loses deals to MB2, Guardian, and DCA. Above 11x, the economics break.

The critical insight from voice of customer data: the multiple is necessary but not sufficient. Dentists who report regret ("seven or eight out of 10 are frustrated or regretful") got acceptable multiples. What failed was the post-deal reality -- autonomy erosion, broken support promises, equity structures that underperformed. SGA's competitive advantage is not paying the highest multiple. It is delivering on everything the multiple buys.

## 3. Patient Services: Implant Pricing Strategy

### Implant Pricing Landscape (MODIS/Innovative Dental Vertical)

| Procedure | National Price Range | SGA/MODIS Target | Positioning |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Single implant (crown included)** | $3,000-$6,000 | $3,500-$5,500 | Mid-market. Competitive but not discount. Quality materials, experienced surgeons. |
| **All-on-4 (per arch)** | $20,000-$35,000 | $22,000-$30,000 | Premium-accessible. Not the cheapest (signals quality), not the most expensive (not exclusionary). |
| **Full-mouth (both arches)** | $40,000-$70,000 | $40,000-$55,000 | Transparent pricing. Dr. Olson's commitment to honesty: "I want to be transparent because I think it is difficult for people to find that answer." |
| **Zygomatic implants (complex)** | $30,000-$50,000/arch | $28,000-$45,000 | Premium procedure requiring specialized skill. Pikos-trained surgeons justify the investment. |
| **Snap-in denture (implant-retained)** | $12,000-$25,000 | $14,000-$22,000 | Entry point for patients who cannot afford fixed All-on-4 but want implant stability. |

### Financing Strategy

Linda Dawson -- the retired teacher on $48K/year -- cannot write a check for $45,000. Financing is not a nice-to-have. It is the mechanism that converts intent into treatment.

| Financing Option | Terms | Target Patient | Monthly Payment Example |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Third-party medical financing (CareCredit, Proceed Finance)** | 0% for 12-24 months on qualifying amounts; 8.99-14.99% APR for longer terms | Patients with 680+ credit score | $40K full-mouth at 60 months = ~$750/month (0% promo) or ~$890/month (9.9% APR) |
| **In-house payment plan** | 3-6 month interest-free for deposits; balance at treatment | Patients who need short-term flexibility | $5,000 deposit split over 3 months = $1,667/month, remainder financed |
| **Insurance coordination** | Accept PPO insurance; maximize benefits toward treatment cost | Patients with dental insurance (even partial coverage) | Insurance covers $1,500-$3,000 of implant costs, reducing out-of-pocket by 5-10% |
| **Bundled pricing** | All-inclusive fee covering surgery, prosthetics, follow-up, and warranty | All implant patients | Eliminates "surprise bills." Patient knows the total cost from day one. |

**Pricing psychology:** Lead with the monthly payment, not the total cost. "$249/month for teeth that last a lifetime" reframes the investment from "impossible" to "manageable." The sticker price ($45K) triggers loss aversion. The monthly frame ($249) triggers comparison to existing denture costs, adhesive purchases, and avoided dental bills.

2. **Valuation environment:** EBITDA multiples in 2025-2026 are at or near cyclical highs for mid-market dental practices. Interest rate movements, PE fund deployment cycles, and market saturation could compress multiples.
3. **Technology gap acceleration:** Every year a practice delays investment in digital workflows, guided implant systems, and modern patient experience, the gap widens. SGA provides that capital investment immediately.
4. **Talent market:** The staffing crisis in dentistry is not improving. 76% of practices find hygienist recruiting "extremely challenging." SGA's recruiting infrastructure and benefits package become more valuable as the labor market tightens.

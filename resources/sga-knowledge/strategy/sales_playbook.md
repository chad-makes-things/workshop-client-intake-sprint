## 1. Sales Call Scripts

### Guiding Principles for All Calls

Before reading any script below, internalize these rules. They override any specific language in the templates.

1. **Talk 43%, listen 57%.** This is not a pitch. It is a peer-level conversation with someone who built a business from nothing and is deciding whether to trust you with it.
2. **Mirror their language.** Dentists say "my practice," "my team," "my patients." Never say "your asset," "the business," or "the entity." It is always theirs.
3. **Never trash a competitor by name.** Draw contrasts against the MODEL. "Some DSOs do X. We do Y. Here is why." Let the prospect connect the dots.
4. **Lead with evidence, not claims.** Names. Numbers. Verified outcomes. "Clinical autonomy" is category wallpaper. Every DSO says it. Prove it.
5. **Acknowledge the fear before countering it.** "You have heard the horror stories. So have we. That is exactly why we built something different." Never dismiss skepticism -- it is earned and legitimate.
6. **This is a trust funnel, not a sales funnel.** The timeline is 6-18 months. Manufactured urgency is toxic. Authentic urgency (market conditions, valuation environment, talent pressure) is fair game.

### Discovery Call Script (15-20 min)

**Goal:** Qualify the prospect, identify primary pain, understand their decision landscape, and earn the right to a deeper conversation.

**Pre-call preparation:**
- Review practice website, Google reviews, state dental board records
- Check LinkedIn for the owner's career history, associations, speaking engagements
- Note practice size (operatories visible in photos), number of providers listed, specialties offered
- Check if they have spoken with other DSOs (search their name on Dentaltown, dental transition forums)
- Prepare 2-3 specific observations about their practice to reference in the opening

---

#### OPENING: Rapport and Agenda (2-3 min)

```
"Dr. [Name], thanks for making time. I know you have patients on
either side of this call, so I want to be respectful of your schedule.

Before we get into anything, I am curious -- what prompted you to
take this conversation? What is on your mind?"
```

**[LISTEN. This answer tells you everything. Let them talk. Do not interrupt.]**

If they give a vague answer ("just exploring," "a colleague mentioned you"):

```
"Totally fair. Most of the dentists I talk to were not planning to
call anyone. Something just started nagging -- maybe the HR headaches
got worse, maybe a broker called, maybe they saw a peer do something
interesting. Is there a version of that for you?"
```

**Set the up-front contract (Sandler method):**

```
"Here is what I would like to do with our time: I will ask you some
questions to understand your practice and what you are trying to solve.
You can ask me anything -- no topic is off limits. And at the end, we
will both decide honestly whether it makes sense to keep talking.
If it does, great. If it does not, I will tell you and I hope you
will tell me. Sound fair?"
```

#### DISCOVERY: Problem Identification (5-7 min)

**Problem Questions (uncover the pain they came with):**

```
"What is the single biggest headache in running your practice right
now -- not clinical, but the business side?"
```

```
"If I talked to your office manager right now, what would she say
keeps her up at night?"
```

```
"How are you handling recruiting? We hear from a lot of owners that
finding hygienists has become nearly impossible."
```

**Implication Questions (most powerful -- make the cost of inaction concrete):**

```
"When you are doing books at midnight or chasing an insurance claim
instead of being home, what does that cost you? Not just in dollars
-- what does it cost you personally?"
```

```
"You mentioned [specific problem]. If nothing changes and that
continues for the next 3-5 years, what does that look like?"
```

```
"How much time per week would you say you spend on non-clinical
work? And what would you do with that time if you got it back?"
```

**Need-Payoff Questions (let them articulate the value):**

```
"If you could wave a wand and fix one thing about the business side
of your practice, what would it be?"
```

```
"What would it mean for you if someone handled all of that --
billing, payroll, HR, compliance, recruiting -- and you just
practiced dentistry?"
```

#### QUALIFICATION CHECKLIST

During discovery, confirm these data points. Do not ask them as a rapid-fire list -- weave them into conversation naturally.

| Data Point | What You Need | Why It Matters |
|---|---|---|
| Practice size | Locations, ops, providers | Determines deal tier (community, group, specialty) |
| Revenue | Approximate annual revenue | Minimum $1M for community; $1.25M+ ideal |
| EBITDA estimate | Owner compensation + add-backs | Drives valuation range |
| Years in practice | How long they have owned | Indicates career stage and motivations |
| Staff count | FTE clinical and admin | Transition complexity indicator |
| What prompted interest | Why now? | Reveals primary pain and urgency |
| Timeline | When they envision making a decision | Sets realistic follow-up cadence |
| Decision influencers | Spouse, financial advisor, attorney, partner | Identifies who else you need to convince |
| Prior DSO conversations | Who they have talked to | Competitive intelligence |
| Specialty mix | General, perio, implants, ortho, peds | Routes to correct SGA vertical |

## 2. Objection Handling Playbook

### Framework: LAER Method

Every objection follows the same process:

1. **Listen** -- Let them finish completely. Do not interrupt. Silence is a tool.
2. **Acknowledge** -- Validate the concern without agreeing with the premise. Never say "I understand, but..."
3. **Explore** -- Ask a follow-up question to find the root issue. The stated objection is rarely the real one.
4. **Resolve** -- Address with evidence: a named partner, a specific number, a verifiable fact. Then confirm: "Does that address your concern?"

---

### Top 15 Objections

---

#### Objection 1: "I don't want to lose control of my practice."

**Underlying fear:** "I built this from nothing and a corporation is going to tell me how to run it."

**Listen:** Let them describe exactly what "control" means to them. It is usually specific: materials, lab, schedule, staffing, treatment planning.

**Acknowledge:**

```
"That is the right concern to have. If I were in your shoes with
22 years of my life in this practice, it would be my first question
too. And honestly, the fact that you are asking it tells me you have
done your homework on what happens at other DSOs."
```

**Explore:**

```
"When you say 'control,' what specifically are you most protective
of? Is it who you work with, how you treat patients, what materials
you use -- or is it all of the above?"
```

**Resolve:**

```
"Here is how SGA handles this, and I want to be specific because
vague promises are worthless.

You choose your materials. You choose your lab. You set your
schedule. You determine treatment plans. SGA does not have 'crown
days' or 'Invisalign days.' There are no production quotas.

Our Chief Clinical Officer is Dr. Mitch Ellingson -- a practicing
dentist who left private practice specifically to protect other
dentists from losing their autonomy. His exact words: 'I love being
a dentist, and I want to protect my profession from becoming like
medicine.'

And here is the proof: call Dr. Godat at Periodontal Associates of
Memphis. Still his name. Still his team. Still his way of practicing.
Ask him if anyone has ever told him how to practice."
```

**Confirm:**

```
"Does that address the concern, or is there a specific scenario you
are worried about that I have not covered?"
```

### Proposal Template Outline

Every SGA partnership proposal should follow this structure, in this order. The order is deliberate: lead with the relationship, not the numbers.

#### Section 3: Financial Summary (2 pages)

- ROI comparison: solo vs. SGA partnership (customized to their numbers)
- Tax considerations overview (encourage independent review)

#### Section 4: The SGA Difference (1 page)
- The Thurston Group: 38 years, $4B+, 8 platforms

- Three verticals: community, collaborative, implantology

- Named partner references with contact information

#### Section 5: Partner Stories (1-2 pages)
- 2-3 case studies of partners in similar situations
- Before/after narrative arc (before state, turning point, current state)
- Specific metrics where available (revenue growth, staff retention, clinical development)
- Direct quotes from named partners

#### Section 6: What Happens Next (1 page)
- Timeline from proposal to close
- Key milestones and owner responsibilities
- Transition team introduction
- How to reach us with questions
- Clear call to action: "Schedule a conversation to discuss this proposal"

#### Appendix
- Full operational support detail
- Benefits summary for staff
- Technology investment options
- SGA leadership bios
- Thurston Group overview

#### Proposal Presentation Rules

1. **Never email a proposal without a walkthrough.** Always present it live (in-person preferred, video call acceptable). The proposal is a conversation tool, not a leave-behind.
2. **Start with Section 1, not Section 3.** The emotional connection comes before the numbers.
3. **Bring a current partner to the proposal meeting** when possible. Peer validation at this stage is the highest-leverage move.
4. **Leave the financial section for last.** By the time you get to numbers, they should already believe in the model.
5. **After presenting, ask:** "What questions does this raise?" Not "What do you think?" The first invites dialogue; the second invites a snap judgment.

## 4. Sales Process Map

### Stage Definitions

SGA's sales process has 7 stages, mapped to the typical 6-18 month dental practice acquisition cycle. Each stage has defined entry criteria, required actions, and exit criteria.

---

| Stage | Name | Description | Entry Criteria | Exit Criteria | Avg Duration |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **1** | **Lead** | Initial contact or inbound inquiry. No qualification yet. | Name + practice + contact info | Discovery call scheduled | 1-2 weeks |
| **2** | **Qualified** | Discovery call completed. Practice meets minimum criteria. Pain identified. | Discovery call completed; revenue > $1M; EBITDA > $400K; owner interested | Deep-dive call scheduled | 1-2 weeks |
| **3** | **Discovery** | Deep-dive call completed. Avatar mapped. Competitive landscape understood. Stakeholders identified. | Deep-dive call completed; primary pain confirmed; decision influencers named | Prospect agrees to receive a proposal OR requests partner reference call | 2-4 weeks |
| **4** | **Proposal** | Formal partnership proposal delivered and presented. Financial model shared. | Proposal delivered via live walkthrough; partner reference provided | Prospect engages with proposal (asks questions, shares with advisors, requests modifications) | 4-8 weeks |
| **5** | **Negotiation** | Active deal structuring. Terms being discussed. Advisors and attorneys involved. | Prospect indicates intent to move forward; valuation discussion underway | Verbal agreement on key terms (multiple, structure, timeline) | 4-8 weeks |
| **6** | **Due Diligence** | Legal and financial review. LOI signed. Practice financials audited. | LOI executed; legal counsel engaged on both sides | Due diligence complete; definitive agreement drafted | 4-8 weeks |
| **7** | **Close** | Definitive agreement signed. Partnership official. Transition begins. | Definitive agreement executed | First 30-day transition milestones completed | 1-2 weeks to sign; 90 days to full transition |

---

### Average Time per Stage

Based on dental practice acquisition industry benchmarks, adjusted for SGA's JV equity model (which typically takes longer than outright acquisitions due to the equity education and trust-building required).

| Stage | Best Case | Average | Worst Case | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Lead to Qualified | 3 days | 10 days | 30 days | Depends on scheduling; some owners take weeks to return calls |
| Qualified to Discovery | 5 days | 14 days | 45 days | Scheduling deep-dive; owner may need time to process |
| Discovery to Proposal | 7 days | 21 days | 60 days | Partner reference calls, spouse/advisor conversations |
| Proposal to Negotiation | 14 days | 42 days | 90 days | This is where deals stall most often. Advisor review, emotional processing, competitive shopping. |
| Negotiation to Due Diligence | 14 days | 30 days | 60 days | Term negotiation, attorney engagement |
| Due Diligence to Close | 21 days | 45 days | 90 days | Financial audit, legal review, landlord consent, credentialing |
| **Total: Lead to Close** | **~64 days (2 months)** | **~162 days (5.5 months)** | **~375 days (12.5 months)** | Industry average for DSO acquisitions: 3-9 months |

**Key insight:** The longest stage is almost always Proposal to Negotiation. This is where the dentist is processing the emotional weight of the decision. Do NOT rush this stage. Maintain contact, provide value, connect them with partner references, and let the relationship do the work.

### Pipeline Dashboard Recommendations

### Weekly Deal Review Framework

BD Director should conduct a 30-minute weekly review with each BD rep, covering the top 3-5 deals by priority.

**Five coaching questions per deal:**

1. "What is the identified pain, and who told you about it?"
2. "Who are all the decision influencers, and have you engaged them?"
3. "What is the biggest risk in this deal right now?"
4. "What is the next concrete action, and who owns it?"
5. "What would need to be true for this to close by [estimated date]?"

**Rules:**
- Ask questions before offering advice
- Use deal data to guide, not override, the rep's judgment
- Track coaching themes over time to identify systematic skill gaps
- If a deal has been in the same stage for 2x the average duration, make a go/no-go decision: escalate, change approach, or move to nurture

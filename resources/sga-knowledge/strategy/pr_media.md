# SGA Dental Partners -- PR and Media Relations Plan

**Prepared:** March 2026
**Status:** Strategic foundation for earned media, thought leadership, and press operations
**Scope:** B2B (practice acquisition, specialist recruitment) and B2B2C (patient-facing earned media)
**Brand Launch Moment:** Consolidation of Gen4 Dental Partners + MODIS Dental Partners under the SGA Dental Partners umbrella

---

## Strategic Overview

SGA Dental Partners is launching into a market where every DSO says the same things -- "clinical autonomy," "partnership not acquisition," "we're different." The media landscape mirrors this: dental trade press runs surface-level company announcements and deal flow news. Nobody is telling the deeper story.

SGA's PR strategy operates on a different premise. We are not pitching a company. We are pitching a thesis -- that the way dental consolidation has been done for 30 years is wrong, and that a Marine-turned-McKinsey-consultant, a practicing dentist, and a 38-year PE firm with $4B+ in returns decided to prove it.

That is a story journalists want to tell. Our job is to give them the proof to tell it.

**The PR north star:** Every media placement, every byline, every podcast appearance should move the reader/listener to one of two conclusions:
1. "I should explore a partnership with SGA" (B2B dentist audience)
2. "SGA is the kind of company I can trust with my dental care" (patient audience)

**Proof hierarchy for all media work:**
- Third-party validation (press mentions, awards, ADA endorsement) over self-generated authority
- Named customer results (Dr. Cavendish: +16.5% revenue, 100% staff retention) over abstract claims
- Specific numbers ("$4B+ returned over 38 years") over category language ("world-class PE")
- Founder/practitioner voices over corporate spokesperson language

### 1.5 HARO Strategy (Reactive PR)

HARO (Help a Reporter Out) and its competitors (Qwoted, SourceBottle, ProfNet) generate inbound journalist queries. A monitoring and response system for SGA should cover the following query categories:

**Monitor daily for queries on:**
- Dental practice ownership, DSO/dental consolidation, practice transitions
- Private equity in healthcare, PE-backed healthcare companies
- Small business acquisitions, entrepreneurial exit strategies
- Dental implants, implant dentistry market trends
- Dental continuing education, clinical training, dental technology
- Healthcare workforce retention, dental staffing

**Designated responders:**
- Lamonte Jensen: Business/PE/entrepreneurship queries, DSO market commentary
- Dr. Mitch Ellingson: Clinical autonomy, dentist wellbeing, "protecting the profession" angles
- Dr. Michael Pikos (MODIS): Implantology clinical innovation, education, CE
- Dr. Grant Olson: Patient-facing angles, dental implant patient experience, content strategy

**Response protocol:**
1. Identify queries within 2 hours of delivery (morning and afternoon review)
2. Match query to the best spokesperson based on angle
3. Draft a 150-250 word response with at least one data point and a specific proof story (not generic claims)
4. Include spokesperson bio, headshot link, and contact for follow-up
5. Track responses and resulting placements in the PR calendar

**Response tone standard:** HARO responses that win are specific, data-backed, and tell a human story. Generic "our company believes..." responses are ignored. Every HARO response from SGA should lead with a named example, a number, or a direct contradiction of a common belief.

### 2.4 Byline Article Outlines

#### Byline Article 1

**Title:** "Why 70% of Dentists Regret Selling to a DSO -- and How to Be in the Other 30%"

**Target Publication:** Dental Economics (primary), Group Dentistry Now (secondary)
**Author:** Dr. Mitch Ellingson, CCO, Gen4 Dental Partners / SGA Dental Partners
**Word count:** 1,200-1,500 words
**Tone:** Peer-to-peer, candid, evidence-based

**Outline:**

Opening hook: The 70% regret statistic from Dentist Advisors. Not an abstract number -- pull the language: "seven or eight out of ten are pretty bugged or annoyed or frustrated or regretful." This is a crisis, not a footnote.

Section 1 -- Why the regret happens (the structural problems, not excuses)
- The five things DSOs almost always get wrong: clinical autonomy in name only; staff displacement after acquisition; equity provisions dentists did not understand; production pressure disguised as "performance support"; losing the name and culture that patients trusted
- These are not execution failures -- they are design failures. The model was built for the DSO, not the dentist.

Section 2 -- What the 30% did differently
- They asked three questions before signing: What happens to my staff? Who makes clinical decisions? What does my equity actually mean in three years?
- They asked for referrals from current partners -- not testimonials on a website, but phone numbers of dentists in the platform
- They evaluated culture fit, not just deal multiples
- They chose platforms with a track record of exits -- not promises

Section 3 -- What we built at SGA specifically to solve this
- The Individual Growth Engine (mentorship, not management)
- Clinical autonomy protected by structure: CCO is a practicing dentist, not an operations executive
- JV equity model: partners own a piece of the platform, not just a promise
- The "dentists teaching dentists" culture -- what this looks like day-to-day
- Named proof: Dr. Cavendish's story with specific metrics

Closing: The question is not whether consolidation will happen. It will. The question is which platform you choose. And the way to choose is not to find the highest multiple -- it is to find the organization that makes you a better dentist after the deal closes.

CTA: Invite readers to request the Practice Partnership Assessment -- a self-evaluation tool.

### 3.3 FAQ for Media

The following questions represent the most common objections and angles journalists will pursue. Each answer is calibrated for a media context -- honest, specific, and responsive without being promotional.

**Category 1: The Private Equity Question**

**Q: Private equity in dentistry has a bad reputation. PE firms load up debt, squeeze margins, and flip practices in 3-5 years. Why should dentists trust SGA?**

A: The reputation is earned -- by most PE firms in the dental space. The reason Thurston Group built a different model is that they have watched those failures happen to competitors' platforms. Thurston has been building dental and healthcare platforms for 38 years. They have returned more than $4 billion to partners across 8 platforms. That is not a projected return -- that is historical. The specific challenge for any dentist evaluating a DSO is to look at the track record, not the pitch. Thurston's track record in dental is publicly verifiable: Smile Doctors, US Endodontics Partners, US Oral Surgery Management, and others. Dentists can call former partners from those exits and ask how it went. We tell them to.

**Q: What is Thurston Group's typical exit timeline, and what happens to partners when they exit?**

A: Thurston structures its investments as patient capital -- the timeline is tied to the platform's maturity, not a fund's return window. Across 38 years, they have operated under a thesis that the right time to exit is when the platform has maximized value for all partners, not when the fund clock runs out. On the equity question: SGA's JV equity model means partners own shares in the combined platform. What happens at exit depends on the deal structure individual partners agreed to at close -- and Thurston has a track record of transparent equity provisions, which is specifically why we encourage prospective partners to talk to people who have been through prior Thurston exits.

**Q: Are there legislative risks to the PE-in-dentistry model?**

A: Yes. Several states have considered or are considering legislation limiting PE involvement in dental practices. We monitor these developments actively. The clinical autonomy protections that are structurally built into SGA's model -- a practicing dentist as CCO, peer-led clinical governance, no clinical protocols set by operations -- are specifically designed to address the concerns that drive this legislation. The regulatory environment is one reason the clinical-autonomy-first model is not just good for dentists; it is good for the business.

---

**Category 2: The Clinical Autonomy Question**

**Q: Every DSO claims clinical autonomy. What makes SGA's claim different?**

A: Three structural elements, not marketing claims. First, the CCO is a practicing dentist. Dr. Mitch Ellingson spent 13 years owning practices in Phoenix before founding Gen4. He practices dentistry. He has sat in the chair the partners sit in. When the clinical advisory board makes decisions, they are made by clinicians, not operations executives. Second, no SGA partner has lost their practice name. Not one. Dental Partners of SW Georgia is still Dental Partners of SW Georgia. Periodontal Associates of Memphis is still Periodontal Associates of Memphis. Five and ten years in. Third, we publish our staff retention rate: 85%. No competitor publishes this number. Staff retention is the most reliable proxy for whether a practice's culture was preserved. If you force change on a practice, the staff leaves. Ours stays. Those are structural outputs, not promises.

**Q: What does clinical autonomy look like day-to-day? Do partners ever feel pressure to treat differently?**

A: The answer from Dr. Brian Harris is the most direct we have heard: "This organization is fully invested in us -- in us loving what we do and being successful in what we do so we can provide excellent care to our patients." We do not set treatment protocols from corporate. We do not run "crown days" or "Invisalign days" -- the language that shows up in Heartland's Glassdoor reviews as the exact opposite of what we do. Partners choose their own materials, their own labs, their own schedule. What we provide is the back-office infrastructure, the peer community, and the education platform. The clinical decision stays with the doctor.

---

**Category 3: The Consolidation Criticism Question**

**Q: DSO consolidation has been criticized for prioritizing profit over patient care. How does SGA respond?**

A: The criticism is correct when applied to Gen2-model DSOs that standardize care around cost reduction and production targets. That is why SGA was built specifically not to do that. The founding ethos from Dr. Ellingson is direct: "I love being a dentist, and I want to protect my profession from becoming like medicine. In medicine, as it consolidated it became less about the doctors and patients, and more about the organization." We built SGA because we believe consolidation and clinical excellence are not in conflict -- but only if the model keeps the doctor in charge of clinical decisions. The proof is in the partner results: Dr. Cavendish's practice has 100% staff retention and revenues up 16.5% since joining. That does not happen if the clinical model is being degraded.

**Q: How does SGA prevent the same problems that have plagued other DSOs?**

A: The structural protections are specific. Clinical decisions are made by the clinical advisory board, chaired by a practicing dentist (Dr. Ellingson). No non-dentist in the organization sets clinical protocols. Staff retention is monitored at 85% network-wide -- an early warning system for cultural disruption that we publish externally. Partner equity is tied to the long-term health of the platform, not short-term production. And the education platform (Pikos Institute, 4Front Symposium) is designed to make dentists better at their craft, not just more productive at their throughput.

**Q: The ADA has historically been skeptical of DSOs. What is the ADA's stance on SGA?**

A: The ADA president addressed a Gen4 dental event -- the first time an ADA president had addressed a DSO event. His message: "The ADA has your back. You are the living embodiment of the ADA's vision to achieve optimum dental health for all." The ADA's position has evolved as it has seen DSO models that preserve clinician autonomy. We believe the endorsement is specific to models that keep dentists in charge of clinical care -- which is what SGA is designed to do.

---

**Category 4: The Deal Structure Question**

**Q: What does a partnership with SGA actually look like financially?**

A: The SGA model is a joint-venture equity partnership. The dentist receives a liquidity event at close -- a real cash event based on practice valuation -- while retaining an ownership stake in the combined SGA platform. This is structurally different from: outright acquisition (Heartland, DCA -- cash only, no ongoing equity), franchise-like models (Aspen -- you operate under their brand), management services (PDS -- they charge you fees, you own your practice independently), or partial equity deals (MB2 -- similar concept, but without Thurston's track record transparency). Specific deal terms depend on practice size, profitability, and geography -- we do not disclose multiples publicly, and we do not think any DSO should, because the number matters less than the structure.

**Q: Why should a dentist take an equity stake in SGA rather than simply selling and investing the proceeds?**

A: The equity stake is the mechanism by which a dentist benefits from the compounding value of the platform -- not just their practice, but all practices in the combined SGA network. If the platform grows (and Thurston has grown platforms to meaningful scale across eight tries), the equity appreciates. The alternative -- sell the practice outright and invest the cash -- gives the dentist the capital but removes them from the growth trajectory of the platform they helped build. The choice depends on the individual dentist's financial situation and timeline. We tell every prospective partner to talk to a financial advisor before signing anything.

---

**Category 5: The Patient Impact Question**

**Q: How does DSO ownership affect patient care quality?**

A: SGA's model is specifically designed so that patients notice nothing negative and often notice improvements. Practices keep their name, their team, and their clinical leadership. The back-office changes are invisible to patients: billing is handled more efficiently, recruiting is supported so the team stays intact, technology investments often improve the clinical experience. The patient-facing change that partners most often report is that their doctors are more present -- less stressed about operations, more focused on the appointment in front of them. Dr. Godat's words: "Our partnership allows us to focus on changing lives." That is a patient-care outcome, not just a business result.

---

**Category 6: The Competitive Differentiation Question**

**Q: How is SGA different from MB2 Dental, which also positions as a "DPO" with partner equity?**

A: MB2 has built something real -- 800+ practices is a meaningful scale. The core difference is in three areas: First, the PE track record. Thurston has done this eight times across dental and healthcare, returned $4B+, and has been doing it for 38 years. MB2's financial backers are not publicly disclosed. For a dentist evaluating PE risk, that transparency matters. Second, the education platform. MB2 has no CE infrastructure. SGA has the Pikos Institute, the 4Front Symposium, and the Individual Growth Engine -- a clinical development ecosystem with no analog in the market. Third, the three-vertical architecture. MB2 is a general dentistry platform. SGA has community dentistry, collaborative general and specialty practice, and a dedicated implantology vertical. A periodontist, a general dentist, and a pediatric practice all have a specifically designed home inside SGA.

**Q: What gives SGA a competitive advantage over Guardian Dentistry Partners in the Southeast?**

A: Guardian has built something in the Southeast -- 160+ practices, strong messaging, Inc. 5000 recognition. Our advantage is scale, capital, and depth. SGA has a $350M credit facility. Guardian is family-office backed with limited growth capital. SGA has the Pikos Institute and 4Front Symposium as clinical development infrastructure. Guardian has no education platform. And SGA's three-vertical architecture means a Southeast practice joins a platform with national reach, specialty capabilities, and implantology infrastructure -- not just a regional network.

---

**Category 7: The Brand Consolidation Question**

**Q: What happens to the Gen4 Dental Partners and MODIS Dental Partners brands?**

A: SGA Dental Partners is the parent platform. Gen4 and MODIS continue to operate as the distinct verticals within the platform. For partner practices, nothing visible changes at the practice level -- local brand names are preserved. The consolidation is a backend organizational structure: unified leadership, shared operational infrastructure, cross-referral capabilities. Externally, the Gen4 collaborative model and MODIS implantology vertical retain their distinct positioning while benefiting from the combined platform's scale and resources.

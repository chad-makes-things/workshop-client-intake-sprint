# Partnership Readiness Scorecard -- Follow-Up Email Sequences

**Asset type:** 12-email micro-sequence library (3 emails per result tier)
**Trigger:** Scorecard completion and email capture
**Companion documents:** `partnership_readiness_scorecard.md`, `results_categories.md`
**Sender:** SGA Dental Partners <partners@sgadental.com>
**Reply-to:** partners@sgadental.com (monitored inbox)

---

## Sequence Architecture

Each tier receives a 3-email micro-sequence over 7 days:

| Email | Timing | Purpose |
|-------|--------|---------|
| Email 1 | Day 0 (immediate) | Results delivery + personalized insights |
| Email 2 | Day 3 | Deep dive on weakest category + how SGA addresses it |
| Email 3 | Day 7 | Social proof story matching their tier + CTA |

After the 3-email micro-sequence, prospects are routed based on tier:
- **Foundation Builder:** Monthly educational nurture
- **Growth Ready:** PA-Indoctrination sequence (5 emails)
- **Partnership Primed:** PA-Engagement sequence starting at GAIN email
- **Strategic Acquirer:** Manual BD/executive follow-up (no further automation)

**Preview text:** A focused breakdown of your weakest category with specific actions you can take this month.

**Body:**

[First name],

Three days ago your scorecard revealed that **[weakest category]** is your lowest-scoring area at [X]/15. Let us talk about why that matters more than any other category -- and what you can do about it.

**[IF WEAKEST = Operational Independence]**

Your Operational Independence score tells us that your practice depends heavily on you. You are likely the primary producer, the final decision-maker, and the person everyone turns to when something breaks. That is not unusual -- most practices are built on the owner's shoulders. But it creates a problem you feel every day: exhaustion.

One practice owner described it this way: "I'm the dentist, the HR department, the IT guy, and the therapist for my whole staff. I didn't sign up for any of that." If that resonates, your operational independence score is reflecting a reality that is not sustainable for another 10-15 years.

Here is what high-operational-independence practices do differently:

**They document before they delegate.** You cannot hand off what is not written down. Start with three workflows: patient intake, appointment scheduling, and accounts receivable follow-up. Spend one hour per workflow creating a step-by-step guide. This alone reduces your personal bottleneck by 20-30%.

**They hire for the role, not the task.** If your office manager is doing scheduling, billing, HR, and ordering supplies, you do not have an office manager -- you have a second owner without the equity. Consider whether splitting that role into two positions (patient coordinator + operations lead) would create more capacity than one overwhelmed person.

**They build decision frameworks, not decision trees.** Instead of approving every purchase over $200, create a monthly budget per category and empower your team to spend within it. Instead of reviewing every schedule change, define scheduling rules (e.g., no same-day cancellations within 48 hours) and let staff enforce them. Your job shifts from making decisions to designing the system that makes decisions.

The practices that are most valuable -- whether they pursue partnership or stay independent -- are the ones that run on systems, not on one person's stamina.

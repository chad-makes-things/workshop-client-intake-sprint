# The Dental Partnership Readiness Scorecard

**Asset type:** Interactive diagnostic scorecard (Daniel Priestley methodology)
**Funnel position:** Top-of-funnel lead capture and segmentation for Practice Acquisition (PA) and Specialist Partnership (SP) funnels
**Target personas:** Dr. Mark Hendricks (practice owner), Dr. James Whitfield (specialist)
**Estimated completion time:** 3-4 minutes
**Total questions:** 15 (original) / 8-10 (optimized -- see CRO Audit recommendation below)
**Score range:** 15-75 (original) / 8-40 or 10-50 (optimized)

**[CRO AUDIT RECOMMENDATION -- CRITICAL: Reduce from 15 to 8-10 questions]**
> The scorecard is strategically brilliant but operationally bloated. Each question presents 5 paragraph-length answer options. On mobile, a single question fills 2-3 screens. With the email gate at Q15, completion rates will likely fall 15-25% instead of the targeted 35%. Recommended optimized set: Q1, Q3, Q4, Q6, Q8, Q10, Q13, Q15 (8 questions). Cut Q2 (EBITDA -- too technical for top-of-funnel), Q5 (redundant with Q4), Q7 (weak discriminator), Q9 (redundant with Q8), Q11 (nice-to-have), Q14 (too sensitive for quiz). Shorten answer options to single sentences. Score range adjusted to 8-40, tier thresholds recalibrated proportionally.
>
> **Progressive email capture:** Capture email after Q3 (completing Category 1) with partial result: "Your Practice Performance Score: X/15. Enter your email to continue and see your full results." This captures email at 20% completion vs. 100%, dramatically increasing lead capture.
>
> **Progress bar:** Add persistent progress bar from Q1 onward showing category labels: "Category 1 of 5: Practice Performance -- Question 1 of 3."

## Strategic Intent

This scorecard is a DIFFERENT asset from the Practice Partnership Assessment (the gated PDF lead magnet described in the funnel architecture). That assessment is a self-evaluation PDF delivered post-capture. This scorecard is an interactive online tool that serves three purposes simultaneously:

1. **Diagnostic value:** Gives the prospect a genuine, quantified understanding of where their practice stands across five critical dimensions -- regardless of whether they ever contact SGA.
2. **Segmentation engine:** Routes prospects into four result tiers, each with distinct follow-up sequences, CTAs, and BD team routing logic.
3. **Conversation starter:** Provides the business development team with a data-rich profile (category scores, weakest areas, self-reported answers) that replaces cold outreach with informed dialogue.

The scorecard positions SGA as the Sage archetype -- the wise evaluator who helps you understand your situation before prescribing a solution. It does NOT sell. It diagnoses.

Each question is scored 1-5. Total score = sum of all 15 answers. No weighting -- all categories contribute equally. This prevents gaming and ensures the scorecard surfaces genuine gaps rather than rewarding strength concentration.

Partnership Primed and Strategic Acquirer tiers are genuinely difficult to reach without strong systems, diversified operations, and clear personal vision.

## Category 1: Practice Performance

*This category measures the financial health and trajectory of your practice. Strong practices have growing revenue, healthy margins, and loyal patient bases.*

### Q1: Revenue Growth Trajectory

**"Over the past three years, how would you describe your practice's revenue trend?"**

| Answer | Score | Criteria |
|--------|-------|----------|
| Revenue has been declining or flat for three or more years | 1 | Negative or zero growth signals structural issues -- market saturation, patient attrition, or failure to expand services. The practice may be contracting. |
| Revenue fluctuates year to year with no clear trend | 2 | Inconsistency suggests dependence on external factors (insurance changes, staff turnover, seasonal variation) rather than deliberate growth strategy. |
| Revenue has grown modestly (1-5% annually) | 3 | Keeping pace with inflation and organic growth. Solid but not accelerating. Typical of mature solo practices that have hit a natural ceiling. |
| Revenue has grown steadily (6-12% annually) | 4 | Above-market growth indicates active investment in services, marketing, or capacity. The practice is winning share or expanding scope. |
| Revenue has grown aggressively (13%+ annually) through new services, locations, or capacity expansion | 5 | Exceptional growth. Usually requires multi-location expansion, new specialty services, or significant patient acquisition investment. Rare for solo operators. |

**VoC framing note:** This question mirrors the financial anxiety Dr. Hendricks feels -- "I've got all my eggs in one basket." It quantifies the trajectory without asking for specific dollar figures (which prospects resist sharing at this stage).

### Q2: Profitability and Margin Health

**"How confident are you in your practice's EBITDA margins (profit after all operating expenses, before interest, taxes, depreciation, and amortization)?"**

| Answer | Score | Criteria |
|--------|-------|----------|
| I am not sure what my EBITDA is or have never calculated it | 1 | Financial blindness is a significant risk factor. The owner is running the practice on intuition rather than metrics. Common among clinician-owners who avoid the business side. |
| My margins are thin (below 15%) and I worry about profitability regularly | 2 | Sub-15% EBITDA suggests operational inefficiency, overhead bloat, or under-collection. The practice is vulnerable to any cost shock (staff turnover, equipment failure, insurance renegotiation). |
| My margins are acceptable (15-25%) but I know there is room for improvement | 3 | Healthy but not optimized. This is the typical range for well-run solo practices. Improvement usually requires scale efficiencies the owner cannot achieve alone. |
| My margins are strong (25-35%) and I track them quarterly | 4 | Above-average profitability with financial discipline. This practice is attractive to any acquirer and the owner knows their numbers. |
| My margins exceed 35% and I have a clear understanding of every line item driving profitability | 5 | Elite financial performance. Indicates a sophisticated operator with strong systems, favorable payer mix, and tight cost control. Rare in solo practice. |

**VoC framing note:** The parenthetical definition of EBITDA is deliberate -- many practice owners have heard the term from broker calls but do not fully understand it. Providing the definition without condescension matches the Sage archetype.

## Category 2: Operational Independence

*This category measures how well your practice runs without you in the room. The most valuable practices are systems-driven, not owner-driven.*

### Q4: Owner Dependency

**"If you took a two-week vacation tomorrow with no phone or email access, what would happen to your practice?"**

| Answer | Score | Criteria |
|--------|-------|----------|
| The practice would essentially stop. No one else can do what I do, clinically or operationally. | 1 | Total owner dependency. The practice IS the owner. This is the highest-risk scenario for any transition -- and the most common reason dentists feel trapped. |
| Major problems would surface within days. Staff would try, but critical decisions would stall. | 2 | Partial dependency. The team can handle routine work but anything non-standard (staff conflict, patient complaint, financial decision, equipment failure) requires the owner. |
| Most things would run, but a few key functions (scheduling, financial approvals, complex treatment decisions) depend on me. | 3 | Moderate dependency. The practice has some systems but the owner is still a bottleneck for specific high-value functions. Common in practices with strong office managers but weak delegation systems. |
| The practice would run smoothly for two weeks. My team handles day-to-day operations independently. | 4 | Low dependency. The owner has successfully built a team and systems that operate without constant oversight. This practice is attractive to partners and acquirers. |
| My team runs the practice better than I do. I could take a month off and production would not miss a beat. | 5 | True operational independence. Rare. This owner has invested in leadership development, documented systems, and empowered decision-making at every level. |

**VoC framing note:** The "two-week vacation" framing is deliberate. It is concrete, non-threatening, and immediately reveals the truth. Every practice owner who reads this question already knows their answer -- and most are uncomfortable with it. This mirrors the exhaustion theme: "I'm the dentist, the HR department, the IT guy, and the therapist for my whole staff."

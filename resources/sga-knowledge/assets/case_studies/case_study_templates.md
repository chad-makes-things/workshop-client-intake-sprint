# SGA Dental Partners -- Case Study Templates

**Prepared:** March 2026
**Purpose:** Three production-ready formats for capturing and deploying partner and patient proof stories
**Audiences:** Practice owners, associate dentists, patients (implant vertical), specialists, office managers
**Tone standard:** Evidence first. Let results speak. No corporate puffery.

---

## Overview: Choosing the Right Format

| Format | Length | Best Use | Deployment Channels |
|--------|--------|----------|---------------------|
| Long-form | 1,500-2,000 words | Website case study library, PDF for sales handoffs, partnership deck appendix | sgadental.com, email sequences, direct partner outreach |
| One-page | 400-500 words | Printed handout for in-person sales meetings, email attachment, partnership deck insert | Sales meetings, email, direct mail |
| Video testimonial script | 3-5 min on camera | Website homepage, landing pages, YouTube, social media | All digital channels, paid social, YouTube pre-roll |

Produce all three formats from a single interview. The interview questions bank (see `interview_question_bank.md`) generates enough raw material for all three.

## Format 1: Long-Form Case Study (1,500-2,000 Words)

### Purpose

The long-form case study is the evidentiary anchor. It exists for the prospect who has gotten past "what is SGA?" and is now asking "does this actually work for people like me?" It needs to answer that question so completely that reading it feels like a conversation with a trusted peer who already made the decision.

### Structure and Writing Prompts

---

**HEADLINE**

Formula: [Result] + [Who] + [Timeframe]

Examples:
- "How Periodontal Associates of Memphis Grew Revenue 16.5% and Got Its Doctor Back to Dentistry"
- "From Solo Periodontist to Full-Arch Platform: One Specialist's First Year with MODIS"
- "What Happened After [Practice Name] Stopped Running QuickBooks and Started Running a Dental Practice"

Writing note: The headline is a promise. Make it specific. Vague headlines ("A Better Partnership Experience") are skipped. Specific headlines ("Revenue Up 16.5%, Staff Turnover Down to Zero") are read.

---

**SNAPSHOT BOX** (placed at the top of the article, pulled out visually)

Format:
- Partner: [Name, credentials, practice name, city/state]
- Practice type: [General / Periodontal / Implant / Pediatric / etc.]
- Years in practice: [#]
- Challenge: [One sentence]
- Solution: [One sentence]
- Key result: [Single most compelling number]
- Partnership timeline: [Date joined SGA/Gen4/MODIS]

Writing note: The snapshot is for the skimmer. If they read nothing else, this box tells the essential story. Make every field specific -- "Revenue up 16.5% in 12 months" not "significant revenue growth."

---

**SECTION 1: BEFORE -- The Situation (300-400 words)**

Prompt questions for the writer:
- Where was this person 12-24 months before partnering with SGA? What did their day look like?
- What was the specific problem they were carrying? Be precise -- not "administrative burden" but "spending Sunday nights reconciling payroll because my billing coordinator quit."
- What had they already tried to fix the problem? Why did it fall short?
- What was the cost of staying in that situation -- financial, emotional, professional?
- Was there a specific moment or event that made them think "something has to change"?

Writing guidance: This section earns the right to present the solution. The reader needs to recognize themselves in the "before" state -- not a sanitized, corporate-friendly version of struggle, but the real thing. Use the partner's own words wherever possible. First person is powerful.

Signal phrases to pull from if the interview surface them:
- "QuickBooks no longer calls me to the back office" -- the relief Dr. Hallissey described
- "I was in a spot of complacency, not being challenged" -- Dr. Cavendish's admission
- "There's no downside to having a mentor" -- hint at what was missing

Do NOT write: "Like many dental practice owners, [Name] was feeling the pressure of running a modern practice." That is wallpaper. Write: "At 7 PM on a Tuesday in March 2024, [Name] was at his desk reviewing insurance denials instead of at his daughter's soccer game. Again."

---

**SECTION 2: THE DECISION -- Why SGA (250-350 words)**

Prompt questions for the writer:
- What had the partner heard or thought about DSOs before this conversation?
- What specifically made them take the first call seriously?
- Who referred them, or how did they find SGA?
- What did they expect going in, and what surprised them about the process?
- What came close to stopping them from moving forward?
- What ultimately convinced them?

Writing guidance: This section handles objections before the reader can raise them. If the partner was skeptical of corporate DSOs, say so. If they were worried about losing autonomy, staff, or their name -- say they were worried, then show what they discovered. Skepticism voiced and resolved is more powerful than skepticism ignored.

Key tension to surface: Most practice owners have heard the horror stories. So did this one. Name the fear. Then show how it was resolved.

Do NOT write: "After a thorough evaluation process, [Name] chose SGA Dental Partners as the right fit." That tells us nothing. Write: "He had taken two other DSO calls before SGA. One wanted to change his brand immediately. The other wouldn't let him choose his own lab. SGA was the first conversation where they asked what he wanted his practice to look like in five years."

---

**SECTION 3: THE PARTNERSHIP JOURNEY -- First 90 Days to Year One (400-500 words)**

Prompt questions for the writer:
- What was the onboarding experience like? What did SGA do in the first 30 days?
- What was the first tangible thing that changed? (The iSolved payroll system, the first recruiting support, the marketing team building a new website)
- Was there a moment of "this is actually different from what I expected"?
- Did they encounter any friction or challenges during implementation? How were they resolved?
- For Gen4 partners: when was the first peer interaction that felt meaningful? When did they meet their mentor?
- For MODIS partners: when did they first access the Pikos Institute, or connect with other MODIS partners?
- For practice managers: what was the first administrative task that disappeared from their plate?

Writing guidance: This section makes the abstract concrete. "Operational support" means nothing. "The billing team caught $47,000 in denied claims in the first 90 days" means everything. This is where specific systems, specific people, and specific moments live.

Reference where authentic: Spencer Pierce's (VP of People & Culture, Gen4) description of onboarding: "After onboarding is completed, there's an abundance of relief. It's like 'Okay, I understand what this means for me now.'"

---

**SECTION 4: THE RESULTS -- What Changed (300-400 words)**

Prompt questions for the writer:
- What specific numbers can the partner share? (Revenue change, EBITA change, staff turnover rate, new patient volume, production per visit, overhead percentage)
- What changed that does not show up on a spreadsheet? (Leaving the office earlier, mentoring a younger doctor, enjoying complex cases again, attending family events)
- Were there any unexpected benefits they did not anticipate when they signed?
- How does their practice compare to where it was 12 or 24 months ago?
- What would they tell a peer who is considering SGA but is still on the fence?

Writing guidance: Quantitative results come first. Qualitative results close. The format: number, then the human meaning behind the number.

Reference where authentic -- Dr. Matt Cavendish's actual results from the Gen4 case study:
- Revenue TTM up 16.5%
- EBITA beating plan by 2.3 points and beating prior year by $45,266
- Total payroll expenses down
- Staff retention at 100%
- Cost of dental supplies beating plan by 6.4 points

Then Cavendish in his own words: "When you've been in dentistry for 20 years like I have, sometimes it's not as fun as it used to be. So to be able to do a complex case like this, with somebody who is also working at a very high skill level -- it is fun."

That last sentence is worth as much as every number above it.

---

**SECTION 5: THE QUOTE -- Shareable Sound Bite**

Pull one 2-4 sentence quote that captures the entire arc: where they were, what changed, and how they feel now. This quote will appear in ads, email, social, and sales decks. It needs to be:

- Specific (names a real outcome or moment)
- Emotional (conveys feeling, not just fact)
- In their natural voice (no corporate language)
- Attributed fully (first and last name, practice name, city)

Template for eliciting this quote (from interview question bank): "If you were having coffee with a dentist who is exactly where you were 18 months ago -- same situation, same fears -- what would you say to them?"

---

**CLOSING + CTA (100-150 words)**

Short paragraph transitioning the reader from the story to the next step. Do not hard sell. The story should make the sale; the CTA should open the door.

Template:
"[Practice name] is still [practice name]. [Name]'s patients still see his/her face when they walk in. His/her staff is still there. What changed: [one-sentence summary of what SGA made possible].

If you are a practice owner thinking about what your next chapter looks like, the best first step is a direct conversation -- not with a broker, not with a sales team, but with a partner dentist who has already made the decision. [Name] made himself available for those calls. So have we.

[CTA button: Schedule a Confidential Conversation]"

## Format 2: One-Page Case Study (400-500 Words)

### Purpose

The one-page is a leave-behind. It is read in 90 seconds at a conference table, glanced at in an email, or handed to a spouse who wants to understand what their partner is considering. It has to land the essential story without losing the specificity that makes it credible.

### Structure and Writing Prompts

---

**HEADER**

Practice name | Partner name, credentials | City, state | Specialty/practice type

---

**CHALLENGE (75-100 words)**

One focused paragraph. Lead with the specific pain, not the category of pain.

Writing prompt: What was the thing this partner most needed to be true about their practice situation that was NOT true before joining SGA? Describe that gap as directly as possible.

Example: "[Name] had spent [X] years building [practice name] into a [adjective] practice in [city]. But by [year], he was spending more time managing his staff and chasing insurance claims than treating patients. His billing team was inconsistent. Recruiting had stalled. And the growing stack of compliance requirements was sitting at the back of his desk, unread. He needed to find a way to protect what he had built without running it alone."

---

**WHY SGA (75-100 words)**

What was the specific thing that convinced them?

Writing prompt: This section must answer one question -- "Why SGA and not another DSO or no DSO at all?" Surface the decision detail that shows differentiation.

Example: "After two disappointing DSO conversations -- one wanted to rebrand his practice immediately, another couldn't tell him who would handle his billing -- [Name] reached SGA through a colleague's referral. What was different: the first conversation was with a practicing dentist, not a sales rep. 'They asked what I wanted my practice to look like in five years,' [Name] said. 'Nobody had asked me that before.'"

---

**RESULTS (100-150 words)**

Bulleted metrics first. One to two sentences of qualitative impact.

Format:
- Revenue: [X% increase / TTM change]
- EBITA: [Beating prior year by $X]
- Staff retention: [X%]
- Overhead / supply costs: [Change]
- [Other specific metric relevant to this partner]

Then the human number: one to two sentences about what the metrics made possible in their daily life.

---

**IN THEIR WORDS (50-75 words)**

Pull quote. Must be attributed with full name, practice name, city.

---

**FOOTER**

"[Practice name] is still [practice name]. The name on the door has not changed. The team has not changed. The patients have not changed. What changed: [Name] is not carrying the business alone anymore.

To learn what a partnership conversation looks like: [URL or contact]"

## Format 3: Video Testimonial Script

### Purpose

Video does what written case studies cannot -- it puts a real face, real voice, and real emotion in front of the skeptical prospect. The highest-converting proof format in B2B sales and patient acquisition. Aim for 3-5 minutes of final edited content.

### Pre-Interview Setup

**Technical requirements:**
- Location: The partner's own practice (authentic; the surroundings tell the story)
- Camera: Eye-level, natural light or professional softbox, clean background (treatment room, hallway, welcome area)
- Audio: Lapel mic required; eliminate room echo
- B-roll list: see below
- Interviewer: Off-camera. Friendly, unhurried. Remind them this is a conversation, not a performance.

**Briefing the partner before filming:**
"We are going to have a conversation about your experience. We are not looking for polished answers -- we are looking for honest ones. If you stumble, we will cut it. If you say something we cannot use, we will not use it. The only thing that matters is that it sounds like you."

---

### Interview Script (Questions in Order)

These questions are designed to create a natural narrative arc. The partner should tell the story, not answer a questionnaire.

**Warm-up (not filmed, used to get them comfortable):**
- "How long have you been in practice?"
- "Tell me about this practice -- what makes it yours?"

**On camera:**

1. "Before you joined SGA -- what was a typical week like for you?" [Let them describe it fully. Do not interrupt. This is your "before" footage.]

2. "What was the thing that was bothering you most? The thing that kept you up at night or made you dread Monday mornings?"

3. "Had you looked at other DSOs before this? What happened with those conversations?"

4. "What made you take the first call with SGA seriously?"

5. "What surprised you about that first conversation?"

6. "Walk me through the first 90 days after you joined. What actually changed?"

7. "What is different now -- and I mean specifically, not generally. Give me a number or a moment."

8. "What do you spend your time on now that you used to spend time on before -- or what don't you spend time on anymore?"

9. "Is there a moment in the last [timeframe] that captures what this partnership has meant to you? One specific story or day?"

10. "If you were having coffee with a dentist who is where you were 18 months ago -- same situation, same fears -- what would you say to them?"

---

### Shot List for Video Editor

**On-camera interview footage:**
- Wide shot of partner in practice (establishes context)
- Medium shot (chest to head, this is your primary talking-head angle)
- Close-up (face only, for emotional moments and key quotes)

**B-roll footage to capture during the visit:**

For practice owner / specialist stories:
- Partner arriving at the office, greeting staff
- Partner with a patient (with patient consent -- blurred face or no face if needed)
- Partner at the treatment chair, working
- Front desk team interacting with patients
- Technology in use (CBCT scan, CEREC, guided implant system)
- Partner reviewing a case on the computer (looking focused, in control)
- Partner laughing with a staff member
- Exterior of the practice building with signage (the name on the door -- this is important for "your name stays on the door")

For patient stories:
- Patient in the consultation chair, looking at camera (with signed consent)
- Before photos (if available and consented)
- After smile (patient smiling, full face -- the emotional payoff)
- Patient doing the thing they could not do before (eating, laughing with family, singing -- whatever their "before" pain was)
- The treatment space itself (clean, calm, professional)
- The team interaction (warmth, not clinical)

For associate dentist stories:
- Associate with a mentor, examining an X-ray or CBCT together (the mentorship visual)
- Associate performing a procedure (clinical skill, complexity)
- Associate in a learning environment (4Front Symposium footage if available)
- Associate in conversation with staff (leadership, not just technical)

---

### Post-Production Guidance

**Editing structure:**
1. Open with the most emotionally resonant moment from the interview (not necessarily the first question answered -- find the best line and open on it)
2. Establish context: practice, partner, duration
3. Before: the challenge in their words
4. The decision: what made SGA different
5. The journey: specific changes, one or two moments
6. Results: the number + the human meaning
7. Close: the "coffee with a colleague" answer
8. Sign-off: partner's name, practice name, city. Simple lower-third text.

**Subtitle requirement:** All video content must have accurate subtitles. Most views on social media are silent.

**Cut-downs to produce from every full interview:**
- 30-second version (key quote + result, for paid social)
- 60-second version (challenge + result, for YouTube pre-roll)
- Full 3-5 minute version (for website case study pages and email sequences)

## Quality Checklist: Before Publishing Any Case Study

**Specificity check:**
- [ ] Headline includes a specific result (not "better outcomes" but "revenue up 16.5%")
- [ ] The "before" section describes a specific moment or situation, not a category of pain
- [ ] Results section includes at least two quantified metrics
- [ ] Partner is named with full name, practice name, city, and credentials
- [ ] The quote does not sound like marketing -- it sounds like a human being

**Authenticity check:**
- [ ] No corporate language ("world-class," "premier," "holistic approach")
- [ ] Partner's natural speech patterns are preserved in quotes
- [ ] Objections are acknowledged, not ignored
- [ ] The "why SGA and not someone else" question is answered specifically

**Proof hierarchy check:**
- [ ] This case study is deployed at the appropriate funnel stage (long-form for MOFU/BOFU, video for BOFU/homepage)
- [ ] The case study matches the avatar it is meant to persuade (owner story for owners, patient story for patients)
- [ ] The proof is strong enough to address the #1 objection for that avatar

**Legal and permissions check:**
- [ ] Partner has approved quotes in writing
- [ ] Specific financial figures (revenue, EBITA) are confirmed with the partner before publishing
- [ ] Patient video testimonials have signed release forms on file
- [ ] Practice name, credentials, and location have been verified

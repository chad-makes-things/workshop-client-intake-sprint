# SGA Dental Partners -- Interview Question Bank

**Prepared:** March 2026
**Purpose:** Comprehensive question library for case study interviews, testimonial collection, and proof asset production
**Users:** Partnership team, marketing team, any SGA representative conducting a partner or patient interview
**Structure:** 30+ questions organized by narrative section, with avatar-specific variants and interviewer guidance

---

## Before You Start: Interviewer Principles

The quality of the answers depends almost entirely on how the questions are asked. A bad interviewer gets a polished, forgettable answer. A good interviewer gets the true story.

**Principle 1: Ask and then shut up.**
After asking a question, stop talking. Let the silence do the work. Most interviewees will fill silence by going deeper, adding the specific detail they were about to skip, or saying the most honest thing in the conversation. Your job after asking is to listen.

**Principle 2: Follow the specifics.**
When a partner says "things got a lot better," the follow-up is always: "What specifically got better? Can you give me an example?" The general answer is wallpaper. The specific answer is the case study.

**Principle 3: Acknowledge, then go deeper.**
When someone shares something emotionally significant, acknowledge it before moving on. "That sounds like it was a genuinely difficult period. How long had things been like that before you took the first call with SGA?" Acknowledgment earns the deeper story.

**Principle 4: Be comfortable with discomfort.**
The most valuable answers come from uncomfortable questions. "What almost stopped you from doing this?" is more useful than "What made you excited?" Do not flinch.

**Principle 5: Let them tell the story in their order.**
The framework below is a guide, not a script. If the interviewee wants to start with the results and work backward, let them. Guide gently to cover the essential sections, but do not interrupt a good story because it is in the wrong order.

**Principle 6: The best quote is the one they almost did not say.**
When an interviewee says "I probably shouldn't say this, but..." or trails off with "I guess what I'm really trying to say is..." -- that is your most deployable quote. Invite them to finish that thought.

**Principle 7: Always ask for the "coffee with a colleague" answer.**
Every interview should end with some version of: "If you were having coffee with someone who is where you were 12 months ago, what would you say?" This question reliably produces the quotable, deployable sound bite that every other question has been building toward.

## Section 1: Before SGA -- Context, Pain, and the Tipping Point

These questions establish the "before state" that makes the transformation meaningful. Skip these and the case study has no foundation.

### Universal Questions (All Audiences)

**Q1. Set the scene for me.**
"Before you made this decision, what did a typical week look like for you? Walk me through it." [Let them describe their daily reality in detail. Do not interrupt.]

**Q2. What was the thing that was wearing on you most?**
"If you had to name the one thing -- the thing that was draining your energy or keeping you up at night -- what was it?" [Pause. Wait for the specific answer.]

**Q3. What had you tried before?**
"Before you connected with SGA, had you tried to fix this problem in other ways? What did you try, and why did it fall short?"

**Q4. The cost of not changing.**
"If you had kept doing what you were doing -- if nothing had changed -- where would things be now? What would your practice / life / career look like in three to five years?"

**Q5. The tipping point.**
"Was there a specific moment or event that made you think 'something has to change'? What was it?" [This question produces the "buying trigger" detail. Let them tell the full story.]

---

### Practice Owner Variants (Dr. Hendricks archetype)

**Q1-A. The business vs. the dentistry.**
"You went to dental school to be a dentist. How much of your time were you actually spending on dentistry versus running the business side when things were at their most difficult?"

**Q2-A. The financial picture.**
"A lot of practice owners carry all their financial security in one practice. Is that something you thought about? What did your financial situation look like going into this?"

**Q3-A. What you were afraid of losing.**
"Your practice has your name on it -- your reputation, your relationships. What were you most afraid would change if you brought in a partner?"

**Q4-A. Other DSO experiences or conversations.**
"Had you spoken with any other DSOs before SGA? What happened with those conversations? What did they do that made you skeptical?" [This surfaces the competitive comparison data and the objections SGA needs to counter.]

**Q5-A. The staff and patient concern.**
"One of the things practice owners worry about is how their patients and staff will react to a partnership. What were you expecting would happen with them?"

---

### Associate Dentist Variants (Dr. Nair archetype)

**Q1-B. Describe your previous position.**
"Before SGA, where were you working? What was the daily reality of that position? Not the job description -- the actual day."

**Q2-B. Clinical frustration.**
"Were there types of cases or procedures you wanted to do that you were not getting to do? What was being held back?"

**Q3-B. The debt and the career math.**
"Student loans shape a lot of early-career decisions. Was that part of how you were thinking about your options? What did the math look like to you?"

**Q4-B. The mentorship gap.**
"When you had a clinical question or a difficult case, who did you go to? Was there someone to ask?"

**Q5-B. What 'career' meant to you then.**
"At the point you were considering leaving your previous position, what would the ideal career have looked like? What were you actually hoping for that you were not finding?"

---

### Patient Variants (Linda Dawson archetype)

**Q1-C. The daily experience.**
"Before your procedure, what was a normal day like in terms of your teeth? Not the dental terms -- just what it actually felt like to live with the situation."

**Q2-C. The thing you stopped doing.**
"Were there things you stopped doing -- activities, foods, social situations -- because of your dental situation? Can you tell me about one specific time?"

**Q3-C. The research journey.**
"How long had you been researching options? What was that process like? What confused you or frustrated you about it?"

**Q4-C. The fear.**
"What scared you most about the idea of the procedure? Was it the surgery? The cost? Something else?"

**Q5-C. Why you almost didn't do it.**
"Was there a moment where you almost talked yourself out of it? What almost stopped you?"

### Specialist Variants (Dr. Whitfield archetype)

**Q1-D. The infrastructure gap.**
"What was the ceiling on your practice -- the thing you wanted to do that you could not do with your current setup?"

**Q2-D. Recruiting reality.**
"Had you tried to bring in another specialist? What happened with that process?"

**Q3-D. The succession concern.**
"You've spent 14 years building this practice. What would have happened to it if you had been unable to practice tomorrow? Had you thought about that?"

**Q4-D. PE and DSO skepticism.**
"What did you know about DSOs and private equity before this conversation? What were you most worried about?"

**Q5-D. What the Pikos Institute meant to you.**
[For MODIS prospects specifically] "You've attended Pikos courses. What does the Pikos Institute represent to you as a clinician? And how did that factor into how you thought about MODIS?"

### Office Manager / Staff Variants (Tamara Reeves archetype)

**Q1-E. What you heard first.**
"Do you remember the moment you found out your practice was joining SGA? What was your first reaction?"

**Q2-E. What you were afraid of.**
"What were you most worried about -- for yourself, for your team, for the patients?"

**Q3-E. The administrative reality.**
"Before the transition, what were the two or three things that took up most of your time and energy that had nothing to do with patients? Walk me through one of those."

**Q4-E. What you told your team.**
"Your team came to you with questions when they found out. What were they asking, and what did you tell them?"

**Q5-E. The first week.**
"What was the first week after the transition like? What changed, and what stayed the same?"

## Section 2: The Decision -- Alternatives Considered and What Convinced Them

These questions surface the competitive comparison data and the specific proof points that overcame objections. This is the most strategically valuable section for sales collateral.

### Universal Questions

**Q6. What alternatives did you consider?**
"When you were making this decision, what other options were on the table? Staying solo? Other DSOs? Other solutions entirely? How did you evaluate them?"

**Q7. What made you take the first call with SGA seriously?**
"A lot of people hear about DSO partnerships and don't even take the call. What made you decide to have this conversation?"

**Q8. What surprised you in the early conversations?**
"What was different about the SGA conversation from what you were expecting? What caught you off guard -- in a good way or a bad way?"

**Q9. What almost stopped you?**
"Was there a point where you almost walked away? What was the concern? How was it resolved?" [This is the objection-handling gold mine. Do not skip it.]

**Q10. What ultimately convinced you?**
"When you finally decided to move forward, what was the thing that pushed you over the line? Was it a specific conversation, a piece of information, or something you felt?"

---

### Practice Owner Variants

**Q6-A. The autonomy question specifically.**
"The autonomy concern is the one that stops most dentists from ever having this conversation. How did you think about that? What were your specific concerns about clinical and operational autonomy, and how did those get resolved?"

**Q7-A. The equity model.**
"The JV equity model is different from what most people assume when they hear 'DSO.' What did you understand it to mean when you first heard about it, and how did your understanding change?"

**Q8-A. What you wish you had known earlier.**
"Is there anything you found out during the process that you wish you had known at the very beginning? Something that would have made the evaluation easier or faster?"

---

### Patient Variants

**Q6-C. The cost conversation.**
"Cost is the thing most people are most afraid to ask about. How did the cost conversation go? What helped you feel like it was manageable?"

**Q7-C. Choosing this practice over others.**
"Had you called other offices? What was different about this one?" [Often the answer reveals the content type or communication approach that reached them first -- Dr. Olson's YouTube, transparency about pricing, a referral.]

**Q8-C. What made you trust the team.**
"Trust is hard in healthcare, especially when you're talking about a significant procedure. What was the moment you decided to trust the team here?"

---

### Specialist Variants

**Q6-D. The PE fear directly.**
"Private equity has a reputation in healthcare that makes a lot of specialists nervous. How did you think through the PE question? What evidence or information changed your mind, if it did?"

**Q7-D. The clinical autonomy for specialists.**
"Full-scope periodontics is not just implants. Was there any concern that MODIS would push you toward the high-margin cases and away from the work that keeps your practice whole?"

## Section 3: The Transition -- First 90 Days, Surprises, Support Experience

These questions produce the specific operational details that make "support" mean something concrete rather than remaining an empty promise.

### Universal Questions

**Q11. Walk me through what happened first.**
"After the partnership was official, what was the first thing that changed? Not the first thing on paper -- the first thing you actually noticed in your daily life."

**Q12. The first 30 days.**
"What were the first 30 days like? What did SGA's team actually do? What did you have to do?"

**Q13. What went smoothly.**
"What was easier than you expected? What worked well from the start?"

**Q14. What was harder than expected.**
"What was harder or different from what you anticipated? Was there friction, a learning curve, a surprise?" [Do not let partners skip this. A case study with zero friction is not believable.]

**Q15. The first moment you thought "this is different."**
"Was there a specific moment in the first few months where you thought -- this is actually different from what I expected? What was it?" [This produces the "before/after moment" that makes video content compelling.]

---

### Practice Owner Variants

**Q11-A. The first operational change.**
"What was the first administrative or operational task that got taken off your plate? Walk me through it -- what changed, specifically?"

**Q12-A. The peer community experience.**
"When did you first connect with other SGA or Gen4 partner dentists? What was that experience like?" [If they attended 4Front, ask specifically about that.]

**Q13-A. The mentor relationship.**
[If Gen4 Individual Growth Engine is relevant] "Did you work with a mentor through the program? How did that relationship start? What did you learn from it that you could not have learned alone?"

**Q14-A. Staff response.**
"How did your staff respond to the transition? What were they most concerned about, and how did those concerns get resolved?"

**Q15-A. Patient response.**
"What did your patients notice, if anything? Did anyone ask questions or express concerns?"

## Section 4: Results -- Quantified Outcomes, Unexpected Benefits, What They Would Tell Peers

This section produces the deployable metrics and the quotable "after state" content. Push for specifics. Resist the temptation to accept "better" when "16.5% higher" is available.

### Universal Questions

**Q16. Give me a number.**
"What is the most important metric you track, and what has happened to it since the partnership? Revenue. Staff retention. Patient volume. Production per visit. Whatever you measure -- what does it say now vs. what it said a year ago?" [Get the exact figure. This is the most important question for long-form and one-page case studies.]

**Q17. What changed that does not show up on a spreadsheet.**
"Not the financial stuff -- what changed about your daily experience? What is different about how you feel when you go to work, or come home, or spend time with your family?"

**Q18. The unexpected benefit.**
"Was there something you got out of this that you did not expect? Something nobody told you would happen?"

**Q19. What you miss about before.**
"Is there anything about how things were before the partnership that you miss or wish you could have back?" [This question is uncomfortable for many interviewers but produces the most credible content because it acknowledges that nothing is perfect.]

**Q20. A specific story from the last six months.**
"Give me one specific story or moment from the last six months that captures what this partnership has meant to you. Not a general feeling -- an actual event." [Require specificity. "Everything is better" is not a story. "I coached my son's baseball game for the first time on a Tuesday because I left the office at 5:30" is a story.]

---

### Practice Owner Variants

**Q16-A. The financial results, in order.**
"Walk me through the financial picture. What changed in revenue? EBITA? Staff costs? Supply costs? What are the numbers?" [Frame these as a list to prompt comprehensive reporting.]

**Q17-A. What your family noticed.**
"Did your family notice a change? What did they observe that you might not have noticed yourself?"

**Q18-A. A clinical case you could not have done before.**
"Did you do a procedure or take on a case in the last year that you would not have attempted or been equipped for before the partnership? Tell me about it."

---

### Associate Variants

**Q16-B. Clinical growth measurement.**
"What can you do now that you could not do 12 months ago? Specifically -- what procedures, what complexity of cases?"

**Q17-B. Equity status.**
"Have you made the equity investment as a Class A shareholder? What does that feel like -- having a stake in something bigger than your practice?"

---

### Patient Variants

**Q16-C. The functional result.**
"What can you eat now that you could not eat before? Give me specific foods." [This question reliably produces the most emotionally resonant and shareable content. The steak moment. The apple. The corn on the cob.]

**Q17-C. The social change.**
"Before your procedure, were there things you were avoiding -- social situations, photos, events? What have you done in the last few months that you would not have done before?"

**Q18-C. The confidence measure.**
"On a scale of 1 to 10, how would you describe your confidence about your smile before the procedure? And now?" [If they give a number, ask: "What does that number change? What do you do differently at a 10 than you did at a [whatever they said before]?"]

**Q19-C. Your first meal.**
"A lot of patients remember their first meal after getting their new teeth. Do you remember yours? What did you choose, and what was it like?" [Do not skip this question. It is the most deployable piece of patient proof content available. The specificity of food names, taste descriptions, and who they ate with is what makes it real.]

### Specialist Variants

**Q16-D. Capital investment impact.**
"What did the capital investment -- the CT scanner, the lab, the surgical suite, whatever changed -- make possible clinically that was not possible before?"

**Q17-D. The recruiting outcome.**
"Were you able to bring on an associate? What changed about your ability to recruit?"

**Q18-D. The succession picture.**
"How has your financial and succession situation changed? What does the risk picture look like now compared to before?"

### Office Manager Variants

**Q16-E. Hours reclaimed.**
"Roughly how many hours a week were you spending on tasks that are now handled by SGA's team -- payroll, benefits, HR? What do those hours go to now?"

**Q17-E. Staff stability.**
"Has anyone left the practice since the transition? Has anyone specifically said they are staying because of something SGA brought in?"

## Section 5: The Quotable Moment -- Designed to Elicit Shareable Sound Bites

These questions are placed last because the best quotes come when the interviewee has warmed up, processed their story fully, and is speaking from a place of genuine reflection rather than first-impression answers.

### Universal Closing Questions

**Q21. The coffee with a colleague.**
"If you were having coffee with a [practice owner / new grad associate / implant patient / specialist] who is exactly where you were 12-18 months ago -- same situation, same fears -- what would you say to them?" [This is the single most important question in the bank. Ask it every time. Never skip it.]

**Q22. What you wish someone had told you.**
"Is there anything you wish someone had told you before you made this decision? Something that would have made it easier to say yes, or helped you understand what you were getting into?"

**Q23. What you would say to skeptics.**
"A lot of people in your position are skeptical of this kind of partnership. They've heard horror stories. They're afraid to make a move they'll regret. What would you say to that person?" [This produces direct objection-handling content, voiced by a peer rather than by SGA.]

**Q24. The thing that nobody talks about.**
"What is the thing about this partnership that people don't talk about -- the thing that is hard to convey in a brochure or a website, the thing you would not have believed until you lived it?"

**Q25. One word or phrase.**
"If you had to describe what this partnership has meant to you in one word or phrase -- not for marketing purposes, just honestly -- what would it be?" [Short, punchy, unexpected answers. Good for social media captions and short-form content.]

## Interview Logistics Guidance

### Recommended Interview Formats

| Format | Best For | Duration | Output |
|--------|----------|----------|--------|
| In-person (at the practice) | Video testimonial production + case study | 60-90 minutes | Written case study + video footage |
| Video call (Zoom / Teams) | Remote partners; faster turnaround | 30-45 minutes | Written case study; audio-only or basic video |
| Email written response | Quick testimonials; busy partners | Asynchronous | Pull quotes; one-page case study input |
| Phone call + notes | Lowest friction; for resistant interviewees | 20-30 minutes | Quotes and rough outline for writer to develop |

### Recording Protocol

- Always ask permission to record before starting.
- Preferred language: "I would like to record this so I don't miss anything and can get your words right. The recording is just for our notes -- is that okay?"
- Use a dedicated transcription service (Otter.ai or equivalent) to create a searchable transcript.
- Store recordings and transcripts securely with the partner's release documentation.

### Post-Interview Process

1. Within 24 hours: send a thank-you email. Include what you heard as the core story. This gives the partner a chance to correct or confirm.
2. Within one week: draft the case study or pull the quotes. Send the draft to the partner for review before any publication.
3. Before publishing: confirm the following in writing:
   - The partner approves the specific quotes attributed to them
   - The partner approves any specific financial figures cited
   - The partner has reviewed and approved the final version of the case study
   - Photo or video release forms are signed and on file

### Handling "I Don't Want My Name Used"

Some partners will share valuable information but not want to be identified. Options:
- Use the content anonymously: "One SGA partner, a periodontist in the Southeast with 14 years in practice..."
- Ask if there are any details they ARE comfortable with (practice type, region, specialty, timeframe, general metrics)
- Ask again at 6 months. Comfort with being named often increases as the partnership delivers results.
- Never publish identifying information without explicit written permission.

# SGA Dental Partners -- Soap Opera Sequence

**Framework:** Andre Chaperon's Soap Opera Sequence (SOS)
**Primary Audience:** Practice owners (Dr. Mark Hendricks avatar)
**Sequence Length:** 5 emails over 10 days
**Funnel Tag:** `pa_soap_opera`
**Trigger:** Engaged with 3+ emails in welcome/nurture OR downloaded the Confidential Partnership Guide OR attended a webinar/conference touchpoint
**Voice:** Sage archetype -- wise storyteller who has earned the right to share because he has lived it. Dr. Mitch Ellingson as the narrative character. First-person where noted, third-person narrative elsewhere.
**Goal:** Use story to create emotional identification, build the case for partnership through narrative rather than data, and drive to a confidential conversation booking

**How This Sequence Differs from the Welcome/Nurture Sequences:**
The welcome sequence delivers information. The nurture sequence delivers industry intelligence. This sequence delivers a *story* -- a single narrative arc told across five emails, each one a chapter that ends on an unresolved note so the reader has to open the next one. The selling happens inside the story, never outside it.

**Open Loop Architecture:**
- Email 1 opens the loop: "What I discovered surprised me."
- Email 2 deepens the tension: the problem is worse than anyone admits
- Email 3 delivers the epiphany: the moment the solution crystallized
- Email 4 reveals unexpected benefits the reader did not see coming
- Email 5 closes the loop with urgency rooted in real market dynamics

## Email 1: Set the Stage

**Send:** Day 0

**Subject Line Options:**
1. I have been on both sides of the DSO conversation
2. A practicing dentist's confession about partnership
3. The hardest transition I never expected

**A/B Test Variants:**
- A: "I have been on both sides of the DSO conversation" (identity/curiosity)
- B: "A practicing dentist's confession about partnership" (vulnerability/intrigue)

**Preview Text:** What I discovered after going through it myself surprised me.

---

**Body:**

Dr. {{first_name}},

I need to tell you something that most people in my position would never admit.

My name is Dr. Mitch Ellingson. I am a dentist. Not "was a dentist" -- am. I still practice. I still see patients. I still pick up a handpiece every week. That matters, and I will explain why in a moment.

But I am also the Chief Clinical Officer of SGA Dental Partners. And before that, I co-founded Gen4 Dental Partners. And before *that*, I owned multiple practices in Phoenix and founded Spear Practice Solutions.

So I have been on both sides of this conversation.

I have been the practice owner lying awake on Sunday night, dreading Monday because the office manager just quit and the schedule is a disaster and nobody else is going to fix it. I have been the guy doing payroll at 10 PM when he should have been watching his kids' soccer highlights. I have been the dentist who loved the craft and slowly started to resent the business.

And I have been through the transition. The one where you go from being king of your own island to being part of something larger. The one where every colleague tells you you are crazy, and every forum post warns you about selling your soul.

I did it anyway.

What I discovered surprised me.

Not the operational stuff -- I expected the relief of someone else handling HR and billing and compliance. That part was exactly what they promised.

What surprised me was something nobody warned me about. Something I had lost so gradually I did not even realize it was gone until it came back.

I will tell you what it was in my next email. But first, I want you to sit with a question:

When was the last time you were genuinely excited about a Monday morning in your practice?

Not "okay with it." Not "pushing through." Genuinely excited.

If you cannot remember, you are not failing. You are carrying something that was never supposed to be carried alone.

More tomorrow.

Mitch Ellingson, DDS
Chief Clinical Officer
SGA Dental Partners

P.S. -- If you already know you want to have a direct conversation instead of waiting for the rest of this story, I respect that. You can schedule a confidential call here: [Schedule a Confidential Conversation]. No brokers. No pressure. Just two dentists talking.

## Email 2: High Drama

**Send:** Day 2

**Subject Line Options:**
1. What happened to the dentists who chose wrong
2. "I wish I would have never done this." He had ten million dollars.
3. The conversation at Hinman that changed everything

**A/B Test Variants:**
- A: "What happened to the dentists who chose wrong" (fear/curiosity)
- B: "I wish I would have never done this." He had ten million dollars. (specific/shocking)

**Preview Text:** Seven out of ten. That number kept me up at night.

---

**Body:**

Dr. {{first_name}},

Yesterday I told you something surprised me about my own transition from solo practice to group partnership.

Before I reveal what it was, I need to tell you about the thing that almost stopped me from making the move at all.

It was a conversation at a dental conference. Could have been Hinman. Could have been a state meeting. The venue does not matter. What matters is what a colleague told me over dinner.

He had sold his practice to a DSO eighteen months earlier. On paper, the deal was excellent. Strong multiple. Clean terms. Big check.

And he was miserable.

"Mitch," he said, "I wish I would have never done this."

I asked him what went wrong. He told me the support they promised never materialized. The "clinical autonomy" language in the contract meant nothing once the regional manager started sending emails about production targets. His long-tenured hygienist left within six months because the benefits got worse. His office manager -- the woman who had been with him for fifteen years -- was "restructured" out because corporate had their own systems.

His name was still on the door. But his practice did not feel like his practice anymore.

I wish I could tell you his was an isolated story. It was not.

The Dentist Advisors podcast put a number on it that still haunts me: seven or eight out of ten dentists who sell to a DSO end up frustrated, annoyed, or outright regretful. "It's not what they thought."

Seven out of ten.

I started paying attention after that dinner. I started asking every dentist I met who had joined a DSO: how is it going? Really?

The pattern was always the same. The check cleared. The relief lasted about ninety days. Then the calls started -- from people who had never held a handpiece telling you which lab to use, which materials to order, how many patients to see per hour. One dentist told me: "Decisions are being made about what your production is supposed to be by people wearing suits in a boardroom."

The staff left. The culture died. "Dr. Jones is gone, there's been 2 to 3 new doctors there, all the old staff are gone." That is a *patient* talking. Not a dentist. A patient who noticed that the practice they trusted had been quietly hollowed out.

I watched this happen to good dentists. Smart dentists. Colleagues I respected. Not because they made bad decisions -- because the model they sold into was designed to extract, not to partner.

And I thought: it does not have to be this way.

That thought became a conviction. The conviction became a company. But the moment it all crystallized -- the specific epiphany -- happened in a room I did not expect.

I will tell you about that room on Monday.

For now, I want to leave you with this: if you have been googling "sell dental practice" and every result fills you with a mix of hope and dread, you are not paranoid. You are paying attention. The dread is earned. The industry has earned it.

But the hope is legitimate too. I have seen the other side.

More soon.

Mitch

P.S. -- There is a line I keep coming back to. I told it to Lamonte Jensen the first time we sat down together: "I love being a dentist, and I want to protect my profession from becoming like medicine. In medicine, as it consolidated it became less about the doctors and patients, and more about the organization."

That is why I do this. That is why I am writing to you. Not to sell you something. To protect something.

## Email 3: The Epiphany

**Send:** Day 4

**Subject Line Options:**
1. The room where it all changed
2. What if we did not just acquire practices?
3. 38 years of getting it wrong -- and then getting it right

**A/B Test Variants:**
- A: "The room where it all changed" (narrative curiosity)
- B: "What if we did not just acquire practices?" (conceptual intrigue)

**Preview Text:** A Marine, a dentist, and a 38-year track record walked into the same room.

---

**Body:**

Dr. {{first_name}},

I promised I would tell you about the room.

It was not a boardroom. It was not a conference hall. It was a working session -- papers everywhere, whiteboards covered in diagrams, coffee that had been sitting too long. The kind of room where real things get built.

At the table: Lamonte Jensen. Me. And the team from the Thurston Group.

Now, you probably have not heard of the Thurston Group. Most dentists have not. They do not run television ads or sponsor dental conferences for visibility. What they do is build healthcare platforms.

They have done it for 38 years. They have returned more than four billion dollars to their partners across eight platforms. Smile Doctors. US Endodontics Partners. US Oral Surgery Management. And South Georgia Dental Management -- which is where the original SGA started.

That is not a pitch. Those are names you can look up. Numbers you can verify. A track record that predates most DSOs by decades.

But here is what mattered to me in that room. It was not the track record. It was the *question* they asked.

Every DSO conversation I had ever been part of started the same way: "What is the practice worth? What are the multiples? How fast can we close?"

The Thurston team started with a different question: "What went wrong with every other model, and why?"

We spent two days tearing apart the Gen1 accounting machines. The Gen2 cookie-cutter standardizers -- "same supplies in the same place in every office." The Gen3 buying groups that had good intentions and no scale. We looked at why seven out of ten dentists end up regretting the decision. Not the symptoms. The structural causes.

And somewhere in the middle of day two, a thought formed that I have not been able to shake since.

What if we did not just acquire practices -- what if we made the dentists inside them *better*?

Not "better supported." Not "better managed." Actually better at the craft of dentistry.

What if the competitive moat was not back-office efficiency or purchasing power or standardized systems -- but *education*? What if we built a platform where a general dentist in Phoenix could observe his first all-on-four procedure alongside a mentor who makes complex cases fun? Where a periodontist could train at the Pikos Institute -- alongside Dr. Michael Pikos himself -- and bring those skills back to patients who would have been referred out or underserved?

What if the thing that made a dentist choose us was not the check we wrote them at closing, but the dentist they became in year two, year three, year five?

That was the epiphany. And we were so convinced it represented a generational shift in dental consolidation that we put it in the company name.

Gen4. The fourth generation.

Lamonte -- a Marine Corps infantry officer with combat tours in Iraq, an MBA from the University of Chicago, a McKinsey consultant, and a former VP at Spear Education who had seen firsthand what dentists actually struggle with -- looked at the whiteboard and said something I have never forgotten:

"I would like to gather a group of very smart, very successful dentists together who like to collaborate so we can change the game a little bit."

We went from 5 group practices to 90 in 20 months. Not because we had the best pitch. Because dentists were starving for a model that treated them as partners, not products.

But what happened next -- the part I promised to tell you two emails ago, the thing that surprised me about my own transition -- is something I did not expect at all.

It was not about the business. It was deeply personal. And it happened to Dr. Matt Cavendish in a way that I think will resonate with you.

That story is next.

Mitch

P.S. -- I told you in my first email that I still practice. I still pick up a handpiece every week. I said that matters, and I promised to explain why. Here is why: I am not writing to you from a corner office. I am writing from the same world you inhabit. I know what it feels like to have a case go sideways at 3 PM and still have two hours of patients on the schedule. I know what it sounds like when the compressor makes a noise it should not be making. I know the specific kind of tired you feel when you love the work but hate everything surrounding it. That is why I co-founded this. Not to manage dentists. To protect the profession I love.

## Email 4: Hidden Benefits

**Send:** Day 7

**Subject Line Options:**
1. He was bored after 20 years. Then everything changed.
2. The thing about partnership nobody told me about
3. Dr. Cavendish had stopped enjoying dentistry. Here is what happened next.

**A/B Test Variants:**
- A: "He was bored after 20 years. Then everything changed." (transformation hook)
- B: "The thing about partnership nobody told me about" (insider knowledge)

**Preview Text:** Revenue up 16.5%. Staff retention at 100%. But that is not the number he talks about most.

---

**Body:**

Dr. {{first_name}},

Three emails ago, I told you something surprised me about the transition from solo practice to partnership. Something nobody warned me about. Something I had lost so gradually I did not realize it was gone.

Here is the answer: it was the spark.

I do not mean motivation. I do not mean "engagement" or whatever corporate HR calls it. I mean the specific feeling of walking into a case and thinking *this is going to be interesting* instead of *let me get through this day.*

I had been practicing for thirteen years when I made the transition. Somewhere around year ten, the cases stopped being interesting. Not because the cases changed -- because I had stopped growing. There was no one challenging me. No one showing me a technique I had not tried. No one asking, "Have you considered doing it this way?"

I was clinically competent and professionally bored. And I did not have a word for it until I saw it happen to someone else.

His name is Dr. Matt Cavendish. Phoenix. Twenty years in private practice.

When Matt joined Gen4, he described himself with a honesty that most dentists would not risk in public: "Maybe I was in a spot of complacency, not being challenged as much, just getting through the day and not enjoying it as much."

Twenty years. Getting through the day. Sound familiar?

Here is what we did. We paired Matt with a mentor -- Dr. Bryan Shanahan -- through a program we call the Individual Growth Engine. Not a corporate training module. Not a webinar series. A one-on-one clinical mentorship with another practice owner who is operating at a level Matt had not yet reached.

Matt observed his first all-on-four procedure alongside Bryan. Watched the surgical technique. Learned how Bryan worked with his lab. Picked apart the treatment planning approach. Things Matt would never have been exposed to in a solo practice, because in a solo practice, there is nobody to observe. There is nobody to ask.

"That's something I would have never had an opportunity to do if I were still in a solo practice," Matt said.

Then the numbers followed.

Revenue TTM: up 16.5%.
EBITA: beating plan by 2.3 points. Beating prior year by $45,266.
Total payroll expenses: down.
Staff retention: 100%.
Cost of dental supplies: beating plan by 6.4 points.

Those numbers are real. They are Matt's. And they are impressive.

But that is not the number Matt talks about most.

The number Matt talks about is not on any spreadsheet. It is the number of times he has said "it is fun" in the last twelve months. "When you've been in dentistry for 20 years like I have, sometimes it's not as fun as it used to be. So to be able to do a complex case like this, with somebody who is also working at a very high skill level -- it is fun."

The spark came back.

And here is the hidden benefit -- the one I did not see coming, the one that the DSO brochures never mention because it cannot be reduced to a bullet point:

When you stop being the smartest person in your practice -- when you are surrounded by peers who challenge you to think bigger, take the next step, evolve the idea -- something physiological happens. Ellingson's word for it is "goose bumps." Senior clinicians brainstorming together at the 4Front Symposium, naturally elevating ideas collectively. "That's where the goose bumps come from. That's where you go, 'Wow, okay!' They're like, 'I've never thought about it like that.'"

That is not an operational benefit. That is not a financial benefit. That is a *human* benefit. It is the antidote to the isolation that every solo practitioner carries but rarely names.

And it only works because the dentists in the room are owners, not employees. Partners, not subordinates. People who chose to be there because they were hungry for exactly this.

I have a few more things to tell you. About what the equity model actually means -- not just the financial structure, but the second bite of the apple that most dentists miss. About the career pathways that exist inside SGA now that the three verticals are combined. About the 85% staff retention rate that nobody in this industry publishes because nobody else wants to be measured by it.

But those are details. The real question is the one I started with:

Are you still excited about Monday morning?

If the answer is no, it may not be because you chose the wrong profession. It may be because you have been carrying it alone for too long.

Tomorrow I will tell you why the timing of this matters more than you think.

Mitch

P.S. -- Matt Cavendish offered one piece of advice for any dentist considering a partnership: "Don't push back. There's no downside to having a mentor." From a twenty-year practice owner, that is not a small statement. That is a man who has swallowed his pride and come out better for it.

## Email 5: Urgency and CTA

**Send:** Day 10

**Subject Line Options:**
1. The window that is closing (and what to do about it)
2. Why the best time to have this conversation is before you need to
3. Every quarter, 200+ practices sell. Most dentists never see this data.

**A/B Test Variants:**
- A: "The window that is closing (and what to do about it)" (urgency/action)
- B: "Why the best time to have this conversation is before you need to" (wisdom/timing)

**Preview Text:** This is not manufactured scarcity. This is market data.

---

**Body:**

Dr. {{first_name}},

I have spent the last week and a half telling you a story. My story. Matt Cavendish's story. The story of what happens when dentists stop carrying the weight alone.

Today, I need to talk about timing. Not because I am trying to manufacture urgency -- I despise that tactic and you would see through it anyway. But because the market is moving in ways that make this conversation more consequential with every quarter that passes.

Here is what I mean.

**The consolidation wave is not slowing down. It is accelerating.**

There are over 1,000 invisible dental support organizations operating in the US right now. But fewer than 100 are considered qualified bidders by industry experts. That means over 900 organizations are making offers to practice owners with neither the capital, the infrastructure, nor the track record to deliver on their promises.

Every quarter, hundreds of practices change hands. Most of those transactions look fine on day one. Eighteen months later, the Dentist Advisors statistic kicks in: seven out of ten end up frustrated or regretful.

**The talent crisis is reshaping the leverage.**

Over 76% of practices find hygienist recruiting "extremely challenging." The best hygienists, the best associates, the best office managers are flowing toward organizations that offer 401(k) plans, real healthcare benefits, PTO, CE stipends, and career development paths. Solo practices are losing the recruiting war not because they are bad places to work, but because they cannot compete on infrastructure.

Every quarter you wait, the recruiting gap widens. And a practice that cannot fill its hygiene chairs sees revenue drop 15-20%. That revenue decline depresses your valuation at the exact moment you may need it most.

**The practices getting the strongest partnership terms are the ones who come to the table from a position of strength -- not desperation.**

This is the part most dentists get backwards. They wait until they are burned out, until the office manager has left, until the compressor has failed and the roof needs replacing and the practice value has softened. Then they start having conversations from a position of weakness. And they get weak terms.

The dentists who get the best partnerships are the ones who start the conversation while their practice is thriving. While their team is intact. While their revenue is climbing. They come to the table with leverage, and they use it.

**I am not asking you to make a decision today.**

A partnership of this magnitude -- one that involves your life's work, your team, your patients, your financial future -- is not a decision that should be rushed. The timeline from first conversation to signed agreement is typically 6-18 months, and it should be. This is not a transaction. It is a relationship.

What I am asking is simpler: have the conversation now, while you have options.

Here is what that looks like:

You schedule a confidential call with our partnership team. Thirty minutes. No brokers, no NDAs at this stage, no commitment of any kind. You ask every hard question you have been carrying -- about autonomy, about equity, about what changes and what does not. And we answer them. With names. With numbers. With the phone numbers of current partners who will tell you exactly how it has been for them.

If it is not the right fit, you will know within thirty minutes. And you will leave the conversation more informed about your options than you were before it.

If it is the right fit, you will have started a process while your practice is strong, your team is intact, and your leverage is at its highest.

[Schedule a Confidential Conversation]

I have one more thing to say, and then I will stop filling your inbox with long emails.

I told you in my first message that I still practice. That I still see patients. That I still pick up a handpiece.

I do this because dentistry is the last great profession that is still run by the people who do it. Not administrators. Not algorithms. Not suits in a boardroom. Dentists. And I want to keep it that way.

If that resonates with you -- if the reason you have read five emails from a stranger is because somewhere in this story you saw a version of yourself -- then the conversation is worth having.

Not because I am selling you something. Because the profession we both love is worth protecting. And protecting it starts with choosing the right partner.

Mitch Ellingson, DDS
Chief Clinical Officer
SGA Dental Partners

[Schedule a Confidential Conversation]

P.S. -- I promised you names and numbers. Here are a few to start:

- Dr. Mitchel S. Godat, Periodontal Associates of Memphis: "SGA supports us and manages things behind the scenes. Our partnership allows us to focus on changing lives."
- Dr. Matt Cavendish, Phoenix: Revenue up 16.5%. Staff retention 100%. "It is fun."
- The Thurston Group: 38 years. 8 dental and healthcare platforms. $4 billion returned to partners. Not projections. History.
- SGA Network: 85% staff retention rate. Published, because we are not afraid to be measured.

These are not anonymous testimonials. These are people you can talk to. Ask us to connect you. We will.

### Segmentation Notes

- **High engagement (opened all 5):** Tag `pa_sos_engaged`. Priority for business development follow-up even if they do not click.
- **Dropped off at Email 2 or 3:** May indicate the narrative style is not resonating. Route to the data-driven nurture sequence instead.
- **Clicked Email 5 CTA but did not book:** Tag `pa_sos_hot`. Trigger a personal email from BD team within 24 hours.
- **Opened but never clicked:** Tag `pa_sos_passive`. Add to the ISM sequence (longer relationship play).

### A/B Testing Priority

1. **Email 1 subject lines** -- test identity-based ("I have been on both sides") vs. vulnerability-based ("confession") vs. challenge-based ("hardest transition")
2. **Email 2 with/without CTA** -- test adding a soft CTA vs. keeping it pure narrative
3. **Email 5 CTA placement** -- test single CTA at end vs. dual CTA (mid-email + end)
4. **Sequence timing** -- test 2-day gaps (10 days total) vs. daily send (5 days total)

### Integration with Other Sequences

- This sequence should NOT run simultaneously with the welcome or nurture sequences. It is an alternative engagement path for prospects who have already been introduced to SGA.
- Prospects who complete this sequence and do not convert should enter the ISM sequence (ism_sequence.md) with a 7-day gap.
- Prospects who book a call from this sequence should be immediately removed from all other prospect sequences and tagged `pa_discovery_call`.

# SGA Dental Partners -- Invisible Selling Machine Sequence

**Framework:** Ryan Deiss's Invisible Selling Machine (ISM) -- 4-phase email relationship model
**Primary Audience:** Practice owners (Dr. Mark Hendricks avatar)
**Sequence Length:** 7 emails over 28 days
**Funnel Tag:** `pa_ism`
**Trigger:** Completed Soap Opera Sequence without booking a call, OR completed Welcome Sequence and engaged with 3+ emails but did not advance, OR opted in via conference/podcast touchpoint
**Voice:** Sage archetype -- institutional, values-driven, radically transparent. Emails alternate between Dr. Ellingson's clinical perspective and the SGA institutional voice. Sender noted per email.
**Goal:** Build a relationship through shared values, radical transparency, and education so deep that the reader trusts SGA before they ever get on a call. The "selling" is invisible -- it happens through alignment, not persuasion.

**How This Sequence Differs from the Soap Opera Sequence:**
The SOS tells a story with dramatic arc and open loops. This sequence builds a *relationship* through shared identity, values alignment, and trust-building transparency. Each email stands alone as valuable content. The reader could forward any single email to a colleague and it would make SGA look good. The conversion happens because the reader gradually realizes: "These are my people."

**ISM Phase Architecture:**
- Phase 1: Indoctrination (Emails 1-2) -- Establish who we are and what we believe
- Phase 2: Engagement (Emails 3-4) -- Provide value so good it changes how they see the market
- Phase 3: Ascension (Emails 5-6) -- Present the offer through specifics and transformation
- Phase 4: Segmentation (Email 7) -- Identify where they are and route them accordingly

Dr. {{first_name}},

This email is designed to be useful whether you ever partner with SGA or not. Print it. Save it. Forward it to a colleague who is getting approached by brokers. The information is the same regardless.

**Three things your practice broker probably will not tell you about DSO valuations:**

---

**1. "7-9x EBITDA" does not mean what you think it means.**

You have heard the multiples. Mid-market practices (1-3 doctors, $1M-$3M revenue) are trading at 7-9x EBITDA. Group practices with 4+ doctors are commanding 9-12x. Those numbers sound impressive.

But here is what the headline multiple obscures:

The multiple is applied to *adjusted* EBITDA, not to the number on your P&L. Every DSO adjusts differently. Some add back your above-market salary. Some subtract projected capital expenditures. Some apply a management fee that reduces the EBITDA before the multiple is applied -- so a practice that earns $500K in EBITDA might get valued on $350K after "adjustments."

One dentist described it on the Dentist Advisors podcast: "Some dentists believe they sold their practice for 4-6x EBITDA, but the DSO manipulated the math based on the note the dentist carries and the management fee."

The number that matters is not the multiple. It is the net proceeds after all adjustments, holdbacks, and earn-out conditions. Ask any DSO you are talking to for the walk-through calculation. If they cannot provide it in plain language, that tells you something.

---

**2. The equity you retain may be worth more than the check you deposit.**

Most practice owners focus on the upfront check. That is natural. It is the number you can see and touch.

But in a joint-venture equity partnership -- the model SGA uses -- you receive two things: a liquidity event at closing AND an ongoing equity stake in the combined platform.

Here is why the equity matters: as the platform grows (more practices, more revenue, more operational efficiency), the value of your equity stake compounds. In many cases, the retained minority equity position ends up being worth far more than if the doctor had kept 100% of the practice and continued growing solo.

This is not speculative. The Thurston Group has demonstrated this pattern across 8 healthcare platforms over 38 years. "The joint venture equity partnership model we use has generated wealth for partners across many industries, with repeated success in the dental world," MODIS notes on their partnership page. "It's designed to accelerate growth and create value faster than you might on your own."

The question to ask any DSO: what is my equity worth in 3 years? In 5 years? Show me the precedent from a prior platform. If they cannot, that tells you something.

---

**3. Fewer than 100 of the 1,000+ DSOs are considered qualified bidders.**

There are over 1,000 invisible dental support organizations operating in the US right now. The vast majority are undercapitalized, operationally thin, and backed by first-time funds experimenting with the dental space.

Group Dentistry Now reports that "fewer than 100 of the 1,000-plus IDSOs are considered qualified bidders by industry experts." That means over 900 organizations are making offers they may not be able to fulfill.

Before you evaluate any offer, verify three things:

- **Capital source:** Who is the financial sponsor? How long have they been in healthcare? What is their fund size relative to their acquisition pace? (A $200M fund buying $50M of practices per year is going to need to recapitalize within 3-4 years. That recapitalization changes your deal.)
- **Operator track record:** How many dental platforms has the management team built before? What happened to those platforms? Can you talk to dentists who were in the previous platform?
- **Staff retention:** Ask for the number. If they will not give it, that tells you something. SGA's is 85%.

---

**Bottom line:** A DSO deal is not a number. It is a structure. The multiple is one variable in a multi-variable equation that includes EBITDA adjustments, equity terms, management fees, capital investment commitments, earn-out conditions, and the operational track record of the organization making the promise.

Get the structure right, and a partnership can be the best financial decision you have ever made. Get it wrong, and you join the 70% who end up regretting it.

## What SGA Dental Partners Is

SGA Dental Partners is a dental partnership platform backed by the Thurston Group, consolidating three entities under one umbrella:

- **SGA (original):** Community dentistry across the Southeast
- **Gen4 Dental Partners:** Collaborative general and specialty practices nationally
- **MODIS Dental Partners:** Implantology and specialty, anchored by Dr. Michael Pikos and the Pikos Institute

The model is a joint-venture equity partnership. You receive 60-70% of your practice's valuation in upfront cash and retain 30-40% as equity in the combined SGA platform. You keep your practice name, your team, your clinical autonomy. The Thurston Group has built this model 8 times across dental and healthcare, returning $4B+ to partners over 38 years.

The core differentiators: an integrated education platform (Pikos Institute + 4Front Symposium + Individual Growth Engine), three clinical verticals, and the most verifiable PE track record in the dental space.

## Why People Search for Alternatives

We have talked to enough dentists to know the real reasons someone might look for an SGA alternative. Here they are -- honestly.

### 1. "SGA is too small. I want the stability of a larger network."

This is a legitimate concern. SGA has 60-125+ practices across 10-14+ states. Heartland has 1,800+. Aspen has 1,300+. PDS has 1,000+. MB2 has 800+.

If being part of the largest possible network matters to you -- for resources, geographic mobility, purchasing power, or simply the reassurance that comes with scale -- SGA is not the biggest option today. We are growing (a $350M credit facility and three-platform consolidation position us for substantial expansion), but we are honest about where we are right now.

**If scale is your primary criterion:** Consider [Heartland Dental](/sga-vs-heartland) (1,800+ practices, 38 states) or [MB2 Dental](/sga-vs-mb2) (800+ practices, 41 states).

### 2. "I want a clean, full cash exit. I don't want ongoing equity complexity."

SGA's JV equity model is designed for dentists who plan to remain clinically active and want their financial outcome to compound over time. The retained equity is a feature for dentists who see it that way -- but for some, it is complexity they do not want.

If you are 62, ready to retire in 18 months, and want maximum cash at close with no ongoing involvement, SGA's equity model may add unnecessary complexity to your exit.

**If you want a clean cash exit:** Consider [Heartland Dental](/sga-vs-heartland) or Dental Care Alliance -- both offer outright acquisition models with full liquidity at close.

### 3. "I don't want any private equity involvement. Full stop."

We understand. The PE backlash in dentistry is real and rooted in legitimate concerns: short hold periods, aggressive cost-cutting, broken promises. The Thurston Group's 38-year track record and $4B+ returned to partners is our answer to those concerns -- but if PE involvement of any kind is a hard dealbreaker, we respect that.

**If you want zero PE exposure:** Consider [PDS Health](/sga-vs-pds) (founder-led, minority PE only) or Guardian Dentistry Partners (family office backed).

### 4. "I want a nationally recognized consumer brand driving patient volume."

SGA is an operator brand -- built for dentist partners, not consumer patients. Your patients will not see SGA's name on your door. That is by design: we preserve your brand identity.

But if you want a turnkey branded practice with national marketing driving patient demand, SGA does not offer that. We provide marketing support, not a consumer brand.

**If you want a consumer-facing brand:** Consider [Aspen Dental](/sga-vs-aspen) -- the strongest consumer dental brand in America, with national advertising driving patient volume to 1,300+ offices.

### 5. "I'm a pediatric dentist or orthodontist. SGA doesn't seem built for my specialty."

You are right. SGA's specialty focus is on implantology, periodontics, and oral surgery through the MODIS vertical. We do not have a dedicated pediatric or orthodontic operational model. If your practice is primarily pediatric or ortho, a DSO built specifically for those specialties will serve you better.

**If you are a pediatric dentist or orthodontist:** Consider [SALT Dental Partners](/sga-vs-salt) -- purpose-built for your specialty, with 156+ locations and 65+ affiliated brands.

### 6. "I want to own my practice outright. I don't want to sell any portion."

SGA's model involves selling a portion of your practice equity. That is how the liquidity event works. If parting with any ownership stake is fundamentally unacceptable to you, SGA is not the right fit.

**If full ownership is non-negotiable:** Consider [PDS Health](/sga-vs-pds) -- their Owner Clinician model means you own 100% of your practice and PDS provides management services for a fee. No equity changes hands.

### 7. "I've heard the pitch and it sounds too good to be true."

Healthy skepticism is not a problem -- it is a sign of good judgment. Every DSO makes promises during the sales process. The question is whether those promises hold up under scrutiny.

Here is what we suggest: do not take our word for it. Ask to speak with current SGA partners. Not the ones we cherry-pick for you -- ask for partners in situations similar to yours (practice size, specialty, geography, career stage). Ask them what the experience has been like 1, 2, or 3 years post-close. Ask about autonomy, equity, education, and whether the promises matched the reality.

If what you hear does not match what we told you, you should absolutely look elsewhere. But if it does match, that is the evidence that matters -- not our marketing materials.

## How to Evaluate Any DSO (Including Us)

Whether you end up partnering with SGA or one of the alternatives listed here, use this framework to evaluate any DSO:

### 1. Talk to current partners -- not the ones they select for you

Ask for 3-5 partners in similar situations (practice size, specialty, career stage). Ask them:
- What has changed since you joined?
- What has stayed the same?
- What do you wish you had known before signing?
- Would you do it again?

### 2. Understand who is behind the capital

Ask: Who is the PE firm, family office, or financial sponsor? What is their track record specifically in dental? What is the typical hold period? What happens at recapitalization? What is the exit strategy?

### 3. Have your own advisors review the deal

Your attorney and financial advisor should review the partnership agreement independently. Do not rely on the DSO's representations. Pay attention to: equity mechanics, non-compete provisions, exit provisions, clawback language, and what happens if you want to leave.

### 4. Visit a partner practice

Go see what the partnership looks like in the real world. Does the practice still feel like the owner's practice? Is the staff happy? Are the patients treated well? Does the dentist seem engaged or demoralized?

### 5. Ask about the post-deal experience, not just the deal

Every DSO sells the transaction beautifully. The question is what happens in month 6, year 2, year 5. Ask: What does the first 90 days look like? When does the education start? How quickly does the operational support kick in? What is the partner retention rate?

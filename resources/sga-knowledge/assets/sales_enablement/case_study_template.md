# SGA Dental Partners -- Case Study Template & Interview Guide

**Prepared:** March 2026
**Audience:** Marketing team, BD team, content producers
**Purpose:** Standardized framework for capturing, writing, and deploying partner transformation stories
**Philosophy:** Case studies are not testimonials with filler. They are proof narratives. Every case study must answer one question the prospect is silently asking: "What will happen to me?"

---

## Part 1: Interview Preparation

### Before the Interview

1. **Research the partner.** Read their SGA partnership history, practice profile, and any existing quotes or media appearances. Know their practice name, location, specialty, tenure in dentistry, and approximate partnership date.

2. **Identify the story arc you suspect.** Every great case study follows: Before (pain) --> Turning Point (decision) --> After (transformation). Before the interview, hypothesize what each section might contain so you can probe deeper.

3. **Set expectations with the partner.** Tell them: "This will take 30-45 minutes. I am going to ask about your life before SGA, what made you take the conversation seriously, and what has changed since. There are no wrong answers. The more specific you are -- names, numbers, moments -- the more powerful the story."

4. **Record the conversation** (with permission). Transcribe afterward. The best language comes from unscripted moments you cannot recreate.

## Part 2: Interview Question Bank

### Section A: Before SGA (The Pain State)

Goal: Capture the specific, visceral reality of their life before partnership. Surface pain AND hidden pain.

1. Take me back to 12-18 months before you partnered with SGA. What did a typical day look like for you?

2. What was the single biggest frustration you dealt with as a practice owner that had nothing to do with dentistry?

3. How much time per week were you spending on non-clinical tasks -- payroll, HR, compliance, scheduling, supply ordering?

4. Was there a specific moment or incident that made you realize something had to change? Walk me through that day.

5. What was your biggest financial worry at that time? How much of your net worth was tied up in the practice?

6. How were you feeling about dentistry itself? Were you still enjoying the clinical work, or had the business side started to erode that?

7. What had you already tried to solve the problem -- consultants, study clubs, hiring, delegation? Why did those fall short?

8. When did you first start researching DSOs or partnerships? What triggered that search?

9. What was the reaction of your spouse or family when you started talking about a possible partnership?

10. What was the scariest thing about the idea of partnering? What kept you up at night?

### Section B: The Turning Point (The Decision)

Goal: Capture the specific moment, conversation, or proof point that tipped the scales.

11. How did you first hear about SGA? Was it a colleague, an event, a BD call, an article?

12. What was your initial impression? Were you skeptical?

13. What did SGA say or show you during the early conversations that was different from what you had heard from other DSOs?

14. Was there a specific person at SGA who made an impression? Who, and why?

15. Did you talk to existing SGA partners before deciding? What did they tell you?

16. What was the most important factor in your decision -- financial terms, clinical autonomy, the education platform, the Thurston track record, the peer community, or something else?

17. What was the hardest part of saying yes? What almost made you walk away?

18. How did you explain the decision to your staff? What was their reaction?

19. How did you explain it to your patients? Did you have to?

### Section C: After SGA (The Transformation)

Goal: Capture specific, measurable changes -- operational, financial, clinical, emotional. Numbers matter.

20. Walk me through the first 90 days after partnership. What changed first?

21. What operational burden was the first to come off your plate? How did that feel?

22. Have you seen measurable changes in your practice's performance? Revenue growth, patient volume, case acceptance, cost savings -- any specific numbers you can share?

23. What about staff retention? Has anyone left? Has the team dynamic changed?

24. Have you grown clinically since joining SGA? New procedures learned, mentorship experiences, Pikos Institute or 4Front attendance?

25. What is the biggest surprise about the partnership -- something you did not expect?

26. How has your personal life changed? Are you leaving the office earlier? Spending more time with family? Feeling differently about Monday mornings?

27. If you could go back to the version of yourself who was Googling "sell dental practice" at midnight, what would you tell that person?

28. Would you do it again? Would you recommend SGA to a colleague?

### Section D: Proof Points (The Specifics)

Goal: Extract the concrete numbers and named details that make the story credible.

29. What specific metrics can we share? Revenue change (percentage), staff retention rate, new procedures learned, technology invested, time saved per week?

30. Can you name a specific clinical case you would not have been able to do before SGA?

31. Can you describe a specific interaction with your SGA mentor or the peer community that changed your thinking?

32. What would it have cost you to replicate SGA's support independently? Did you ever price it out?

33. Is there one quote or phrase that captures your experience -- something you would say to a colleague considering a partnership?

## Part 3: Writing Formats

### Format A: Long-Form Case Study (1,500 words)

**Structure:**

```
TITLE: [Transformation headline -- focus on the outcome, not the partnership]

SUBTITLE: [Practice name, location, and one-line context]

---

PULL QUOTE: [The single most powerful sentence from the interview, large font]

---

SECTION 1: BEFORE (400 words)
- Open with a specific scene or moment (not a generic description)
- Paint the daily reality: the hours, the burden, the isolation
- Name the specific pains: financial, operational, emotional
- End with the turning point moment

SECTION 2: THE DECISION (300 words)
- How they found SGA
- What was different about the conversation
- The specific proof point that tipped the scales
- The hardest part of saying yes

SECTION 3: AFTER (500 words)
- The first 90 days: what changed first
- Measurable outcomes: revenue, retention, time, clinical growth
- The emotional transformation: what the work FEELS like now
- A specific moment that captures the "after" state

SECTION 4: BY THE NUMBERS (sidebar or callout box)
- 3-5 specific metrics in large, bold format
- Revenue change: +XX%
- Staff retention: XX%
- Time saved: XX hours/week
- New procedures: [list]
- Time as SGA partner: XX months

SECTION 5: IN THEIR WORDS (closing quote)
- The "would you do it again?" answer
- Or the advice-to-self quote from Q27

---

FOOTER:
Practice name | Location | Specialty | Partner since [date]
"Ready for a confidential conversation? [CTA]"
```

### Format C: Video Testimonial Script (3-5 minutes)

**Shot List & Interview Guide:**

```
SEGMENT 1: THE HOOK (15-30 seconds)
[Tight shot on partner's face]
Prompt: "What is the one thing that changed most since you partnered with SGA?"
Goal: The single most compelling soundbite, used as the video's opening.

SEGMENT 2: THE BEFORE (45-60 seconds)
[Medium shot, partner in their practice setting]
Prompt: "Take me back to before SGA. What was your biggest struggle?"
Goal: Specific, relatable pain. Audience should think: "That sounds like me."

SEGMENT 3: THE TURNING POINT (30-45 seconds)
[Medium shot, conversational]
Prompt: "What made you take the conversation with SGA seriously?"
Goal: The specific moment or proof point. Creates identification for the prospect.

SEGMENT 4: THE TRANSFORMATION (60-90 seconds)
[Wide shot showing practice, team, technology; intercut with partner speaking]
Prompt: "Walk me through what changed. What does a day look like now?"
Goal: Specific outcomes -- numbers, moments, feelings. B-roll of the practice running smoothly.

SEGMENT 5: THE CLOSE (15-30 seconds)
[Tight shot, direct to camera]
Prompt: "What would you say to a dentist who is where you were 2 years ago?"
Goal: Peer-to-peer advice. The audience hears from someone like them, not from SGA.

---

B-ROLL LIST:
- Partner greeting patients
- Partner in operatory (clinical work, NOT staged)
- Practice exterior with local signage (proving the name stayed)
- Team interacting naturally
- Technology in use (CEREC, scanner, digital workflow)
- Partner leaving the office at a reasonable hour
- If available: Partner at 4Front Symposium or Pikos Institute

PRODUCTION NOTES:
- Natural light preferred. No corporate set.
- Partner should wear what they normally wear. No costume changes.
- Interview in their practice, not a studio. Authenticity > production value.
- Total runtime: 3-5 minutes (can be cut to 60-second and 30-second versions)
- Subtitles mandatory (most social viewing is sound-off)
```

# "It Is Fun Again": How a 20-Year Veteran Rediscovered Why He Became a Dentist

**Dr. Matt Cavendish | Gen4 Partner | General Dentistry**

---

> "When you have been in dentistry for 20 years like I have, sometimes it is not as fun as it used to be. So to be able to do a complex case like this, with somebody who is also working at a very high skill level -- it is fun."

---

### Before

Twenty years into his career, Dr. Matt Cavendish knew something had shifted.

The clinical work was fine. His patients were loyal. His practice was profitable. By every external measure, he was succeeding. But internally, Dr. Cavendish had hit a wall he did not quite know how to name.

"I was in a spot of complacency," he later admitted. "Not being challenged. Just getting through the day."

It is a diagnosis few dentists will say out loud, but many recognize. After two decades of the same procedures, the same routine, the same Sunday-night administrative grind, the spark that drew him to dentistry had dimmed. He was not burned out in the dramatic sense -- no crisis, no blowup. It was subtler than that. A slow erosion of the excitement that used to carry him through a clinical day.

He was not alone. Research from the Dentist Advisors podcast suggests that the majority of dentists who remain in solo practice beyond 15 years experience some version of this: professional isolation, clinical plateau, and the creeping weight of running a business that never stops demanding attention.

Dr. Cavendish had tried the usual remedies. CE courses here and there. A study club that met occasionally but changed nothing about his daily practice. The suggestions were always the same: new marketing, new technology, new systems. Nobody ever said: "Maybe what you need is a peer who challenges you to do things you have never done before."

### The Turning Point

Dr. Cavendish's introduction to Gen4 (now part of SGA Dental Partners) came through the dental network. What caught his attention was not the financial terms or the operational support -- those were things every DSO promised. What caught him was the education platform and the peer collaboration model.

"Most DSOs talk about taking things off your plate," he said. "Gen4 talked about putting things ON your plate -- new skills, new cases, new peers. That was different."

The Individual Growth Engine -- SGA's 1:1 clinical mentorship program -- was the specific mechanism that tipped his decision. He would be paired with an experienced clinician who could expand his capabilities, not just manage his business. The 4Front Symposium offered annual immersion with peer clinicians. The Pikos Institute offered a pathway into implant dentistry he had never seriously considered.

For a dentist in a "spot of complacency," this was not a sales pitch. It was a lifeline.

### After

The transformation was both measurable and deeply personal.

Through the Individual Growth Engine, Dr. Cavendish was paired with a mentor who introduced him to full-arch implant procedures. He observed his first all-on-four case -- not in a lecture hall, but in a live clinical environment alongside a colleague who walked him through the technique, the lab relationships, and the case planning.

"To be able to do a complex case like this, with somebody who is also working at a very high skill level -- it is fun," he said. The word "fun" carried weight. For a 20-year veteran who had forgotten what clinical excitement felt like, fun was not a small thing. It was everything.

The numbers confirmed the transformation. Trailing twelve months after partnership, Dr. Cavendish's practice achieved:

- **16.5% revenue growth** (TTM)
- **100% staff retention** -- not a single team member left
- **EBITDA beating plan** -- the practice outperformed financial projections

But the metric Dr. Cavendish talks about most has nothing to do with a spreadsheet.

"When you have been in dentistry for 20 years, sometimes it is not as fun as it used to be," he said. "SGA changed that. Not by changing how I practice. By giving me peers who challenge me to practice at a level I did not know I was capable of."

His practice still has his name. His team is still his team. His patients still see his face first. What changed is that Dr. Cavendish is no longer carrying it all alone -- and he is no longer just getting through the day.

He is doing work that excites him again.

## Part 5: Distribution & Usage Guide

### Where to Deploy Case Studies

| Format | Channel | Purpose |
|--------|---------|---------|
| Long-form (1,500 words) | Website partner stories page, PDF for email follow-up, conference handout | Deep proof for serious prospects in evaluation phase |
| One-page (500 words) | Leave-behind at meetings, attachment to BD emails, social media link | Quick proof for prospects in early consideration |
| Video testimonial (3-5 min) | Website, YouTube, LinkedIn, Facebook ads, conference presentations | Emotional proof for all stages; social media amplifies reach |
| 60-second video cut | Instagram Reels, LinkedIn video, YouTube Shorts, paid social ads | Awareness and top-of-funnel engagement |
| 30-second video cut | Pre-roll ads, conference booth loops, email signature embed | Hook attention, drive to full story |
| Pull quote + metric | Social media posts, email subject lines, presentation slides | Proof snippets for ongoing content calendar |

### Target: Build a Library

**Minimum viable library:** 5 case studies covering:
1. Community practice owner (Dr. Hendricks archetype)
2. Group practice leader (multi-location)
3. Specialist / periodontist (Dr. Whitfield archetype)
4. Associate-to-partner journey (Dr. Nair archetype)
5. Long-tenured partner (5+ years with SGA -- demonstrates long-term value)

**Stretch library (12+ case studies):**
- Geographic diversity (Southeast, Southwest, Midwest, West)
- Specialty diversity (general, perio, oral surgery, implantology, pediatric)
- Demographic diversity (age, gender, career stage)
- Motivation diversity (succession planning, clinical growth, financial, burnout relief)

### Quality Standards

Every published case study must include:
- [ ] At least one specific metric (revenue change, retention, time saved)
- [ ] At least one direct quote from the partner
- [ ] The practice name and location (proving the name stayed)
- [ ] The before-turning point-after narrative arc
- [ ] No unverified claims or projected numbers
- [ ] Partner sign-off before publication
- [ ] CTA directing the reader to the next step

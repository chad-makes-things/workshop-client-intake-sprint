### Ah-Ha Moment #1: The Three Models (8:00 - 15:00)

**[SLIDE: "3 Models. 3 Fundamentally Different Experiences." with icons for each]**

**MITCH:**

"Let me walk you through the three partnership models that actually exist. Not the marketing names -- the structural realities.

**[SLIDE: "Model 1: The Corporate Acquirer (Gen2 DSO)"]**

"Model 1 is what most people think of when they hear 'DSO.' The Corporate Acquirer.

Here is how it works. They buy your practice outright. You receive cash at closing -- often a decent multiple. You sign an employment agreement and stay on for 3 to 5 years as a salaried dentist. Maybe a production bonus. Your name may or may not survive. Your clinical decisions are increasingly influenced by corporate protocols. And here is the critical part: your financial upside ends at closing. There is no second act.

This is the Heartland model. The Aspen model. The DCA model for many of their acquisitions.

I am not here to tell you this is always bad. For a dentist 18 months from retirement who wants maximum cash now and does not care what happens to the practice after they leave, this can work.

But for most of the dentists I talk to -- people who are 45, 50, 55, who want to keep practicing for another decade, who care about their team and their patients and their name on the building -- this model is the source of almost all of the regret in the '7 out of 10' statistic. Because you sign the deal feeling like an owner, and 18 months later you feel like an employee.

As one dentist put it on the Dentaltown forums: 'When you sell, you are no longer the employer -- you are the employee.' The transition from owner to employee is the single most underestimated emotional shock in dental consolidation."

**[SLIDE: "Model 2: The Management Company"]**

"Model 2 is less well-known but increasingly common. The Management Company.

This is the opposite structure. They do not buy your practice. You retain full ownership. Instead, you pay them a management fee -- typically 15-25% of collections -- and in return they provide operational support: billing, payroll, HR, marketing, procurement.

On paper, this sounds great. You keep ownership, you get support, nobody is telling you what to do.

In reality, the economics are brutal. You are paying 15-25% of your collections for services that may or may not be worth it, with no equity appreciation to offset the cost. If the management company underdelivers, you are stuck paying for services you are not receiving -- and extricating yourself from the contract is harder than you think.

There is no platform equity in this model. You are paying for a service, not joining a partnership. And the management company's incentive is to charge you more, not to make your practice more valuable."

**[SLIDE: "Model 3: The Equity Partnership (Gen4 / IDSO)"]**

"Model 3 is what we built. The Equity Partnership.

Here is the structure. You sell a portion of your practice equity -- typically 55-70% -- and receive meaningful liquidity at closing. But you retain 30-45% of that equity, which is rolled into the combined platform. So your financial upside does not end at closing. As the platform grows -- through additional partnerships, organic growth, and operational improvements -- your equity appreciates.

Clinical autonomy is not just a talking point. It is contractual. You choose your materials, your lab, your schedule, your treatment plans. Your name stays on the door. Your team stays in the chairs. Your patients do not know anything changed -- except that the billing got better and you started coming home earlier.

Now, here is the critical distinction: not all equity partnerships are the same. The 'invisible DSO' or 'IDSO' space has exploded from a handful of players to over 1,000 organizations. And the Dentist Advisors podcast was right -- fewer than 100 of those 1,000+ are considered qualified by industry experts.

So the question is not 'should I do an equity partnership?' The question is 'how do I tell a genuine equity partnership from one that uses the same language but delivers the same experience as a corporate acquirer?'

That is what the next section answers."

## SECTION 4: TEACHING SECTION 3 -- "The Economics Nobody Explains" (35:00 - 45:00)

### Ah-Ha Moment #3: The Real Math (35:00 - 42:00)

**[SLIDE: "The Economics Nobody Explains"]**

**LAMONTE:**

"This is the section where I am going to be blunt, because the financial literacy gap in dental consolidation is costing practice owners real money.

Let me start with multiples, because that is where every conversation begins and -- unfortunately -- where most conversations end.

**[SLIDE: EBITDA Multiple Ranges graphic -- community practice 5-7x, group practice 7-9x, high-performing group 9-12x, specialty 6-8x]**

You will hear numbers thrown around: '5x,' '7x,' '10x.' Let me decode what those actually mean.

The multiple is applied to your EBITDA -- your earnings before interest, taxes, depreciation, and amortization. For a practice doing $2.4 million in revenue with healthy margins, the EBITDA might be $600,000 to $800,000. At 7x, that is a $4.2 to $5.6 million deal.

Sounds great. But here is where the math gets complicated -- and where some organizations play games.

**First: How is EBITDA calculated?** Are they using your actual trailing-twelve-month EBITDA? Or are they using an 'adjusted' EBITDA that adds back owner compensation, discretionary expenses, and pro-forma synergies? The same practice can have an EBITDA of $600,000 or $900,000 depending on which methodology is used. A '7x deal' on $600K is very different from a '7x deal' on $900K.

**Second: What is the actual cash-at-close?** A 7x deal does not mean you receive 7x in cash. The structure matters enormously. If 65% is cash at close and 35% is rolled equity, your immediate liquidity on a $5 million deal is $3.25 million, not $5 million. And the 35% you rolled? Its value depends entirely on what happens to the platform over the next 5-10 years.

**Third: Earnouts.** Some deals include a 15-20% earnout component tied to revenue retention or EBITDA maintenance over 2-3 years. If your practice dips -- even for reasons outside your control, like a hygienist leaving or a pandemic -- you may not receive the full deal value.

I am not saying these structures are unfair. Some are genuinely aligned. But you need to understand the difference between the 'headline multiple' and the 'effective multiple' -- what you actually receive in present-value terms."

**[SLIDE: "The Second Bite"]**

"Now let me explain the concept that makes equity partnerships fundamentally different from outright sales.

In an equity partnership, you receive two financial events:

**The first bite:** Cash at closing. Your immediate liquidity event.

**The second bite:** When the platform itself transacts -- when the PE sponsor recapitalizes, merges, or completes a subsequent liquidity event -- your retained equity participates.

Here is why this matters. If you sell 100% of your practice for 7x EBITDA, you receive 7x and your financial relationship with that practice is over.

If you sell 65% for the equivalent of a 7x headline and retain 35% in the platform, and the platform appreciates to the equivalent of 12-15x on a combined basis at the next transaction -- your retained equity can be worth more than the cash you received at closing.

Industry analysts have written about this directly: 'In many cases, the retained minority equity position will be far more valuable in the future than if the doctor had kept 100% of the practice.'

This is the second bite of the apple. And it is the mechanism that aligns your financial interests with the platform's long-term success. But -- and this is critical -- the second bite only works if the PE sponsor has a track record of creating that appreciation. Which brings us back to Question 3 in the framework."

## Appendix B: Speaker Notes -- Tone & Delivery

### Mitch (Throughout)

- Speak as a peer, not a presenter. Use "we" and "you" -- never "our clients" or "our portfolio."
- Reference personal experience: "I have been through this." "I was terrified of exactly this."
- When addressing objections, validate before countering: "I hear this in every conversation" before the reframe.
- Pace: conversational. Not rushed, not overly polished. This is a dentist talking to dentists, not a keynote speaker.
- Pauses: use them after key insights. Let the 7-out-of-10 stat land. Let the "clinical autonomy is meaningless" line breathe.
- Voice: lower the volume when delivering the most important line. "Fun. That is the word." should be said quietly, not emphatically.

### Lamonte (Throughout)

- Bring the analytical lens. When Mitch is emotional and relational, Lamonte is precise and evidence-based.
- Numbers should be delivered cleanly and without hype: "38 years. 8 platforms. $4 billion returned."
- When discussing PE fears, acknowledge them fully before countering. "Some of that fear is earned" before "But here is the reframe."
- Marine credibility: do not lean on it, but the brief mention in the bio establishes a discipline-and-service orientation that dentists respect.
- When doing the financial education section, slow down. Give people time to process the EBITDA math. Ask "does that make sense?" before moving to the next concept.

### Joint Dynamics

- Mitch opens, Mitch closes. Lamonte takes the financial and PE sections.
- Natural hand-offs: "Lamonte, you built the financial model -- take them through this." Not: "I'll now hand it over to my colleague."
- Occasional genuine aside between speakers: "Lamonte, remember when [partner name] asked us that exact question?" Reinforces that this is a real relationship, not a scripted presentation.
- During Q&A, let Mitch anchor clinical questions and Lamonte anchor financial/structural questions. On overlap questions, let one answer and the other add 1-2 sentences.

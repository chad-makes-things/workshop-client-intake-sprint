## Why This Assessment Exists

You did not go to dental school to manage payroll disputes at midnight.

Yet here you are -- reviewing financials on a Sunday night, fielding calls from your office manager about a staffing crisis, and wondering if you will ever feel the way you felt when you first opened your doors. Somewhere between the pride of what you built and the weight of carrying it alone, a question started forming:

*Is there a better way to do this?*

You are not the first practice owner to ask that question. Hundreds of dentists -- owners of thriving, community-rooted practices -- have sat exactly where you are sitting right now: proud of what they built, exhausted by what it demands, and uncertain about what comes next.

This assessment is not a sales pitch. It is a diagnostic tool -- the same framework that experienced dental practice advisors use to help owners evaluate their readiness for a partnership conversation. It will help you:

- **See your practice clearly** across six dimensions that matter in any transition
- **Identify your strengths** and the gaps that may be costing you value, time, or clinical fulfillment
- **Understand your readiness tier** -- whether you are in a building phase, an exploration phase, or ready for a serious conversation
- **Ask the right questions** of any DSO, partner, or advisor who approaches you

The assessment takes 10-15 minutes. Your results are yours -- no one sees them but you.

One more thing: this tool was designed to be genuinely useful regardless of whether you ever speak to SGA or anyone else. A practice owner who completes this assessment and decides to stay solo will still walk away with a clearer picture of where they stand. That is the point.

Let's begin.

You are earlier in the journey. Your practice may have significant areas that need strengthening before a partnership conversation would serve you well -- or you may simply not be emotionally ready for the idea, and that is perfectly fine.

This score does not mean partnership is not in your future. It means the smartest move right now is to invest in the fundamentals.

**Your recommended next steps:**

### Tier 2: Exploring (51-79 Points)

**What this means:**

You have a solid foundation with clear room for growth. You are likely feeling the tension between what your practice demands and what you want from your career and life. You may be in the "midnight Googling" phase -- searching "sell dental practice" or "DSO pros and cons" late at night, not yet ready to pick up the phone but increasingly aware that something needs to change.

This is the most important phase of the journey, because the decisions you make now -- who you talk to, what you learn, how you evaluate your options -- will determine whether your eventual transition is one of the 3 in 10 that go well or one of the 7 in 10 that end in frustration.

**Your recommended next steps:**

1. **Get a baseline practice valuation.** Not from a broker who is incentivized to get you to sell, but from an independent dental practice appraiser. Know your number before anyone else gives you theirs.

2. **Talk to dentists who have been through it.** Not the ones on the DSO website (they were selected for those testimonials). Find dentists in your network who have made a transition -- any transition -- and ask them what surprised them. Ask what they would do differently. Ask about year two, not just closing day.

3. **Understand the models.** Not all partnerships are the same. An outright sale to Heartland is fundamentally different from a joint-venture equity partnership with a collaborative platform. Learn the difference between:
   - Corporate DSOs (you become an employee; your name may change; clinical protocols are standardized)
   - Management service organizations (you keep ownership; they charge fees; no equity upside)
   - Joint-venture equity partnerships (you retain equity in the combined platform; your identity is preserved; you participate in the platform's growth)

4. **Identify your non-negotiables.** What must be preserved in any partnership? Your practice name? Your team? Your clinical autonomy? Your schedule? Write them down. Any partner worth considering will ask you this question -- have your answer ready.

5. **Evaluate your personal financial picture with your advisor.** How much liquidity do you need? What does your retirement timeline require? What would it mean to diversify your net worth out of a single illiquid asset? These questions shape what kind of deal makes sense for you.

**Resources:**
- The "Questions to Ask Any DSO" checklist below
- Dental practice valuation guide from your state dental association
- Dentist Advisors podcast (search "DSO" episodes for candid perspectives)

### Tier 3: Ready to Talk (80-110 Points)

**What this means:**

Your practice is well-positioned, your financial house is in order, and you are emotionally ready to explore what a partnership could look like. You are not looking for a rescue -- you are looking for an amplifier. You want to know: what happens to my practice when I am no longer carrying it alone? What does year two look like? Year five?

This is a position of strength. You have options, you have leverage, and you have the luxury of choosing a partner based on fit rather than necessity.

**Your recommended next steps:**

1. **Have three to five confidential conversations.** Talk to multiple potential partners -- not to create a bidding war, but to understand the range of models, cultures, and deal structures available. The quality of the conversation itself will tell you a lot about the quality of the partner.

2. **Bring your advisors to the table.** Your CPA, your financial advisor, and your attorney should all be part of the evaluation process. A good partner will welcome their involvement. A partner who tries to keep your advisors out of the room is a partner to avoid.

3. **Ask the hard questions.** Use the "Questions to Ask Any DSO" checklist below. Ask for named references from partners who joined 2-3 years ago (not 6 months ago). Ask about year-two satisfaction, not just closing-day excitement.

4. **Visit a partner practice.** Nothing substitutes for walking into a practice that has been inside the platform for 18+ months and seeing how it actually runs. Talk to the staff. Talk to the dentist. Ask what changed and what stayed the same.

5. **Negotiate from knowledge.** You know your EBITDA. You know your market position. You know your non-negotiables. Use them. The right partner will respect clarity and conviction. The wrong partner will try to rush you.

**A note on timing:** Being "ready to talk" does not mean you need to move fast. The best partnerships are built over months of conversation, site visits, and mutual evaluation. Urgency is almost always the buyer's pressure, not yours. Take the time to get it right.

## Questions to Ask Any DSO

### The Due Diligence Checklist Every Practice Owner Needs

The following questions are designed to separate the partners who have built something real from the ones who have built a pitch deck. Use them in any conversation with a DSO, IDSO, management company, or equity partner. Their answers -- and especially their willingness to answer -- will tell you everything.

---

### Section A: Track Record and Stability

1. **How long has your backing entity been in business, and how many healthcare platforms have they built?** (First-time funds have a learning curve. You do not want to be their tuition.)

2. **How much capital has your backer returned to partners across all investments?** (Ask for verifiable numbers, not projections.)

3. **Can I speak directly with 3-5 partners who joined your platform 2+ years ago -- partners you did not select for me?** (If they only offer curated references, that is a red flag.)

4. **What is your total partner count, and what is your partner retention rate?** (How many dentists who joined are still there? How many left, and why?)

5. **What is your staff retention rate across the network?** (Staff turnover is the most honest metric of organizational health. If they do not track it or will not share it, that tells you something.)

---

### Section B: Autonomy and Identity

6. **Will my practice keep its name, branding, and community identity?** (Get this in writing. "We preserve your brand" is not the same as a contractual guarantee.)

7. **Who makes clinical decisions -- treatment plans, materials, labs, scheduling, hiring?** (Ask for specifics. "You have full clinical autonomy" is a claim. "These decisions are contractually yours, and here is the clause" is a commitment.)

8. **Will I be required to use specific vendors, labs, or supply contracts?** (Some DSOs mandate centralized purchasing. This can save money, but it can also force you to use inferior materials.)

9. **What happens if I disagree with a corporate decision that affects my practice?** (Every partnership has friction. What matters is how it is resolved. Is there a governance structure, a clinical advisory board, a dispute resolution process?)

10. **Can I see the partnership agreement template before I commit to due diligence?** (Transparency here is non-negotiable. If they will not show you the structure until you are deep into the process, walk away.)

---

### Section C: Financial Structure

11. **Walk me through the equity model in detail. What percentage do I retain? How is it valued? What triggers a liquidity event on my retained equity?** (This is where most regret stories begin. Understand every term.)

12. **What management fee does the platform charge, and what does it cover?** (Management fees range from 5% to 25%+ of collections. What you get for that fee varies enormously.)

13. **Are there clawback provisions, earnout penalties, or non-compete implications I should know about?** (Ask directly. "The money, they have a clawback or they had some type of provision that they didn't know about" is the most common regret.)

14. **What is your expected hold period, and what happens at exit?** (If they are backed by a PE fund with a 5-year exit horizon, you need to understand what the next ownership transition looks like and whether your equity carries through.)

---

### Section D: Operational Support

15. **What specific back-office functions will you take over, and what is the timeline?** (Billing, payroll, HR, compliance, recruiting, marketing -- get the list and the timeline. Vague promises of "support" are worthless.)

16. **What technology investments will you make in my practice, and over what period?** (Capital investment is one of the biggest draws of partnership. Pin it down.)

17. **How do you handle recruiting when I need a new associate, hygienist, or front desk team member?** (Recruiting support is one of the highest-value services a platform provides. Understand their infrastructure.)

18. **What continuing education resources are available, and are they included in the partnership?** (Is CE a line-item stipend or a genuine education ecosystem? There is a vast difference between "$1,500 a year for CE" and "access to a world-class institute with hands-on mentorship.")

---

### Section E: Culture and Community

19. **What does the peer community look like? How often do partners interact, and in what format?** (Some platforms have genuine peer collaboration. Others have an annual dinner. The difference matters.)

20. **How do you handle the transition with my existing staff?** (The first 30 days post-close are make-or-break for staff morale. Ask for the onboarding playbook.)

21. **What career development opportunities exist for my non-clinical team?** (A partner that invests in your office manager's growth is a partner that understands retention.)

22. **What does your partner NPS or satisfaction score look like?** (If they do not measure it, they are not managing it.)

---

### Red Flags to Watch For

- **They rush the timeline.** A good partner wants mutual fit. A bad one wants signed paper.
- **They will not provide unscripted references.** If you can only talk to hand-picked testimonials, the story they are not telling you is the one that matters.
- **They avoid specifics on equity terms.** Vagueness on financial structure is never accidental.
- **They dismiss your advisors.** A partner who discourages you from involving your CPA or attorney is protecting their position, not yours.
- **They promise everything will "stay the same."** Some things change in every partnership. The honest partner tells you what will change and why.
- **Their staff turnover is high or undisclosed.** Culture is revealed in retention numbers, not in mission statements.

### Green Flags That Signal a Real Partner

- **They encourage you to talk to your advisors before going further.**
- **They provide named, contactable references -- including partners who joined 2+ years ago.**
- **They can articulate exactly what changes and what stays the same, in writing.**
- **They have a verifiable track record across multiple platforms and years.**
- **Their partners speak about the partnership with specificity, not just enthusiasm.**
- **They measure and share staff retention, partner satisfaction, and clinical outcomes.**

"Seven or eight out of 10 are pretty bugged or annoyed or frustrated or regretful. It's not what they thought."

One dentist they spoke to had $10 million in cash from the sale. Ten million dollars. And he said:

"I wish I would have never done this."

So what goes wrong? After studying the patterns across every available source -- Dentaltown, White Coat Investor, Student Doctor Network, Glassdoor reviews, industry podcasts -- three structural failures account for most of the regret:

**Failure 1: Promised support never materialized.**

"I didn't get the support I thought. They just like took control or sort of control, but I'm still like running it."

The DSO promised to handle billing, HR, compliance. In practice, they charged a management fee and handed the dentist a login to new software. The administrative burden did not decrease. It just moved from QuickBooks to a different platform with a different set of problems.

**Failure 2: Equity and financial terms were worse than expected.**

"The money, they have a clawback or they had some type of provision that they didn't know about, or the equity did not turn out to be what they thought."

The multiple looked good on paper. But the DSO manipulated the math -- management fees, carried notes, earnout structures with unreachable targets. The headline number and the actual check were very different.

**Failure 3: Autonomy eroded gradually.**

"When you sell, you are no longer the employer -- you are the employee."

Nobody said "you can't use that lab" on day one. Instead, it was a slow drift. New supply contracts. Scheduling templates. Production expectations. Suggested treatment protocols that became mandatory. By month 18, the practice owner looked around and realized they were running someone else's practice with their name on the door.

**What the other 3 out of 10 did differently:**

They chose partners with verifiable track records -- not pitch decks. They talked to existing partners directly, not just the business development team. They verified that clinical autonomy was contractual, not just conversational. And they aligned with organizations where the equity model had been tested across multiple platforms and hold periods.

That is the model we built at SGA. Not because we are smarter than other DSOs. Because we studied why they fail, and we refused to repeat the pattern.

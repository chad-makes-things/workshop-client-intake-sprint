Dental Practice Valuation Guide: What Your Practice Is Actually Worth

How dental practice valuations work in 2026. EBITDA methodology, market multiples, factors that drive premium valuations, and the mistakes that cost practice owners real money.

SGA
SGA Dental Partners Team
May 6, 2026 13 min read

What is your dental practice worth? It is one of the most important financial questions a practice owner will ever ask, and the answer is more nuanced than most dentists expect. Whether you are considering a partnership with a dental service organization, exploring a private sale, or simply want to understand your largest financial asset, a credible practice valuation is the foundation of every decision that follows.

The dental practice M&A market in 2026 is the most active it has ever been. Record transaction volumes, aggressive PE-backed acquirers, and a wave of baby boomer retirements have created a seller's market in many regions. But a seller's market does not mean every practice commands a premium. Valuation depends on methodology, metrics, and a dozen practice-specific factors that separate a standard offer from an exceptional one.

This guide breaks down how dental practice valuations actually work -- the methodologies, the multiples, and the factors that move the needle in either direction.

EBITDA: The Metric That Drives Dental Practice Valuations

If you learn one financial concept before entering any practice sale conversation, make it EBITDA. Standing for Earnings Before Interest, Taxes, Depreciation, and Amortization, EBITDA represents the operating profitability of your practice stripped of financing decisions, tax strategies, and non-cash accounting charges. It is the industry standard metric that buyers use to determine what your practice is worth.

How to Calculate Normalized EBITDA

Start with your practice's net income (bottom line from your P&L statement). Then add back:

  • Interest expense on practice loans and equipment financing
  • Income taxes paid by the practice entity
  • Depreciation of equipment and leasehold improvements
  • Amortization of intangible assets

This gives you raw EBITDA. But the number that matters for valuation purposes is normalized EBITDA -- EBITDA adjusted for owner-specific expenses and one-time costs that would not carry forward to a new owner or partnership structure. Common normalization adjustments include:

  • Owner compensation above market rate: If you pay yourself $450,000 but a market-rate associate would cost $250,000, the $200,000 difference gets added back to EBITDA
  • Personal expenses run through the practice: Vehicle costs, personal travel, family member salaries for non-working roles, country club memberships
  • One-time expenses: Legal fees from a single event, equipment that is not recurring, renovation costs
  • Below-market rent: If you own the building and charge the practice below-market rent, EBITDA should be adjusted to reflect fair market rent

Key insight: Normalized EBITDA is where the negotiation begins. A practice with $1.5M in revenue and $350,000 in normalized EBITDA is worth significantly more per dollar of revenue than a practice with $1.5M in revenue and $200,000 in EBITDA. Margins matter more than top-line numbers.

Current Market Multiples: 2026 Benchmarks

Once you have calculated normalized EBITDA, the valuation is determined by the multiple applied to it. In dental practice transactions, the multiple is influenced by practice size, growth profile, geographic desirability, and the competitive dynamics of the buyer landscape.

Practice Profile Collections Range EBITDA Multiple Implied Valuation
Solo practice, single location $750K - $1.5M 5x - 7x $375K - $1.05M
Small group, 2-3 locations $2M - $5M 7x - 9x $1.4M - $4.5M
Mid-market group, 4-10 locations $5M - $15M 8x - 10x $4M - $15M
Large group, 10+ locations $15M+ 10x - 12x $15M+

These ranges represent the current 2026 market. Several factors are sustaining elevated multiples: continued private equity interest in dental, a limited supply of well-run practices in desirable markets, and the proven scalability of dental platform acquisitions. However, multiples above 12x EBITDA remain rare and are generally reserved for large, fast-growing multi-location groups with strong management teams and diversified revenue streams.

Mid-Market Sweet Spot

The most active segment of the market is practices with $1M to $3M in collections and $200,000 to $600,000 in normalized EBITDA. These practices are large enough to be meaningful acquisitions for DSOs but small enough that a partnership creates genuine operational value. For practice owners in this range, 2026 represents an historically favorable selling environment.

Factors That Increase Practice Value

Not every practice with the same EBITDA is worth the same multiple. Buyers pay premiums for specific practice characteristics that reduce risk and increase growth potential.

Growth Trajectory

A practice with three consecutive years of revenue growth commands a meaningfully higher multiple than a practice with flat or declining revenue, even if their current-year EBITDA is identical. Growth signals market opportunity, patient demand, and operational health. Buyers are purchasing the future, not just the present. If your practice has been growing 5% to 10% annually, that trajectory is worth real money at the negotiating table.

Specialty Mix

Practices that offer high-value specialty services -- particularly dental implants, orthodontics, and oral surgery -- command premiums because they generate higher revenue per patient visit and are more difficult for competitors to replicate. A general practice with a strong implant program is worth more than a general practice without one, all else being equal. This is one reason SGA Dental Partners invests in giving its partner dentists access to the Pikos Institute for advanced implant training -- it increases the clinical capabilities and economic value of every partner practice.

Geographic Desirability

Location affects valuation in two ways. First, practices in underserved or growing markets with favorable demographics command premiums because of the growth opportunity. Second, practices that fill a geographic gap for a specific buyer -- adding density to an existing market cluster or establishing a presence in a new market -- can receive strategic premiums above standard market rates. A DSO with ten practices in Georgia will pay a premium for a practice in a Georgia market where they lack presence, because that acquisition strengthens the entire network.

Associate Leverage

Practices where the owner-dentist produces a smaller percentage of total revenue are less risky for buyers. If you have productive associates generating 40% or more of practice production, the business is less dependent on any single provider. This associate leverage creates succession readiness and justifies higher multiples. Practices that are entirely owner-dependent face valuation discounts of 1x to 2x EBITDA compared to practices with strong associate programs.

Technology and Facility Quality

Modern equipment reduces the capital investment required post-acquisition. Practices with CBCT scanners, intraoral scanners, digital workflows, and well-maintained facilities signal a forward-thinking operation that has been continuously invested in. Outdated equipment and deferred facility maintenance signal deferred costs that buyers will factor into their offer.

Factors That Decrease Practice Value

Just as certain factors command premiums, others create valuation discounts that can cost practice owners hundreds of thousands of dollars.

Owner Dependency

This is the single most common value-destroyer in dental practice transactions. If you are the practice -- if production drops 60% or more when you take a two-week vacation -- buyers see significant transition risk. Reducing owner dependency by building an associate program, empowering your hygiene team, and developing your office manager takes time but directly increases practice value.

Declining Patient Base

A shrinking active patient base signals market problems that are difficult to reverse. Track your new patient numbers, reactivation rates, and active patient counts over the past three years. If these numbers are declining, address the root causes before going to market. Common drivers include local population shifts, increased competition, reduced marketing investment, and patient experience issues.

Deferred Maintenance

Aging equipment, outdated technology, and deteriorating facilities create capital expenditure obligations for the buyer that directly reduce what they are willing to pay. A buyer who needs to invest $200,000 in equipment upgrades within the first year will deduct that amount (or more) from their offer. Strategically investing in equipment and facility improvements before a sale can yield a net positive return through higher valuation.

Unfavorable Lease Terms

Short remaining lease terms, above-market rent, or restrictive lease provisions create risk for buyers. A practice with two years left on its lease and no renewal option is worth less than the same practice with a ten-year lease at market rates. If you own the real estate, be prepared to negotiate lease terms separately from the practice transaction.

Common Valuation Manipulation Tactics

Caution: Not every buyer approaches valuation with transparency. Be aware of these common tactics that can result in an artificially low valuation of your practice.

Aggressive Normalization Adjustments

Some buyers will strip out legitimate expenses as "above market" or add back less than the fair market adjustment for owner compensation. If the buyer's normalization produces a significantly different EBITDA than your own calculations, demand line-by-line transparency on every adjustment.

Cherry-Picking Financial Periods

A buyer who values your practice based on your weakest recent year -- rather than a trailing 12-month average or three-year average -- is artificially suppressing the valuation. If your practice had an anomalous down year due to construction, staffing changes, or a pandemic year, that context should be reflected in the valuation methodology.

Inflated Capital Expenditure Deductions

Some buyers will identify every piece of equipment that is not brand-new and deduct estimated replacement costs from the valuation, even for equipment that is functional and has years of useful life remaining. Push back on unreasonable capex deductions with documentation of equipment condition and service history.

Comparing Incompatible Deal Structures

A 9x EBITDA offer that is 100% cash at close is fundamentally different from a 12x offer that is 40% cash and 60% earnout over five years. Always compare offers on the basis of total guaranteed consideration and the probability-weighted value of contingent payments. An offer with a higher headline multiple is not automatically better.

Getting an Independent Valuation

Relying solely on a buyer's valuation is like having your opponent set the terms of the negotiation. Every practice owner considering a sale or partnership should obtain at least one -- and ideally two -- independent perspectives on practice value.

Step 1: Gather Financial Records

Compile three to five years of tax returns, monthly and annual profit and loss statements, production reports by provider, collection reports, accounts receivable aging, and payer mix analysis. The more organized and transparent your financial documentation, the more credible your valuation will be.

Step 2: Engage a Dental-Specific Advisor

Work with a CPA or valuation firm that specializes in dental practice transactions. General business appraisers often miss dental-specific factors like hygiene program economics, associateship dynamics, and insurance reimbursement trends. A dental-specific advisor will produce a valuation that reflects the nuances of your practice and the current market.

Step 3: Get Multiple Perspectives

The most informed practice owners get valuations from multiple sources: an independent appraiser, a dental-specific broker, and one or more potential DSO partners. Comparing these perspectives reveals where consensus exists and where significant disagreements may indicate either opportunity or risk.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the average EBITDA multiple for a dental practice in 2026?

For a typical single-location practice with $1M to $2M in collections, the average EBITDA multiple in 2026 is approximately 6x to 8x. Multi-location practices and those with specialty services can command 8x to 12x. These averages vary significantly by geography, growth trajectory, and buyer competitiveness.

How long does a dental practice valuation take?

A professional practice valuation typically takes four to six weeks from the time financial documents are submitted to the appraiser. DIY valuations using industry benchmarks can provide a rough range in a few hours, but they lack the precision and credibility needed for actual negotiations.

Should I improve my practice before getting a valuation?

If you have 12 to 18 months before your target transaction date, strategic improvements can yield meaningful valuation increases. Focus on growing EBITDA margins, reducing owner dependency, upgrading critical equipment, and securing favorable lease terms. Do not make major changes within six months of a transaction, as buyers may view them as unsustainable window dressing.

How do DSO valuations compare to private buyer offers?

DSOs typically offer higher EBITDA multiples than individual private buyers because they have access to institutional capital and can realize economies of scale. However, DSO deals often include equity retention, earnout provisions, and ongoing employment terms that should be evaluated as part of the total compensation package, not just the headline multiple.

What is the difference between seller's discretionary earnings and EBITDA?

Seller's discretionary earnings (SDE) adds the owner-dentist's total compensation back to EBITDA, producing a higher number. SDE is commonly used in smaller practice transactions where the buyer will be the primary provider. EBITDA is used in larger transactions and DSO partnerships where the practice will continue to employ a clinical team. Make sure you are comparing offers on the same basis.

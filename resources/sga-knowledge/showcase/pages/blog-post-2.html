What Is a DSO? Dental Service Organizations Explained for Practice Owners

An honest guide covering DSO generations, equity structures, and what practice owners should know before signing anything.

SGA
SGA Dental Partners Team
April 22, 2026 11 min read

If you are a dental practice owner, you have almost certainly been contacted by a DSO. Maybe you have received a cold call, a LinkedIn message, or a letter offering to "discuss your practice's future." Perhaps a colleague recently partnered with one and you are curious -- or concerned -- about what that means. Whatever brought you here, this guide will give you a clear, honest understanding of what dental service organizations are, how they work, and what you should evaluate before considering a partnership.

The term "DSO" gets used as a catch-all, but not all dental service organizations are the same. Understanding the differences is the most important step you can take before engaging in any conversation about your practice's future.

Definition and History: From Gen1 to Gen4

A dental service organization (DSO) is a company that provides non-clinical business support services to dental practices. The concept is straightforward: dentists focus on patient care while the DSO handles the administrative and operational functions of running a business -- billing, payroll, HR, compliance, marketing, procurement, and more.

The DSO model has evolved through four distinct generations, each reflecting a different philosophy about the relationship between business operations and clinical practice.

Generation 1: The Back-Office Machine

The earliest DSOs emerged in the 1990s as accounting-driven operations focused on centralizing administrative functions. These organizations were built around financial efficiency -- consolidating billing, payroll, and purchasing to reduce costs. The clinical side was largely left alone, but there was also minimal investment in clinical quality, education, or community building. The relationship was transactional: the DSO handled paperwork, the dentist handled teeth.

Generation 2: The Corporate Standardization Model

Gen2 DSOs took centralization further by standardizing the clinical environment itself. Same supplies in the same place in every office. Same protocols, same patient flows, same treatment planning guidelines. Large-scale Gen2 organizations like Aspen Dental and early Heartland Dental built national brands on this consistency. The upside was operational predictability and scale. The downside was that dentists often felt like interchangeable parts in a machine -- their clinical judgment subordinated to corporate protocols, their individual practice identity erased in favor of a uniform brand.

Generation 3: The Fragmented Middle

Gen3 represents the wave of buying groups, management services organizations (MSOs), and loose dental networks that emerged as reactions to the Gen2 model. These organizations offered some operational support without the heavy-handed standardization, but they often lacked the capital, infrastructure, and leadership to deliver meaningful value. Many Gen3 organizations were vehicles for real estate plays or quick-flip private equity transactions, not genuine long-term partnerships.

Generation 4: The Collaborative Partnership Model

The 4th generation of DSOs -- the model pioneered by Gen4 Dental Partners and now embodied by SGA Dental Partners -- is built around a fundamentally different premise. Rather than standardizing practices or loosely affiliating them, Gen4 organizations create collaborative networks of autonomous clinicians who share resources, knowledge, and equity while maintaining full independence over their clinical practices. The motto is not "we'll run your practice" but "we'll invest in your growth while you run your practice."

As Lamonte Jensen, CEO of Gen4 Dental Partners, explains: "Most DSOs are built around the synergies of the business -- standardized accounting, automated payroll, bulk purchasing. What we wanted to do was make an organization that was clinician-focused and patient-obsessed."

How DSO Models Work

Understanding the mechanics of DSO partnerships requires looking at three key components: management fees, equity structures, and employment relationships.

Management Services

At their core, all DSOs provide management services to dental practices. These typically include accounts payable and receivable, payroll processing, human resources, regulatory compliance, insurance credentialing, marketing, technology infrastructure, and procurement. The scope and quality of these services varies enormously between organizations. Some DSOs provide robust, well-staffed support teams that genuinely reduce the administrative burden on partner dentists. Others offer bare-bones services that fall short of what the dentist could manage independently.

Equity and Compensation

Compensation structures fall into three broad categories. In an outright acquisition, the dentist sells 100% of the practice and may or may not continue as a salaried employee. In a management services agreement, the dentist retains ownership but pays the DSO a percentage of revenue for operational support. In a joint-venture equity partnership, the dentist sells a portion of the practice, receives a liquidity event, and retains equity in the combined platform entity.

The equity model matters enormously for long-term wealth creation. Equity in the individual practice limits your upside to that single location. Equity in the platform gives you exposure to the growth of every practice in the network. SGA uses the platform equity model, meaning partners own shares in SGA Dental Partners for as long as they remain active partners.

Employment vs. Partnership

Some DSOs employ dentists as W-2 employees with production-based compensation. Others structure the relationship as a partnership where the dentist retains meaningful ownership and governance participation. The distinction matters for everything from tax treatment to career autonomy. In an employment model, the DSO is your employer and can set expectations about production, scheduling, and clinical protocols. In a partnership model, you remain a practice leader with contractual protections around clinical independence.

Types of DSOs

The DSO landscape includes several distinct organizational models, and the terminology can be confusing. Here is a practical taxonomy.

Corporate DSOs

Large-scale, centrally managed organizations that own and operate dental practices under a unified brand or management structure. These are typically Gen2 models with standardized operations. Examples include Aspen Dental, Pacific Dental Services, and Heartland Dental. Corporate DSOs often employ dentists directly and maintain significant control over clinical operations, even when they claim otherwise.

Management Services Organizations (MSOs)

MSOs provide back-office services in exchange for a management fee, typically 15% to 30% of collections. The dentist retains practice ownership. The MSO handles administration. This model offers operational relief without a liquidity event or equity diversification. The risk is that management fees can erode profitability, and the MSO has limited incentive to invest in the practice's long-term growth.

Independent DSOs (IDSOs)

IDSOs preserve practice autonomy while providing shared services and infrastructure. They are typically smaller than corporate DSOs and emphasize practice-level independence. The IDSO label has become popular marketing, however, and not every organization using the term delivers genuine independence. Always evaluate the reality against the branding.

Joint-Venture Equity Partnerships

This model -- exemplified by SGA Dental Partners -- combines a liquidity event with ongoing equity participation. The dentist receives cash at close, retains equity in the combined platform, and continues practicing with full clinical autonomy. The DSO provides operational support, capital investment, and a collaborative peer community. This is the Gen4 model, and it represents the most alignment between the interests of the dentist and the organization.

The Consolidation Landscape in 2026

The dental industry is in the middle of an unprecedented consolidation wave. More than 1,000 organizations now identify as DSOs or IDSOs, up from fewer than 200 a decade ago. Private equity firms have invested billions in the sector, and the pace of acquisition activity shows no signs of slowing.

Yet despite the proliferation of DSOs, fewer than 100 are what most industry analysts would consider "qualified" -- meaning they have the capital, leadership, operational infrastructure, and track record to deliver on their promises to partner dentists. Many of the 1,000+ organizations are thinly capitalized, poorly managed, or primarily designed to generate short-term returns for their financial sponsors rather than long-term value for their dental partners.

This is the central challenge for practice owners evaluating the DSO landscape: there are more options than ever, but telling the good from the bad requires careful due diligence.

What to Evaluate in a DSO Partnership

If you are considering a DSO partnership, focus your evaluation on five critical dimensions.

Clinical Autonomy

Does the DSO contractually guarantee your clinical independence? Not as a marketing claim, but in the partnership agreement itself. Who controls treatment planning, materials selection, lab choices, and scheduling? Ask for specific contractual language, not reassuring words.

Track Record

How long has the DSO's financial sponsor been in dental? In healthcare? How many platforms have they built, and what were the outcomes for partners? The Thurston Group, which backs SGA Dental Partners, has 38 years of experience and has returned over $4 billion to partners across 8+ healthcare platforms. That kind of track record is rare and meaningful.

Equity Performance

How has equity in the DSO's platform performed for existing partners? What is the mechanism for equity valuation and liquidity? What happens to your equity if you retire? These are questions that separate serious partnership organizations from organizations designed primarily to enrich their financial sponsors.

Education and Development

A great DSO should make you a better dentist. Evaluate the organization's continuing education offerings. SGA's ecosystem includes the Pikos Institute for advanced implant training, the 4Front Symposium for peer-led clinical collaboration, and individualized mentorship programs. These are not standard CE stipends -- they are integrated development programs that create measurable clinical growth.

Culture and Community

How do partner dentists interact? Is there a genuine peer community, or are you just another location on a corporate org chart? Ask to attend a partner event or listen in on a partner call before signing anything. At SGA, the culture is described as "a partnership of dentists invested in each other" -- but the only way to verify that claim is to talk to the dentists themselves.

Red Flags and Green Flags

As you evaluate DSO partnership opportunities, watch for these warning signs and positive indicators.

Red Flags

  • Reluctance to share references from current partners
  • Vague equity terms with no written documentation
  • No named clinical leadership -- only MBAs running the organization
  • Aggressive timeline pressure ("this offer expires in 30 days")
  • High partner turnover or frequent leadership changes
  • Marketing that promises "autonomy" without contractual backing
  • A financial sponsor with no prior dental or healthcare experience

Green Flags

  • Willingness to connect you with multiple long-tenured partners
  • Clinical leadership by practicing dentists (not just administrators)
  • Transparent equity mechanics with documented valuation methodology
  • Investment in education that goes beyond a CE stipend check
  • A financial sponsor with a multi-decade track record in healthcare
  • Practice names and identities preserved across the network
  • High staff retention rates (SGA maintains 85%)

"I love being a dentist, and I want to protect my profession from becoming like medicine. In medicine, as it consolidated it became less about the doctors and patients, and more about the organization. We want it to be more about the doctors and the patients." -- Dr. Mitch Ellingson, CCO, Gen4 Dental Partners

The DSO model, when executed well, can provide dentists with financial security, operational relief, clinical development, and professional community. When executed poorly, it produces the regret statistics that have given the model a bad reputation. The difference is not in the model itself -- it is in the people and principles behind the organization.

Understanding the DSO landscape is the first step toward making a confident decision about your practice's future. Whether you ultimately pursue a partnership or remain independent, the knowledge you gain through this evaluation process will make you a more informed, more empowered practice owner.

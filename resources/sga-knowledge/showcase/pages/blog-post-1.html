How to Sell Your Dental Practice: The Complete 2026 Guide

A step-by-step guide for dental practice owners considering a sale or partnership. Covers valuation, DSO evaluation, equity models, due diligence, and how to protect what you built.

SGA
SGA Dental Partners Team
April 15, 2026 18 min read

Selling a dental practice is one of the most significant financial and emotional decisions a dentist will ever make. You have spent years -- often decades -- building a patient base, training a team, and establishing a reputation in your community. Whether you are considering retirement, looking for operational relief, or simply exploring what your next chapter could look like, this guide walks you through the entire process from initial readiness assessment to closing day.

The dental practice transaction market in 2026 is more active than at any point in the profession's history. Over 1,000 dental service organizations (DSOs) now compete for acquisitions, private equity firms have poured billions into the sector, and the average practice owner over 55 is fielding unsolicited offers on a monthly basis. Yet despite all this activity, most dentists enter the process without a clear roadmap. This guide is designed to change that.

Step 1: Assess Your Readiness

Before entertaining any offers or initiating conversations, you need an honest assessment of where you stand -- financially, operationally, and personally.

Financial Health

Start with the numbers. Buyers and partners will scrutinize your last three to five years of financial performance. Key metrics include annual collections, overhead ratio, EBITDA (earnings before interest, taxes, depreciation, and amortization), and net production per provider. If your overhead consistently exceeds 65%, or if collections have been flat or declining, these factors will influence your valuation and limit your options.

A well-run practice with healthy margins, predictable revenue, and a diversified payer mix is positioned for a premium valuation. If your financials need work, investing 12 to 18 months in optimization before going to market can yield a materially better outcome.

Owner Dependency

One of the most significant factors in practice valuation is the degree to which the practice depends on the owner-dentist. If you personally generate 80% or more of production, a buyer faces meaningful risk that revenue will decline post-transaction. Practices with productive associate dentists, strong hygiene programs, and empowered office managers command higher multiples because the business can sustain itself through a transition.

Ask yourself: Could this practice operate without me for 30 days and maintain 70% or more of its normal production? If the answer is no, you have work to do before bringing your practice to market.

Personal Timeline

Selling a dental practice is not something you do on impulse. The best outcomes come from practice owners who start planning two to five years before their target transition date. This runway allows time to improve financial performance, reduce owner dependency, upgrade equipment, and explore multiple partnership or sale options without pressure.

Are you looking to exit completely within a year? Do you want to practice for another five to ten years but with less administrative burden? Your personal timeline fundamentally shapes which transaction structure makes sense.

Step 2: Understand Your Options

The dental practice sale landscape has evolved dramatically. In 2010, most practice transitions were simple private sales -- one dentist selling to another. In 2026, you have a spectrum of options, each with different financial, operational, and lifestyle implications.

Private Sale to Another Dentist

The traditional model: you find a buyer, negotiate a price, and transfer ownership. Private sales typically yield valuations of 60% to 85% of annual collections or 4x to 6x EBITDA. The upside is simplicity and a clean break. The downside is that individual buyers have limited capital, financing can be challenging, and the process can take 12 to 24 months to complete.

Broker-Assisted Sale

Dental practice brokers can help you market the practice, screen buyers, and negotiate terms. Good brokers bring market knowledge and a buyer network. However, broker commissions typically run 7% to 10% of the sale price, and the broker's incentive is to close a deal, not necessarily to find the best deal for you. Always work with a broker who specializes in dental -- generalist business brokers rarely understand the nuances of practice valuation.

DSO Partnership

Dental service organizations offer a fundamentally different model. Rather than an outright sale, many DSOs structure transactions as joint-venture equity partnerships. You receive a significant liquidity event (cash at close) while retaining an equity stake in the combined platform. You continue practicing, your team stays, your practice name remains, and you participate in the growth of a larger organization.

DSO partnerships typically offer higher valuations than private sales -- often 7x to 12x EBITDA -- because DSOs have access to institutional capital and can realize economies of scale. The trade-off is that you are now part of a larger organization, and the quality of that organization varies enormously. Not all DSOs are created equal, and your experience will depend entirely on which partner you choose.

Step 3: Get a Practice Valuation

Understanding what your practice is worth is not optional -- it is the foundation of every negotiation that follows. Yet many practice owners rely on a single valuation from a potential buyer, which is like asking the opposing team to keep score.

EBITDA Methodology

The most widely used valuation methodology in dental practice transactions is the EBITDA multiple approach. EBITDA represents the practice's true operating profitability, adjusted for non-cash charges and owner-specific expenses. To calculate normalized EBITDA, start with net income and add back interest, taxes, depreciation, amortization, and owner-specific expenses such as above-market compensation, personal vehicle costs, and non-essential travel.

In 2026, market multiples for dental practices range from approximately 6x EBITDA for smaller, single-location practices to 10x to 12x for larger, multi-location groups with strong growth trajectories. The multiple applied to your EBITDA depends on a range of factors including practice size, geographic location, specialty mix, growth trajectory, and the competitive dynamics of the buyer landscape.

Market Multiples in 2026

Current market data indicates the following ranges:

  • Solo practices ($750K-$1.5M collections): 5x-7x EBITDA
  • Small groups (2-3 locations, $2M-$5M collections): 7x-9x EBITDA
  • Mid-market groups (4-10 locations, $5M-$15M collections): 8x-10x EBITDA
  • Large groups (10+ locations, $15M+ collections): 10x-12x EBITDA

These ranges are general benchmarks. Your specific valuation will depend on dozens of variables, including payer mix, facility conditions, lease terms, team stability, and growth trajectory.

Strategic Premium Factors

Certain practices command valuations above standard market multiples. Practices located in underserved markets where a DSO has strategic interest in establishing a presence can receive a geographic premium. Practices with established specialty services -- particularly dental implants, orthodontics, or oral surgery -- attract specialty premiums. Practices with strong digital infrastructure, modern facilities, and robust associate programs demonstrate reduced risk and justify higher multiples.

Step 4: Evaluate Potential Partners

If you are considering a DSO partnership, the evaluation process is critical. There are now more than 1,000 organizations calling themselves DSOs or IDSOs, and the quality difference between the best and worst is staggering. Industry surveys suggest that as many as 7 out of 10 dentists who joined a DSO express frustration or regret -- a statistic that reflects poor matching, not the inherent flaw of the model itself.

Clinical Autonomy

The single most important question for any dentist evaluating a DSO partnership: Will I retain full control over clinical decisions? This means control over treatment planning, materials selection, lab choice, scheduling, and patient interactions. The right DSO will put clinical autonomy in writing -- not as a marketing slogan, but as contractual language. If a DSO says "we don't interfere with clinical decisions" but cannot point to specific protections in the partnership agreement, that is a red flag.

Equity Structure

Not all equity is created equal. Some DSOs offer equity in the individual practice entity, which limits your upside. Others offer equity in the parent platform, giving you exposure to the growth of the entire network. Understand what entity your equity is in, how it is valued over time, what triggers liquidity events, and what happens to your equity if you retire or transition out.

The SGA model uses a joint-venture equity partnership where partner dentists receive ownership shares in the combined platform for as long as they remain active partners. This creates diversified wealth -- your financial future is not tied to a single location but to the performance of a growing national network.

Private Equity Track Record

Most DSOs are backed by private equity firms. The PE sponsor's track record matters enormously because it signals how they treat partners, how they approach growth, and what outcomes previous investors and partners have experienced. Ask specific questions: How many healthcare platforms has this PE firm built? What were the outcomes for doctor partners? Can I speak with partners from prior platforms?

Education and Development

A DSO should make you a better dentist, not just a less burdened one. Evaluate the organization's commitment to continuing education. Is CE a perfunctory stipend, or is it an integrated development program? Step 5: Enter Discovery Conversations

Once you have identified one or more potential DSO partners, it is time for deeper conversations. These discovery meetings should feel collaborative, not transactional. A good DSO will be as interested in evaluating fit with you as you are with them.

What to Ask

Prepare a structured list of questions covering clinical autonomy protections, equity mechanics, staff impact, operational support scope, technology investment, and partner community. Ask for specifics, not generalities. "Tell me exactly what happens to my office manager's role in the first 90 days" is a better question than "How do you handle staffing?"

Reference Checks

Ask to speak with at least three current partners who have been in the network for two or more years. Recent partners are still in the honeymoon phase; partners with tenure can speak to real operational realities. Ask these partners: "What surprised you most about the first year?" and "What would you do differently if you went through the process again?"

Site Visits

Visit existing partner practices. Look at the signage -- does the original practice name remain? Talk to the staff -- are they happy? Observe the clinical environment -- does it feel doctor-led or corporate-managed? The best due diligence happens when you see the model in action, not when you read about it in a pitch deck.

Step 6: Complete Due Diligence and Close

The due diligence and closing process for a dental practice partnership typically takes 60 to 120 days. It is a rigorous process, but a well-organized DSO will guide you through each step.

Financial Audit

The buyer's team will conduct a thorough review of your financial records, including tax returns, profit and loss statements, production reports, accounts receivable aging, and payer contracts. This is standard and expected. Be transparent -- surprises discovered during due diligence erode trust and can kill deals.

Legal Review

Engage a dental-specific attorney to review all transaction documents. This includes the partnership agreement, employment agreement, non-compete provisions, equity documents, and any management services agreements. Do not use a general practice attorney for this -- dental practice transactions have specific nuances that require specialized legal counsel.

Staff Transition

One of the most common concerns practice owners have is what happens to their team. In the SGA model, staff stays. Benefits typically upgrade -- SGA provides 401(k), PTO, healthcare, and CE stipends that most independent practices cannot match. The organization maintains an 85% staff retention rate, which speaks to the quality of the transition experience.

Communication is key. Work with your DSO partner to develop a staff communication plan before the close date. Your team should hear the news from you, not through rumors, and they should understand what changes (benefits, support systems) and what stays the same (their jobs, their roles, their patients).

The 60-120 Day Timeline

  • Days 1-15: Letter of intent signed, due diligence begins, legal counsel engaged
  • Days 15-45: Financial audit, facility inspection, lease review, staff assessment
  • Days 45-75: Transaction document negotiation, equity structuring, employment terms
  • Days 75-90: Final document review, regulatory filings, closing preparation
  • Days 90-120: Closing, funding, transition begins

What Monday Morning Looks Like After

Perhaps the most important question -- and the one least frequently answered -- is what happens after the transaction closes. In a well-structured partnership, the answer is surprisingly simple: you practice dentistry. The administrative weight lifts. The billing, compliance, HR, payroll, and marketing functions transfer to the DSO's support infrastructure. Your practice name stays on the building. Your team stays in the chairs. Your patients see the same faces they have always seen.

The difference is that you are no longer managing a business alone. You are leading a clinical practice within a platform that handles the business side, invests in your growth, and connects you with a community of peers who share your commitment to excellent patient care.

Long-Form Case Study (1,500-2,000 Words)

The evidentiary anchor for prospects asking "does this actually work for people like me?"

HEADLINE

Formula: [Result] + [Who] + [Timeframe]

"How Periodontal Associates of Memphis Grew Revenue 16.5% and Got Its Doctor Back to Dentistry"

"From Solo Periodontist to Full-Arch Platform: One Specialist's First Year with MODIS"

"What Happened After [Practice Name] Stopped Running QuickBooks and Started Running a Dental Practice"

The headline is a promise. Make it specific. Vague headlines ("A Better Partnership Experience") are skipped. Specific headlines ("Revenue Up 16.5%, Staff Turnover Down to Zero") are read.

SNAPSHOT BOX Top of article, visually pulled out
Partner: [Name, credentials, practice, city/state]
Practice type: [General / Periodontal / Implant / etc.]
Years in practice: [#]
Challenge: [One sentence]
Key result: [Single most compelling number]
Partnership timeline: [Date joined]

For the skimmer. If they read nothing else, this box tells the essential story. "Revenue up 16.5% in 12 months" not "significant revenue growth."

Section 1: BEFORE -- The Situation (300-400 words)

  • • Where was this person 12-24 months before? What did their day look like?
  • • What was the specific problem? Not "administrative burden" but "spending Sunday nights reconciling payroll."
  • • What had they already tried? Why did it fall short?
  • • What was the cost of staying -- financial, emotional, professional?
  • • Was there a specific tipping-point moment?

DO NOT write: "Like many dental practice owners, [Name] was feeling the pressure of running a modern practice."

DO write: "At 7 PM on a Tuesday in March 2024, [Name] was at his desk reviewing insurance denials instead of at his daughter's soccer game. Again."

Section 2: THE DECISION -- Why SGA (250-350 words)

  • • What had they heard/thought about DSOs before?
  • • What made them take the first call seriously?
  • • What surprised them? What almost stopped them?
  • • What ultimately convinced them?

DO NOT write: "After a thorough evaluation process, [Name] chose SGA Dental Partners as the right fit."

DO write: "He had taken two other DSO calls before SGA. One wanted to change his brand immediately. The other wouldn't let him choose his own lab. SGA was the first conversation where they asked what he wanted his practice to look like in five years."

Section 3: THE PARTNERSHIP JOURNEY -- First 90 Days to Year One (400-500 words)

  • • What was the onboarding experience like? First 30 days?
  • • First tangible change? (iSolved, recruiting, new website)
  • • "This is actually different" moment?
  • • Any friction or challenges? How resolved?

"Operational support" means nothing. "The billing team caught $47,000 in denied claims in the first 90 days" means everything.

Section 4: THE RESULTS -- What Changed (300-400 words)

  • • Specific numbers: revenue, EBITA, staff turnover, new patient volume, production
  • • What changed that doesn't show on a spreadsheet?
  • • Unexpected benefits?

Reference: Dr. Matt Cavendish Results

Revenue TTM: +16.5% EBITA: +$45,266 vs prior year Staff retention: 100% Supply costs: -6.4 pts vs plan Payroll expenses: down

Section 5: THE QUOTE -- Shareable Sound Bite

Pull one 2-4 sentence quote capturing the entire arc. Must be specific, emotional, in their natural voice, and fully attributed.

"If you were having coffee with a dentist who is exactly where you were 18 months ago -- same situation, same fears -- what would you say to them?"

CLOSING + CTA (100-150 words)

"[Practice name] is still [practice name]. [Name]'s patients still see his/her face when they walk in. His/her staff is still there. What changed: [one-sentence summary].

If you are a practice owner thinking about what your next chapter looks like, the best first step is a direct conversation -- not with a broker, not with a sales team, but with a partner dentist who has already made the decision."

[CTA: Schedule a Confidential Conversation]

One-Page Case Study (400-500 Words)

A leave-behind read in 90 seconds at a conference table, glanced at in an email, or handed to a spouse.

HEADER

Practice name | Partner name, credentials | City, state | Specialty/practice type

CHALLENGE (75-100 words)

One focused paragraph. Lead with the specific pain, not the category of pain.

"[Name] had spent [X] years building [practice name] into a [adjective] practice in [city]. But by [year], he was spending more time managing his staff and chasing insurance claims than treating patients. His billing team was inconsistent. Recruiting had stalled. And the growing stack of compliance requirements was sitting at the back of his desk, unread."

WHY SGA (75-100 words)

Must answer: "Why SGA and not another DSO or no DSO at all?"

"After two disappointing DSO conversations -- one wanted to rebrand his practice immediately, another couldn't tell him who would handle his billing -- [Name] reached SGA through a colleague's referral. What was different: the first conversation was with a practicing dentist, not a sales rep."

RESULTS (100-150 words)

Bulleted metrics first. One to two sentences of qualitative impact.

  • • Revenue: [X% increase / TTM change]
  • • EBITA: [Beating prior year by $X]
  • • Staff retention: [X%]
  • • Overhead / supply costs: [Change]
  • • [Other specific metric]

IN THEIR WORDS (50-75 words)

Pull quote. Full name, practice name, city attribution.

FOOTER

"[Practice name] is still [practice name]. The name on the door has not changed. The team has not changed. The patients have not changed. What changed: [Name] is not carrying the business alone anymore."

Video Testimonial Script (3-5 Minutes)

The highest-converting proof format in B2B sales and patient acquisition.

Pre-Interview Setup

Technical Requirements
  • • Location: Partner's own practice (authentic)
  • • Camera: Eye-level, natural/softbox light, clean background
  • • Audio: Lapel mic required; eliminate room echo
  • • Interviewer: Off-camera, friendly, unhurried
Briefing Script

"We are going to have a conversation about your experience. We are not looking for polished answers -- we are looking for honest ones. If you stumble, we will cut it. The only thing that matters is that it sounds like you."

Interviewer Principles

1. Ask and then shut up

Let the silence do the work. Most interviewees fill silence by going deeper.

2. Follow the specifics

"Things got better" needs a follow-up: "What specifically got better?"

3. Acknowledge, then go deeper

Acknowledgment earns the deeper story.

4. Be comfortable with discomfort

"What almost stopped you?" is more useful than "What made you excited?"

5. Let them tell the story their way

Guide gently to cover essentials, but don't interrupt a good story.

6. The best quote is the one they almost didn't say

"I probably shouldn't say this, but..." -- invite them to finish.

Section 1: Before SGA -- Context, Pain & Tipping Point

Q1. Set the scene for me

"Before you made this decision, what did a typical week look like for you? Walk me through it."

Q2. What was wearing on you most?

"If you had to name the one thing -- the thing that was draining your energy or keeping you up at night -- what was it?"

Q3. What had you tried before?

"Before you connected with SGA, had you tried to fix this problem in other ways? What did you try, and why did it fall short?"

Q4. The cost of not changing

"If you had kept doing what you were doing -- if nothing had changed -- where would things be now?"

Q5. The tipping point

"Was there a specific moment or event that made you think 'something has to change'? What was it?"

Section 2: The Decision -- What Convinced Them

Q6. What alternatives did you consider?

"When you were making this decision, what other options were on the table? How did you evaluate them?"

Q7. What made you take the first call seriously?

"A lot of people hear about DSO partnerships and don't even take the call. What made you decide to have this conversation?"

Q8. What surprised you?

"What was different about the SGA conversation from what you were expecting?"

Q9. What almost stopped you?

"Was there a point where you almost walked away? What was the concern? How was it resolved?"

Q10. What ultimately convinced you?

"When you finally decided to move forward, what was the thing that pushed you over the line?"

Section 3: The Transition -- First 90 Days

Q11. Walk me through what happened first

"After the partnership was official, what was the first thing that changed? Not on paper -- the first thing you actually noticed."

Q12. The first 30 days

"What were the first 30 days like? What did SGA's team actually do?"

Q13. What went smoothly

"What was easier than you expected? What worked well from the start?"

Q14. What was harder than expected

"What was harder or different from what you anticipated? Was there friction?"

Q15. The "this is different" moment

"Was there a specific moment where you thought -- this is actually different from what I expected?"

Section 4: Results -- Quantified Outcomes

Q16. Give me a number

"What is the most important metric you track, and what has happened to it since the partnership?"

Q17. What doesn't show on a spreadsheet

"What changed about your daily experience? How you feel when you go to work, or come home?"

Q18. The unexpected benefit

"Was there something you got that you did not expect? Something nobody told you would happen?"

Q19. What you miss about before

"Is there anything about how things were before that you miss or wish you could have back?"

Q20. A specific story

"Give me one specific story from the last six months. Not a general feeling -- an actual event."

Closing Questions -- Designed for Shareable Sound Bites

Placed last because the best quotes come when the interviewee has processed their story fully.

Q21. The coffee with a colleague

"If you were having coffee with someone who is exactly where you were 12-18 months ago -- same situation, same fears -- what would you say to them?" Never skip this question.

Q22. What you wish someone had told you

"Is there anything you wish someone had told you before you made this decision?"

Q23. What you'd say to skeptics

"A lot of people are skeptical. They've heard horror stories. What would you say to that person?"

Q24. The thing nobody talks about

"What is the thing that is hard to convey in a brochure? The thing you would not have believed until you lived it?"

Q25. One word or phrase

"If you had to describe what this partnership has meant in one word or phrase -- honestly -- what would it be?"

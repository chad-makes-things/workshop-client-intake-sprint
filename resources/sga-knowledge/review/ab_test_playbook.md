**Prepared:** March 2026
**Scope:** Full-funnel testing roadmap across landing pages, email, ads, scorecard, pricing page, and webinar
**Audiences:** Practice owners (Dr. Hendricks), associate dentists (Dr. Nair), implant patients (Linda Dawson), specialists (Dr. Whitfield), office managers (Tamara Reeves)
**Methodology:** Pattern-matched against 108+ proven split tests with documented lift percentages; prioritized by ICE scoring (Impact x Confidence x Ease)

## 1. Executive Summary

### Testing Philosophy

This playbook operates on a single principle: **pattern data over opinion.** Every test recommendation is anchored to a proven split test pattern with documented lift percentages from real campaigns. Where no direct pattern match exists, the confidence score is reduced accordingly and the test is deprioritized.

SGA operates four distinct funnels with fundamentally different conversion economics. A practice owner making a once-in-a-career $2.4M decision over 6-18 months requires a different testing strategy than an implant patient booking a consultation in 2-8 weeks. This playbook accounts for those differences.

### Core Meta-Principles Governing All Tests

1. **The offer trumps everything.** Before testing button colors, validate that the offer itself resonates. SGA's offer architecture is strong (JV equity, education platform, brand preservation). Tests should amplify this clarity, not distract from it.
2. **Ugly beats pretty when it is clear.** Red headlines, simple layouts, direct copy -- clarity wins over aesthetics in every documented test.
3. **Show the buy option early.** Hiding the CTA until after a price reveal or long explanation lost in every single documented test.
4. **Payment plans are massive levers.** Adding a 2-pay or 3-pay option has produced +75% to +217% lift in documented tests. For the patient implant funnel, financing presentation is a high-leverage test.
5. **Facebook-style social proof format wins.** Formatting testimonials like social media posts (with name, photo, location, timestamp) outperformed plain text testimonials by +296% in documented tests.
6. **Micro-commitments create momentum.** Survey-first entry points, calculators, and self-assessments consistently outperform direct conversion asks by +95%.
7. **Less is more.** Removing content blocks below the primary conversion element produced +80% lift. Fewer options on a page produced +21% lift.
8. **Measure revenue, not vanity metrics.** Higher email open rates can correlate with lower revenue. Higher CTR can mean lower ROI. Every test in this playbook specifies the primary metric that matters for SGA's business.

## 9. Statistical Significance Guidelines

### Minimum Sample Sizes by Test Type

| Test Type | Minimum Sample per Variant | Minimum Conversions per Variant | Rationale |
|-----------|---------------------------|--------------------------------|-----------|
| Landing page (headline, CTA, layout) | 500 unique visitors | 50 conversions | B2B traffic with moderate conversion rates (5-15%) requires larger samples for reliable results |
| Landing page (minor element: social proof bar, specific numbers) | 300 unique visitors | 30 conversions | Smaller expected effect size; need to detect 10-15% lifts |
| Email (subject line, send time) | 200 sends per variant | 40 opens, 20 clicks | Email metrics are inherently noisy; focus on click rate, not just opens |
| Email (sequence structure) | 200 sequence entrants per variant | 20 conversions (calls booked) | Longer measurement window; downstream metrics matter more than individual email metrics |
| Ads (creative, hook, format) | 3,000 impressions per variant | 30 clicks, 10 conversions | Ad platforms optimize delivery, which can skew results; minimum impression thresholds prevent premature learning-phase exits |
| Ads (audience targeting) | 5,000 impressions per variant | 50 clicks | Audience tests need larger samples because targeting changes introduce multiple variables |
| Scorecard/quiz (question order, gating) | 200 starts per variant | 150 completions | High completion rates expected (>70%); detect 10-15% differences |
| Webinar (title, registration copy) | 300 landing page visitors per variant | 75 registrations | Registration conversion rates of 25-40% expected |
| Webinar (pitch timing, structure) | 100 attendees per variant | 10 conversions | Smaller sample due to audience size constraints; interpret directionally |
| Pricing page (layout, headline) | 300 visitors per variant | 30 CTA clicks | Lower traffic page; longer test duration needed |

### When to Call a Winner

**Decision framework (in order of priority):**

1. **Statistical significance threshold:** 95% confidence level (p < 0.05) for primary metric. Use a Bayesian or frequentist calculator (Google Optimize, VWO, or AB Tasty built-in calculators work).

2. **Minimum test duration:** Regardless of sample size or significance, run every test for a minimum of 7 full days (to account for day-of-week variation in B2B behavior). For email tests, run for a minimum of 2 full sequence cycles.

3. **Practical significance threshold:** Even if statistically significant, do not implement a winner unless the lift exceeds the effort-adjusted minimum:
   - Text-only change (headline, CTA copy, subject line): Implement if lift > 5%
   - Design change (layout, format, section order): Implement if lift > 10%
   - Structural change (sequence length, page restructure, funnel step addition): Implement if lift > 15%

4. **Revenue validation:** For any test that affects a downstream revenue metric (discovery calls booked, consultations scheduled), wait for enough downstream conversions (minimum 10 per variant) to confirm that upstream lift translates to revenue. Higher Assessment start rates that do not produce more discovery calls are a vanity win.

### When to Iterate vs. Declare a Winner

| Scenario | Action |
|----------|--------|
| One variant wins at 95%+ confidence with practical significance | Declare winner. Implement. Move to next test. |
| One variant wins at 90-95% confidence | Continue running for 1 more week. If it holds, implement. If not, consider the test inconclusive and iterate. |
| Neither variant reaches 80% confidence after 2x the estimated duration | Declare inconclusive. The element being tested may not materially affect conversion. Move to a higher-impact test. |
| The variant wins on the primary metric but loses on a secondary metric | Investigate. If the secondary metric loss is significant (e.g., higher Assessment starts but lower discovery call rate), the variant may be attracting lower-quality leads. Run for 2 additional weeks and track downstream conversion. |
| Both variants perform identically | The element being tested does not matter. Implement whichever is easier to maintain and move on. |

### Common Pitfalls to Avoid

1. **Calling tests too early.** A test that shows +50% lift after 100 visitors will often regress to +10% or flat after 500 visitors. Wait for the minimum sample size.

2. **Testing during promotional periods.** If SGA runs a conference sponsorship, webinar, or PR push that changes traffic composition, note it in the test log. Traffic composition changes invalidate test results.

3. **Running too many simultaneous tests on the same page.** Maximum 2 concurrent tests on the main landing page, and they must affect different page sections (e.g., headline + exit intent popup is fine; headline + subheadline is not, because they interact).

4. **Optimizing for opens instead of revenue.** An email subject line that produces +40% open rate but +0% click rate is not a winner. Always track through to the metric that generates revenue.

5. **Ignoring mobile vs. desktop.** SGA's B2B audience may behave very differently on mobile (evening scrolling) vs. desktop (office research). Segment results by device before declaring winners. A variant that wins on desktop but loses on mobile (or vice versa) may need device-specific implementation.

### Low-Traffic Adaptations

For pages or channels with low traffic (pricing page, webinar registration), consider these adaptations:

1. **Increase traffic before testing.** Run additional ad campaigns to the pricing page during the test period to accelerate sample collection.

2. **Use sequential testing instead of simultaneous split testing.** Run the control for 4 weeks, measure, then run the variant for 4 weeks. Less rigorous but practical for low-traffic pages.

3. **Combine similar tests into a single multi-variate test** if the testing tool supports it. For the pricing page, test headline AND tier presentation simultaneously if traffic is too low for separate sequential tests.

4. **Lower the significance threshold to 90%** for low-traffic tests and treat results as directional rather than conclusive. Implement the winner but flag it for re-validation when traffic increases.

Dr. {{first_name}},

This email is designed to be useful whether you ever partner with SGA or not. Print it. Save it. Forward it to a colleague who is getting approached by brokers. The information is the same regardless.

Three things your practice broker probably will not tell you about DSO valuations:

Thing 1

"7–9x EBITDA" does not mean what you think it means.

The multiple is applied to adjusted EBITDA, not to the number on your P&L. Every DSO adjusts differently. Some apply a management fee that reduces the EBITDA before the multiple is applied — so a practice that earns $500K in EBITDA might get valued on $350K after "adjustments."

"Some dentists believe they sold their practice for 4–6x EBITDA, but the DSO manipulated the math based on the note the dentist carries and the management fee." — Dentist Advisors podcast
Ask any DSO for the walk-through calculation. If they cannot provide it in plain language, that tells you something.
Thing 2

The equity you retain may be worth more than the check you deposit.

In a joint-venture equity partnership — the model SGA uses — you receive two things: a liquidity event at closing AND an ongoing equity stake in the combined platform. As the platform grows, the value of your equity stake compounds. "In many cases, the retained minority equity position ends up being worth far more than if the doctor had kept 100% of the practice and continued growing solo." The Thurston Group has demonstrated this pattern across 8 healthcare platforms over 38 years.

Ask any DSO: what is my equity worth in 3 years? In 5? Show me the precedent from a prior platform. If they cannot, that tells you something.
Thing 3

Fewer than 100 of the 1,000+ DSOs are considered qualified bidders.

Group Dentistry Now reports that "fewer than 100 of the 1,000-plus IDSOs are considered qualified bidders by industry experts." That means over 900 organizations are making offers they may not be able to fulfill. Before you evaluate any offer, verify three things: the capital source, the operator track record, and the staff retention rate.

Ask for the staff retention rate. If they will not give it, that tells you something. SGA's is 85%.

Bottom line: A DSO deal is not a number. It is a structure. The multiple is one variable in a multi-variable equation. Get the structure right, and a partnership can be the best financial decision you have ever made. Get it wrong, and you join the 70% who end up regretting it.

The fear of the unknown kills more partnerships than bad economics. Most dentists who hesitate do not hesitate because they dislike the numbers. They hesitate because they cannot picture what happens on Day 1. Day 30. Day 90.

So here is the ambiguity, removed:

Pre-Close — Weeks Before Signing

Three things happen before you sign.

  • Your team is briefed on your terms. You decide when and how your staff hears about the partnership. We provide talking points, but you deliver the message — because it should come from you.
  • Nothing changes on Day 1 of the deal. Your patients' appointments are not disrupted. The transition is invisible to anyone walking in the front door.
  • You get a dedicated transition coordinator. One person. Your point of contact for every question, every "wait, how does this work?" moment. Not a call center. A human being who knows your name.
Days 1–30 — The Relief Phase

This is where the weight starts coming off.

  • iSolved takes over payroll. Your office manager stops spending two days every pay period in QuickBooks.
  • Benefits enrollment rolls out — Cigna for health, Voya for 401(k), Mutual of Omaha supplemental.
  • SGA's back-office handles vendor payments, supply ordering, and insurance credentialing. The stack of invoices on your desk disappears.
  • Nothing clinical changes. You see the same patients. You use the same materials and labs. You set the same schedule.
Days 31–60 — The Growth Phase

The conversation shifts from "what am I losing?" to "what am I gaining?"

  • Mentor assignment through the Individual Growth Engine — a peer, not a corporate trainer.
  • Technology assessment: CEREC, intraoral scanners, guided implant systems. Capital from the platform, not your personal line of credit.
  • Marketing activation: Google Business Profile, website refresh if needed, patient acquisition campaigns. Marketing budget from the platform.
Days 61–90 — The Connection Phase

This is where the loneliness ends.

  • Partner network integration: you join the partner calls, meet the other practice owners, start hearing how colleagues solved the problems you have been wrestling with.
  • 4Front Symposium invitation if timing aligns — the room where goose bumps happen.
  • 90-day partnership review — two-way, not a performance review.

What does not change — at 90 days or ever:

  • Your name stays on the door
  • Your team stays in the chairs
  • You choose the materials, the lab, the schedule
  • You own equity in the combined platform
  • Your patients see you, not a corporate replacement

That is it. No mystery. No corporate rebranding. No production quotas. No regional manager walking in with a clipboard.

If reading this made you think "that sounds manageable" instead of "that sounds terrifying" — that was the point. Partnership should feel like relief, not surrender.

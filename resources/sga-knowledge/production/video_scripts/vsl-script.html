Tone: Not a pitch. A quiet, evidence-heavy conversation between a dentist who has been on both sides and a dentist considering the crossing. Charlie Rose interview energy, not QVC. Pacing is deliberate. Pauses matter.

Visual: Clean, warm, professional. No stock footage of smiling businesspeople. Real offices. Real people. Real equipment. Muted color grade — cinematic but grounded. Documentary, not commercial.

Music: Minimal. Ambient, understated piano or acoustic guitar under problem sections. Silence during key quotes and testimonials. Music should never compete with the words.

Read pace: Written at approximately 140 words per minute (conversational pace). ~2,350 words of spoken dialogue = ~16:45 read-aloud time.

Path 1 You keep going alone.

Path 2 You sell to a corporate DSO.

This is the path seven out of ten dentists regret.

You know the stories. You have heard them at Hinman. You have read them on Dentaltown.

"I didn't get the support I thought. They just took control, but I'm still running it."

"The money — they had a clawback I didn't know about."

"I wish I would have never done this."

On-Screen Text

"I wish I would have never done this." — Dentist with $10M in cash, Dentist Advisors Podcast

That last one? That was said by a dentist who had ten million dollars in the bank.

And he still wished he had not done it.

Because the money is not the problem.

The problem is what happens after.

Your name comes off the door. Your staff gets "reorganized." Your patients start seeing a different face every six months.

And you — the person who built the thing — become an employee in your own practice.

Path 3 You call a practice broker.

Better than the first two. Maybe.

But a broker's job is to close a transaction.

Not to protect your legacy.

Not to invest in your clinical growth.

Not to make sure your team has a career path.

A broker gets paid at closing. What happens to you in Year Two, Year Three, Year Five — that is not their problem.

So.

Three paths. Two dead ends. And one that is transactional by design.

What if there is a fourth?

Teleprompter Notes for Presenter

  • • Script is written for natural speech. Short sentences. Incomplete thoughts where conversational rhythm demands them.
  • • Do NOT read this like a speech. Read it like explaining something important to a friend at dinner.
  • • [BEAT] marks = real silences, 1.5 to 2 seconds. Do not rush past them. Pauses carry as much weight as the words.
  • • Allow the presenter to rephrase in their own words where it feels more natural. Emotional beats matter more than word-for-word fidelity.
  • • If using Dr. Ellingson: naturally passionate and direct. Let him run. Pull back only if he drifts into abstractions instead of specifics.

## Persona 1: Dr. Mark Hendricks -- Practice Owner Considering a DSO Partnership

**Role:** Owner-operator of a two-location general dentistry practice
**Age:** 52 | **Location:** Valdosta, GA | **Practice tenure:** 22 years
**Revenue:** ~$2.4M combined | **Staff:** 18 across both offices
**Education:** DMD, MAGD | **Family:** Married, two kids in college

### Psychographics

Mark built his practice from a single chair into two thriving locations. He is proud, community-rooted, and fiercely protective of his brand. He identifies as a clinician first and a business owner by necessity. He attends the Hinman Dental Meeting every year, serves on his local chamber of commerce, and mentors younger dentists through his state dental association. He is competitive but collegial -- the kind of dentist who will share a technique with a peer at dinner and then try to beat their production numbers the next Monday.

Underneath the confidence, Mark is tired. He did not go to dental school to manage payroll disputes, deal with OSHA compliance audits, or spend Sunday nights working on the schedule because his office manager quit. The business side has slowly eroded his love for clinical work, and he can feel it. He has started googling "sell dental practice" and "DSO pros and cons" late at night.

### Daily Workflow

6:30 AM at the office reviewing the day's schedule. Clinical hours 8-5, seeing 16-20 patients per day. Lunch is often consumed while returning insurance calls or reviewing financial reports. After patients leave, he spends another 1-2 hours on administrative tasks -- signing off on accounts payable, reviewing staff issues with his office manager, and handling supply orders. He gets home around 7 PM, mentally drained, with email still piling up.

### Pain Points (in his own words)

1. **"I'm the dentist, the HR department, the IT guy, and the therapist for my whole staff. I didn't sign up for any of that."** The administrative burden has become all-consuming. Every staff conflict, every benefits question, every broken computer lands on his desk.

2. **"I've got all my eggs in one basket and my financial advisor keeps telling me to diversify, but how do you diversify when your whole net worth is tied up in the practice?"** His retirement plan is essentially "sell the practice someday," but he has no clear succession plan and values are volatile.

3. **"I feel like I'm falling behind. The young dentists coming out of school know digital workflows I've never touched, and I can't afford the time or the money to retool by myself."** He watches peers adopt CEREC, guided implant surgery, and intraoral scanners while he is still taking PVS impressions. The continuing education he attends feels generic and disconnected from his day-to-day.

### Current Solutions and Why They Fall Short

- **Solo ownership:** Full control but also full liability. No economies of scale on supplies, insurance negotiations, or technology.
- **Informal study club:** Meets monthly with 4-5 local dentists. Good for morale, but no real operational or financial impact.
- **Practice management consultant:** Paid $15K for a consulting engagement two years ago. Got a binder of recommendations, implemented maybe 30% of them, then the momentum died.
- **Dental broker inquiries:** Has taken a few calls from brokers pitching DSO deals. The numbers sounded good but the models felt like "selling your soul" -- cookie-cutter offices, production quotas, loss of identity.

### Buying Trigger

The specific event that makes Mark seriously explore a partnership is one of: (a) his long-tenured office manager gives notice, (b) he gets a large unexpected capital expenditure (roof repair, compressor replacement, digital X-ray upgrade), (c) his kids' college tuition hits and he realizes how little liquidity he has, or (d) a colleague he respects joins a DSO and tells him "I wish I'd done this five years ago." The emotional trigger is the moment he realizes he cannot keep doing this alone for another 10-15 years.

### Top 3 Objections

1. **"I'll lose my autonomy. Some corporate suit is going to tell me how to practice."** This is the elephant in the room, and SGA/Gen4 address it directly. As Dr. Mitch Ellingson put it: "The main reason doctors come to Gen4 is because they realize they're still going to have a voice."

2. **"My patients and staff will freak out. They chose me, not some corporation."** He worries about the optics of "selling out." He needs to hear that the practice keeps its name, its team, its culture -- and that existing patients will not even know the difference.

3. **"I don't understand the equity model. Am I getting a fair deal or am I getting taken?"** The financial terms are opaque. He needs transparency around valuation methodology, equity mechanics, and what "partnership" actually means financially. He has heard horror stories from dentists who sold to a DSO and felt lowballed or trapped.

### Where He Spends Time Online

- **Dentaltown forums** (the "DSO" sub-forum is active and contentious)
- **ADA News and state dental association newsletters**
- **LinkedIn** (follows dental industry leaders, occasionally posts)
- **Facebook groups:** "Dental Nachos," "Dentists Who Invest"
- **Podcasts:** The Dental Hacks Podcast, Shared Practices, The Dentist Money Show
- **YouTube:** Watches some clinical content but not a heavy user
- **Google searches** when a specific pain point flares up ("sell dental practice valuation," "DSO vs private practice")

### Awareness Level (Schwartz Scale)

**Solution-Aware.** Mark knows DSOs exist and has a general sense of what they offer. He has not yet determined that SGA/Gen4/MODIS is the right fit -- or even that the "4th generation" collaborative model exists. He still lumps all DSOs into the Heartland/Aspen bucket. He needs to be educated on the distinction between Gen2 standardization and Gen4 collaboration.

### Messaging Angle That Resonates

Lead with **"You built it. We protect it."** -- speak to his identity as a builder and his fear of losing what he created. The strongest proof points are peer testimonials from dentists who sound like him. Dr. Mitchel Godat's quote hits perfectly: "We realized that we needed help to get to the next level. We chose SGA because they support us and manage things behind the scenes. Our partnership allows us to focus on changing lives." Show him that partnership means amplification, not absorption.

## Persona 2: Dr. Priya Nair -- Associate Dentist Seeking a Long-Term Home

**Role:** Associate general dentist, 3 years post-residency
**Age:** 30 | **Location:** Currently in Atlanta, open to relocation across the Southeast
**Education:** DDS, GPR residency | **Debt:** $340K in student loans
**Family:** Engaged, no children yet

### Psychographics

Priya is talented, ambitious, and overwhelmed by the economics of early-career dentistry. She graduated near the top of her class and completed a GPR that gave her broad clinical exposure. She loves comprehensive dentistry -- she wants to do implants, endo, complex restorative -- not just bread-and-butter fillings and prophy checks. But her current associateship at a high-volume corporate practice has her seeing 25-30 patients a day with no time for anything beyond basic procedures. She feels like she is on a production treadmill.

She researched practice ownership and quickly realized she cannot buy a practice with $340K in debt and no savings. She is not opposed to ownership someday, but right now she needs a position that pays well, helps her grow clinically, offers benefits, and gives her a path forward. She is skeptical of corporate dentistry after her current experience but does not want to work for a solo practitioner who might retire in five years and leave her scrambling.

### Daily Workflow

Arrives at 7:45 AM, sees patients from 8 AM to 5:30 PM with a 30-minute lunch. Patient volume is dictated by the schedule template, not by her clinical judgment. She rarely gets to spend more than 15 minutes per patient. After work she studies for her implant fellowship or catches up on CE requirements. Weekends are spent decompressing and researching job opportunities.

### Pain Points (in her own words)

1. **"I feel like a tooth mechanic, not a doctor. I didn't go through four years of dental school plus residency to do nothing but Class IIs all day."** She craves clinical mentorship, complex cases, and the chance to expand her skills -- particularly in implants and full-arch work.

2. **"I'm making decent money but with these loans I can barely save anything. And nobody talks about a career path -- it's just 'produce more.'"** She wants a clear trajectory: How does she go from associate to partner? What does year 3 look like versus year 7? The corporate DSO she works at offers no pathway and no equity.

3. **"I feel totally isolated. There's no one to bounce cases off of or ask for help. The owner comes in two days a week and I'm on my own the rest."** She misses the collegial environment of residency where peers and mentors were always available.

### Current Solutions and Why They Fall Short

- **Corporate DSO associateship:** Pays well ($180K base + production bonus) but no mentorship, no clinical development, high patient volume, no equity, no autonomy. She feels like a cog.
- **Indeed/DentalPost job searches:** She browses listings weekly. Most postings are vague -- "competitive compensation" and "great team" tell her nothing. She cannot distinguish one opportunity from another.
- **CE courses:** She attends Spear and some AGD-accredited courses on her own dime. They are expensive and disconnected from daily practice. No one at her office cares what she learned.

### Buying Trigger

Priya's trigger is a combination of: (a) a particularly demoralizing week where she was pressured to treatment-plan unnecessary procedures, (b) seeing a peer on Instagram post about their new associateship with a DSO that offers mentorship and an equity pathway, or (c) receiving a recruiter outreach from SGA/Gen4 that specifically mentions clinical mentorship, CE support, and a partnership pathway. The Gen4 associate onboarding promise -- "We want to take each individual where they want to go so that it's not just a job; it is a career" -- is exactly what she is looking for.

### Top 3 Objections

1. **"How is this different from the corporate DSO I'm already at? DSO is DSO."** She has been burned once and is wary. She needs concrete evidence of clinical mentorship programs, CE investment, and autonomy -- not just marketing language.

2. **"The locations are mostly small towns in Georgia and the Southeast. I'm not sure I want to live in Hinesville or Hazelhurst."** Geography is a real concern. She needs to understand the quality of life in these communities and whether there are locations that match her lifestyle preferences.

3. **"What does 'pathway to partnership' actually mean? Is that real or is it a recruiting line?"** SGA's career listings mention that new doctors can "invest in the company immediately as a Class A shareholder." She needs specifics: what does that investment look like, what is the timeline, and what have other associates actually experienced?

### Where She Spends Time Online

- **Instagram** (follows dental influencers, clinical case accounts like @thedentistsdentist)
- **TikTok/YouTube Shorts** (watches clinical tip videos, office tour content)
- **Reddit:** r/dentistry, r/DentalSchool
- **DentalPost and Indeed** for job searches
- **Facebook groups:** "New Dentist Now," "Women in Dentistry"
- **Podcasts:** Shared Practices, The New Dentist Podcast
- **LinkedIn** (passively scrolling, not posting)

### Awareness Level (Schwartz Scale)

**Problem-Aware.** Priya knows she is unhappy and knows roughly what she wants (mentorship, growth, equity potential, reasonable patient volume). She has not yet identified SGA or Gen4 as a solution. She may not even know that DSOs offer associate positions with genuine career development paths. She needs to be moved from "all DSOs are the same" to "this one is built differently."

### Messaging Angle That Resonates

Lead with **"Your career is not a production number."** Speak to her desire for clinical growth, mentorship, and a path to something bigger. The Gen4 "individual growth engine" concept -- custom-tailored training, clinical mentorship programs, and leadership development -- is the differentiator. Highlight the story of the Gen4 associate who "relearned how to do implants and now he loves his profession, his patients, everything." Show her that this is a place where she will become the dentist she envisioned in dental school.

## Persona 3: Linda Dawson -- Implant Patient Considering Full-Arch Treatment

**Role:** Retired middle school teacher
**Age:** 64 | **Location:** Springfield, MO (but willing to travel)
**Income:** Fixed retirement income, ~$48K/year | **Insurance:** Medicare + supplemental, no dental coverage
**Family:** Widowed, two adult children, four grandkids

### Psychographics

Linda has been battling dental problems for 20 years. A combination of genetics, dry mouth from medications, and decades of inadequate dental care has left her with failing teeth, a partial denture on top, and a lower denture that slides around constantly. She is embarrassed. She avoids eating in public, covers her mouth when she laughs, and has stopped going to her church choir because she is afraid her denture will slip when she sings. She once told her daughter, "I feel like I'm rotting from the inside out."

She has seen Dr. Olson's YouTube videos and cried watching the patient transformations. She relates to Janice, the 87-year-old who said "It's life changing -- my teeth don't fall out anymore." Linda knows implants exist and wants them desperately, but the cost terrifies her. She has called three dental offices and gotten wildly different quotes ranging from $20K to $70K per arch. She does not understand the variables -- zygomatic vs. standard implants, zirconia vs. acrylic, snap-in vs. fixed. She feels confused, vulnerable, and afraid of making the wrong choice.

### Daily Workflow

Mornings are slow -- coffee and news, sometimes a walk. She babysits her grandkids two days a week and that is when her teeth bother her most -- eating snacks with them, reading stories, smiling for photos. She spends evenings watching TV and scrolling Facebook. She researches dental implants in bursts, usually triggered by a moment of frustration with her dentures (food getting stuck, an embarrassing slippage in public).

### Pain Points (in her own words)

1. **"I can't eat anything I love anymore. Corn on the cob, apples, steak -- forget it. I feel like I'm just surviving, not living."** The functional limitation is daily and demoralizing. As one Innovative Dental patient put it: "How many more years do I have left? Four, five, I don't know. But those years I need to be able to eat and enjoy."

2. **"I'm so embarrassed by my smile that I've stopped doing things I love. I won't take pictures with my grandkids because I hate how I look."** The emotional and social cost is enormous. She has withdrawn from activities that defined her identity.

3. **"Every office I call gives me a different number and I can't tell if they're being honest or just trying to sell me. I feel like a walking wallet, not a patient."** Price opacity in the implant space is a massive barrier. Dr. Olson addresses this directly: "I want to be transparent because I think it's difficult for people to find that answer."

### Current Solutions and Why They Fall Short

- **Lower denture:** Slides, causes sore spots, limits diet to soft foods. She uses adhesive daily and it barely helps.
- **Upper partial denture:** Fits slightly better but is still uncomfortable and looks artificial. Food gets trapped underneath.
- **Her current general dentist:** Good for cleanings and fillings but does not do implants. Referred her to an oral surgeon who quoted $55K for full mouth and had a 6-month wait.
- **Google research:** She has spent hours reading reviews, watching videos, and comparing options. The more she reads, the more confused she gets. Too many variables, too much contradictory information.

### Buying Trigger

Linda's trigger is a convergence of: (a) a particularly humiliating denture moment (a slippage at a family gathering, an inability to eat birthday cake with her grandkids), (b) discovering Dr. Olson's YouTube content and seeing a patient her age get transformed in a single day, and (c) learning that financing at ~$250/month is possible with a 675+ credit score. The emotional moment is when she watches a reveal video and thinks, "That could be me."

### Top 3 Objections

1. **"I can't afford this. $30K-$50K is more than I make in a year."** Cost is the primary barrier. She needs to understand financing options, what insurance might cover, and why the investment is worth it long-term compared to continued denture costs and declining health.

2. **"I'm terrified of the surgery. What if something goes wrong? I'm 64 and I've never had any kind of surgery."** Fear of pain, complications, and anesthesia. She needs reassurance through patient testimonials, procedure explanations, and Dr. Olson's "same-day" framing that makes it feel manageable.

3. **"Springfield is 4 hours from me. How do I manage travel and follow-up appointments?"** Many implant patients travel to centers of excellence. She needs logistics support -- understanding the timeline, how many trips are required, and whether virtual follow-ups are possible.

### Where She Spends Time Online

- **Facebook** (primary platform -- shares posts, comments on friends' updates, belongs to local community groups and a denture support group)
- **YouTube** (watches Innovative Dental videos, searches "dental implant cost" and "dental implant before and after")
- **Google** (searches triggered by pain points: "best dental implants near me," "dental implant financing," "full mouth dental implants cost 2026")
- **Church and community groups** (offline word-of-mouth is powerful for this persona)
- Does NOT use Instagram, TikTok, LinkedIn, or Reddit

### Awareness Level (Schwartz Scale)

**Product-Aware.** Linda knows dental implants are the solution. She has even identified Innovative Dental / Dr. Olson as a potential provider. She is stuck in the evaluation stage -- comparing options, trying to understand pricing, and working up the courage to commit. She needs messaging that reduces fear, builds trust, and makes the path to action clear and simple.

### Messaging Angle That Resonates

Lead with **"You deserve to smile at every one of life's moments."** This echoes Dr. Olson's stated mission: "If you have to think about your teeth, those moments aren't there. I want to dedicate my life to helping my patients and a team rally around the cause of making sure everybody lives full every one of life's moments." Show her transformation stories of patients her age. Give her transparent pricing. Make the first step feel low-risk (free webinar, virtual consultation, refundable deposit).

## Persona 4: Dr. James Whitfield -- Periodontist Considering a Specialty DSO Partnership

**Role:** Solo-practice periodontist with a focus on implantology
**Age:** 46 | **Location:** Jacksonville, FL
**Practice tenure:** 14 years | **Revenue:** ~$1.8M | **Staff:** 10
**Education:** DDS, Certificate in Periodontics | **Family:** Married, three kids (ages 8, 11, 14)

### Psychographics

James is a skilled clinician who built a strong referral network over 14 years. He does bread-and-butter perio (scaling and root planing, crown lengthening, soft tissue grafts) but his passion and growth area is implantology -- particularly full-arch cases. He attended a Pikos Institute course three years ago and it changed his clinical trajectory. He started offering All-on-X procedures and his case acceptance rate has been climbing, but he is bumping against the ceiling of what he can do as a solo specialist.

He needs a dedicated CT scanner (his is aging), a larger surgical suite, an in-house lab, and a marketing engine to attract implant patients directly rather than relying solely on GP referrals. He has priced out the capital investment: $500K+ to build what he envisions. His accountant told him he would need to take on significant debt, and at 46 with three kids heading toward college, the risk feels heavy.

He heard about MODIS through the Pikos Institute network. The idea of a specialty-focused DSO that provides capital, marketing, and operational support while letting him keep his clinical autonomy is appealing -- but he is cautious. He has seen colleagues join DSOs and quietly regret it within 18 months.

### Daily Workflow

Clinical days (Mon-Thu) start at 7:30 AM with surgical cases, then a mix of consults and follow-ups until 5 PM. Fridays are reserved for administrative work, CE, and strategic planning. He spends significant time cultivating referral relationships -- lunches with GPs, study club presentations, occasional guest lectures at the local dental school. Evenings involve case planning, reviewing CBCTs, and managing the business side.

### Pain Points (in his own words)

1. **"I know the full-arch market is exploding but I can't compete with the Innovative Dentals of the world from my little perio office. They have the marketing machine, the in-house lab, the YouTube channel. I've got a referral pad and a fax machine."** He sees the direct-to-consumer implant market growing and feels left behind without the infrastructure to compete.

2. **"Recruiting is killing me. I've been looking for an associate periodontist for 18 months. Nobody wants to move to Jacksonville to join a solo perio practice when DSOs are offering sign-on bonuses and equity."** The specialist labor market is brutally competitive, and a solo practice cannot offer the benefits, training, or career trajectory that larger organizations can.

3. **"I'm 46 and I have no succession plan. If I get hurt tomorrow, my family gets nothing except the lease on the building and a bunch of aging equipment."** The single-point-of-failure risk keeps him up at night. He wants financial diversification and a safety net.

### Current Solutions and Why They Fall Short

- **Solo practice:** Full autonomy but also full risk. No marketing team, no recruiting infrastructure, no leverage on supply costs.
- **Pikos Institute courses:** Excellent for clinical education, but attending courses does not solve his business infrastructure problems.
- **Local dental society networking:** Maintains referrals but does not help him attract direct-to-patient implant cases.
- **Practice broker conversations:** Has talked to two brokers about selling. The valuations came back lower than expected because specialist practices are harder to transition. He does not want a fire sale.

### Buying Trigger

James's trigger is hearing a colleague -- specifically a periodontist or oral surgeon in the MODIS network -- describe their partnership experience in concrete terms: what changed, what stayed the same, and what the financial outcome looked like. The Troy Family Dental or Pensacola Perio stories on the MODIS Community Conversations podcast could be exactly this catalyst. Alternatively, a direct outreach from MODIS VP of Business Development Marcus Morosin with a clear articulation of the equity model and operational support.

### Top 3 Objections

1. **"Private equity scares me. I've heard the stories -- they load up on debt, squeeze margins, and flip the company in 3-5 years. Then I'm stuck with a new owner who doesn't care about me."** He needs to understand the Thurston Group's track record (37 years, 8+ dental platforms, $4B+ returned to partners) and how the equity model aligns long-term incentives.

2. **"MODIS is implant-focused, but 60% of my revenue is still traditional perio. Are they going to push me to drop the bread-and-butter work and only do implants?"** He needs reassurance that clinical autonomy means he can practice the full scope of periodontics, not just the procedures that fit MODIS's growth strategy.

3. **"I don't want to be the junior partner. I've been running my own show for 14 years. I'm not going to take orders from a 30-year-old MBA."** This maps directly to Dr. Mitch Ellingson's observation: "Going through that transition from being king of your own island to being a leader in a group is really hard." James needs to see that MODIS leadership respects clinical expertise and that the relationship is genuinely collaborative.

### Where He Spends Time Online

- **PubMed and clinical journals** (Journal of Periodontology, JOMI, Clinical Implant Dentistry and Related Research)
- **Instagram** (follows implant dentists, posts occasional case photos)
- **Dental implant-specific forums and Facebook groups:** "Implant Dentistry Study Group," "Full Arch Implant Dentistry"
- **LinkedIn** (more active than most dentists -- uses it for professional networking and recruiting)
- **Podcasts:** MODIS Community Conversations, Implant Practice Management, The Dental Implant Podcast
- **Conferences:** Pikos Symposium, AAP Annual Meeting, AO Annual Meeting

### Awareness Level (Schwartz Scale)

**Solution-Aware to Product-Aware.** James knows DSOs exist and has specifically heard of MODIS through the Pikos network. He is evaluating whether MODIS is the right DSO for him -- comparing it against Pacific Dental Services, Aspen, and the option of staying solo. He needs MODIS-specific differentiation: the Thurston Group track record, the Pikos Institute connection, the implant-focused vertical, and the collaborative community of specialists.

### Messaging Angle That Resonates

Lead with **"Your clinical mastery deserves an infrastructure to match."** Speak to his ambition, not just his pain. The Pikos Institute connection is the trust bridge -- Dr. Michael Pikos is a household name in implantology, and his endorsement of MODIS carries enormous weight. The quote from MODIS: "Free yourself to flourish. Position yourself to profit. You worked hard to build your practice. Are you ready to reap the rewards?" hits the right note. Show him what a specialist practice looks like with capital investment, marketing, recruiting, and a peer community of elite implantologists behind it.

## Persona 5: Tamara Reeves -- Practice Manager at a Newly Acquired SGA Practice

**Role:** Office manager at a two-dentist general practice that recently joined SGA
**Age:** 38 | **Location:** Hinesville, GA
**Tenure at practice:** 6 years (started as front desk, promoted to manager 3 years ago)
**Education:** Associate's degree in Business Administration | **Family:** Married, two kids (ages 6, 10)

### Psychographics

Tamara is the glue of her office. She handles scheduling, insurance verification, staff conflicts, supply ordering, patient complaints, and half the bookkeeping. She is intensely loyal to "her" dentist and "her" patients. When the practice was acquired by SGA, she was terrified. She assumed "corporate" meant layoffs, micromanagement, and the death of the family feel that made her practice special. She has seen friends in healthcare get "corporatized" -- standardized scripting, time tracking, top-down mandates -- and she is on guard.

What Tamara does not yet realize is that SGA could actually make her life better. The administrative tasks she juggles alone could be absorbed by a back-office team. The payroll she manages by hand could be automated. The benefits she cobbles together from a PEO could be replaced with Cigna, Mutual of Omaha, and a real 401(k) through Voya. But she needs to see it to believe it. She is from Missouri (figuratively and literally) -- show her.

### Daily Workflow

Arrives 30 minutes before the office opens to review the schedule, follow up on insurance claims, and handle any fires from the previous day. During clinic hours she toggles between front desk coverage, patient check-out, supply ordering, and managing the hygienists' schedules. After clinic hours she processes payroll (every two weeks), reconciles the books, and handles HR paperwork. She is the first person everyone calls when something goes wrong and the last to leave at night.

### Pain Points (in her own words)

1. **"I wear 15 hats and I'm drowning. I can't do HR, bookkeeping, scheduling, and patient care all at the level they deserve. Something always slips through the cracks."** She is spread too thin and knows quality is suffering, but there is no one else to hand things off to.

2. **"I'm scared this acquisition means I'm going to lose my job or become irrelevant. If corporate handles payroll and HR, what do I even do?"** The fear of obsolescence is real. She needs to understand that SGA values her institutional knowledge and local relationships -- and that offloading admin tasks frees her to focus on the patient experience and team development she actually loves.

3. **"The doctors are stressed about the transition and the staff are coming to me with questions I can't answer. I feel like I'm supposed to hold everyone together but nobody's holding me together."** She is absorbing the emotional labor of the entire transition. She needs communication, clarity, and a point of contact at SGA who treats her as a partner in the process.

### Current Solutions and Why They Fall Short

- **Manual processes:** Payroll by hand in QuickBooks, scheduling on paper or in basic PM software, HR compliance managed via Google searches and hope. It works until it does not.
- **Her own tenacity:** She makes it work through sheer willpower and 50+ hour weeks. But she is burning out and her family is feeling the impact.
- **The practice owner's guidance:** Her dentist is supportive but also overwhelmed. He cannot mentor her in the business skills she needs because he is learning them himself.

### Buying Trigger

Tamara is not the "buyer" in the traditional sense -- she does not choose the DSO. But she is a critical influence on whether the transition succeeds or fails. If she resists, the staff resists, patients feel the tension, and the practice underperforms post-acquisition. Her "buying" moment is when she experiences the first tangible benefit: the iSolved payroll system eliminates her biweekly payroll headache, the PeopleOps team answers a benefits question she has been struggling with, or she attends a Gen4/SGA training session and realizes there is a real career development path for non-clinical staff. As Gen4's VP of People & Culture Spencer Pierce described it: "After onboarding is completed, there's an abundance of relief. It's like 'Okay, I understand what this means for me now.'"

### Top 3 Objections

1. **"You don't know our office. You don't know our patients. Some MBA from Richmond Hill is going to come in and tell me how to run my front desk?"** She bristles at any hint of condescension or one-size-fits-all mandates. She needs to be approached as a respected partner, not a subordinate.

2. **"What happens to my people? Are you going to fire anyone? Change the benefits? Cut hours?"** Staff welfare is her number one concern. She needs clear, honest answers about what changes and what stays the same -- ideally before the acquisition closes, not after.

3. **"Is this just a pit stop? Are you going to flip us in three years and then we're dealing with a new owner all over again?"** She has seen private equity transactions in healthcare and is skeptical about long-term commitment. She needs to understand the Thurston Group's holding period and the equity alignment model.

### Where She Spends Time Online

- **Facebook** (primary -- active in local community groups, a dental office managers group, and a "dental office managers" private group)
- **DentalPost** (for recruiting when she has an open position)
- **YouTube** (occasional tutorials on practice management software)
- **Pinterest** (for office decor and patient experience ideas)
- **Google** (searches: "dental office manager salary," "how to handle staff conflict," "DSO acquisition what to expect")
- Does NOT use LinkedIn, Twitter/X, Reddit, or podcasts regularly

### Awareness Level (Schwartz Scale)

**Unaware to Problem-Aware.** Tamara did not choose to engage with a DSO -- the acquisition happened around her. She is not shopping for solutions; she is processing a change she did not initiate. SGA needs to move her from fear and skepticism to understanding and buy-in. The key is not marketing to her -- it is the onboarding experience itself. Every touchpoint during the first 30 days either builds trust or erodes it.

### Messaging Angle That Resonates

Lead with **"You're not losing your role. You're getting a team."** Acknowledge her importance explicitly. The worst thing SGA can do is make her feel like a cost center to be optimized. The best thing is to make her feel like a valued partner who is finally getting the support she deserved all along. Gen4's onboarding approach -- "Our core focus is on the entire team... We want to take each individual where they want to go so that it's not just a job; it is a career" -- is the right tone. Show her the Practice Management Pathways Program and help her see a future where she is not just surviving but growing.
